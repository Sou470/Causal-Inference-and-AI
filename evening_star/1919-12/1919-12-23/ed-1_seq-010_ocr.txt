FUNDAMENTAL, SIMONDS SAYS
Issue Between President and Senate Op
ponents Goes Deeper Than Personal
ities or Partisanship.
II\ FRANK H. SIMON DS
A week at the capital discloses three
salient details in the situation, so far
as the treaty of Versailles is con
cerned.
First: A general conviction that
some treaty will be ratified within
the next thre$ months.
Second: An unmistakable strength
ening* of the tone and feeling* of the
republican senators, who stood solidly
for reservations, growing out of the
absence of any general or impressive
public protest over the defeat of the
treaty a few weeks ago.
Third: The improvement of the
health of the President himself, who
is described as becoming more de
termined in his insistence upon de
feating reservations, notably those in
cluded in Article X, which he regards
as fatal to the moral force of the
document negotiated by him in Paris.
On the surface of things the posi
tion of the Senate and of the Presi
dent would seem to point to a long
continued deadlock, but the reason
that many observers expect a rela
tively rapid accommodation of rival
views is found in the fact that many
democratic observers, and republican
testimony on this point is worthless,
forecast a larger democratic defec
tion from the President's position in
the weeks following the Christmas
holidays.
Situation In Clear.
At bottom the Washington situation j
is very clear. On the one hand the
President stands firmly to his con- I
ception of what the league of na
tions is to be. He remains in the j
mood of his Paris utterances. He j
sees, none better and none more
clearly that such amendments, now
become reservations, as are today the
irreducible minimum of republican
and for that matter, minority demo
cratic, demands?strike at the very
heart of the ideal he had for the
league of nations.
Despite illness and frankly con
ceded disappointment at the failure
of the country to give unmistakable
evidence of its support of his ideal
istic program, the President is by no
means satisfied that this public fail
ure is permanent, and as his strength
returns his disposition "to fight it out
on this line, if it takes;'all summer."
is recognized and testified to by those
who -should be nearest to him.
The President's mood and concep
tion are very little appreciated
either in the country or even in
Washington, because in both places
the inside history of the Paris con
ference is little known. The reserva
tions proposed have excited no wave
of protest and indignation, because
the country has only vaguely and
dimly been informed of the actual
course of American policy in Paris.
But the President sees accurately
that, unimportant as the main reser
vations. exclusive of the preamble
and certain other offensive details,
outwardly appear, they actually go to
the very heart of the idea which he
champioped in Paris. To argue that
mere obstinacy and self-willed deter- r
mination dominate his course is, in
my judgment, to reason incorrectly.
The real truth is that the President
finds on the democratic as well as on
the republican side imperfect under
standing of the larger aspect of his
Paris 0 program, the moral values
which he saw in America's future
oourse. He knows always what he
represented to be American desires
at Paris, what he described Ameri
can idealism to be, and he knows, as
does every one who was in Paris,
that the treaty which could be rati
fied now. with the reservations today
inescapable, constitutes an actual re
pudiation of that American policy he
voiced one year ago.
President's Underlying Idea.
Underlying all else in the Presi
dent's Paris course was the idea of
American responsibility in world af
fairs. the league of nations was only
a technical formulation of a new pol
icy. America, as 1#? and his asso
ciates over and over again explained,
had broken with past isolation and
had come to Europe prepared to as
sume burdens and responsibilities,
moral and material alike, provided the
actual peace terms conformed to
those fourteen points, which repre
sented in his mind American will, be
cause, when he first uttered them,
nearly two years ago, they met with
universal acclaim within the United
States. I
Now the spirit of all the Senate
reservations is absolutely contrary to
the President's own view of the real
and right spirit of America. The
reservations themselves, the most rea
sonable, hardly less than the most ex
treme, openly limit, circumscribe,
practically abolish American respon
sibility or concern with European and
world affairs. They reduce the treaty,
so far as America is concerned, to
little more than a formal assent to the
material terms imposed upon Ger
many and with these the President
was never much concerned, precisely
as, even during the war. his attention
was fixed upon the ordering of the
world, to the obtaining of a new
constitution of world relations after
the struggle had terminated in Ger
man defeat.
Difference Is Fundamental.
It is therefore a mistake, in my
judgment, to dismiss the present con
flict as merely partisan or wholly
personal. If political and human con
siderations enter into the affair, as
they manifestly do, there is some
thing far more important, there is a
real clash between two totally dif
ferent conceptions of American duties
in the world, both to herself and to
the rest of mankind. This is why com
promise is so exceedingly difficult.
Any compromise, such as must be
made by the President, any conces
sion which would lead to a useful and
must constitute for him a surrender
of that ideal for which he fought in I
Paris, to realize which, he went to
Paris.
On the other hand, it is not mere ob
stinacy which animates the President,
it is not less clear that it is not mere
partisanship which dominates the
course of many republicans, of some
democrats in the Senate. These men
differ absolutely from the President
in their conception of the duties of
America, of the role of America in
the world. They are honestly opposed
to entering into such contracts as they
see in the treaty of Versailles as it
I stands, they are unequivocally op-1

J country of the mission the President
j accepted for the nation at Paris.
Why Solution Is Coming.
Why in this situation is there a
Washington belief that the end will
come relatively soon? What is the
possible termination of an absolute
deadlock? The answer is plain. if
the large majority of the democratic j
senators still support the President in j
his stand. Washington feels that they
are becoming influenced by the un- 1
mistakable demand on the part of the
nation for a restoration of peace; the j
capital recognizes that these democratic
senators, many of them, as little under-
stand or support the President's concep-
tion of the ideal mission of the United j
States as their republican opponents.
Some of the highest placed democrats in j
Washington in recent days expressed
the view that, given this situation,
the arrival of an agreement between
republican and democratic senators,
without the sanction of the President
for the compromise agreed to, would j
lead soon after the New Year to the j
ratification by the Senate of the trea-
ty of Versailles with nil of the es-
sential reservations included. In the
same sense Washington dismisses as
ridiculous the proposal to make peace
by concurrent resolution.
Nor do democratic or republican
leaders hail the idea of making the j
ratification wait upon the campaign
and fighting out the issue in the elec
tion. Privately both sides concede
that so far as they can see the public
is not interested in the opposing
principles, has no other feeling than
one of intense weariness, aggravated I

SANTA CLAUS AND GIRL
SCOUTS SCATTER GIFTS

Touring the Slums of South Wash
ington in High-Powered
Automobile.
Surrounded by a bevy of Girl Scout6,
Santa Claus hopped into his high
powered car, overflowing- with Christ
was packages, and started this morn
ing for a tour of the slums of Wash
ington.
In response to scores of letters ad
dressed to "The Girl Scout Santa
Claus Lady," Santa took packages to
poor little youngsters who for weeks
have been fearful that they would
have a dreary Christmas. Each case
had been investigated by the Girl
Scouts' Association, assisted by the
District playground department, and
each had been pronounced worthy.
The role of Santa Claus is being
taken by Charles Graff of Eckington.
As the car pulls up to each home.
Santa will hop out, will be handed
packages by the Girl Scouts in the
car, will deliver them to the poor
family, and will have a little chat
with the children, allowing them to
shake hands with him and possibly
pull his whiskers.
For months the Girl Scouts have
been busy gathering old toys and
clothing and putting them in first
class shape. Legs and arms have been
glued on broken dolls; dirty doll
faces have been washed; smashed toys
have been mended; torn clothes have
l>een patched; everything has been
made as good as new. Every child
has been promised that the toy he re
ceives will actually work.
The packages were put up yesterday
afternoon at Girl Scout headquarters,
in the Woodward building, by members
of Troop 17, which meets at the
Epiphany Church. The girls reread
the Santa Claus letters and then
wrapped up packages which would
meet the individual wishes of the
various children. Apples, nuts and
candy were put in along with toys and
clothing.
