To the Editor of The Star:
What is the future of street rail
roads or, more particularly, city
transportation? It's self-evident in
Washington that the surface of the
business streets- is not sutHcient to

ple and Congress can condemn to
their hearts' content, but that will

It seems to the writer that under
ground rapid transit is the one sure
relief. In a smaller degree than New j
York city was before its rapid transit
system was established, is Washing
ton's present condition. Indeed, no
city in the country converges traffic
so much as this city, with its exten

sever.il thousand employes. Is it not
possible for an artery system of un

that would absorb 50 per cent of the

manently removed the congestion will
continue with increased violence
against the rights of the people. No
one should intentionally or justly
deny capital its right to earn profits,
but sometimes it is compulsory and
expedient to demand common human
rights to supersede all material rights,
and this seems to be one of the things
