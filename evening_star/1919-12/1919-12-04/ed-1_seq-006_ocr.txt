A test of the, "platoon system" in
the public schools, as a means of re
lieving congestion due to inadequate
equipment, has been ordered by the
board of education at the recommenda- i
Ition of Supt. Thurston. This experi
ment, based upon actual operation of
the system in other cities recently
i visited by the superintendent, prom
i ises to aid materially in improving
j conditions in the schools. It is not
merely a method of making fuller j
use of the buildings, however. Ex
perience shows that in the cities where
it has been adopted it has improved
the quality of the educational work
and made for the advancement of the
pupils. Supt. Thurston looks for such
results from the initial test that the
system will be generally adopted here.
This platoon system depends for suc
cess upon large buildings. It is not
adapted to the smaller structures
which prevail here to a considerable
extent. It has been found that the
best work is done in the twenty-four
room buildings, and the hope is that
in the future program of local school
construction this type will bp adopted
wherever possible. The day of the
small schoolhouse has passed. It has
proved to be inadequate for large-city
purposes, and Washington is now in
the large-city class. It, vrould have
been well for the District had the
building program been changed twen
ty years ago, to the end of providing
for new buildings of no less than six
teen rooms, ranging upward to the
twenty-four-room style, which is be
lieved to be the maximum of school
house efficiency.
Whatever the platoon system may
afford in the way of relief from con
gestion there should bo no slackening
of the effort, to bring the District's
school equipment up to date. The
present arrears of houses is gravely
'handicapping. To catch up with im
j mediate needs calls for a large ex
