LONDON, December 3.?Authorita

attitude of the leading British states

reservations :(fecting the league of
nations covenant cannot l>e obtained
at iiresent. However, there is reason
to believe that these officials will ap
proach such reservations as the Sen
ate may vote in a broad, trustful
spirit. That a good many of the most
forceful men in the cabinet will do so
is beyond doubt. Some first-rate stu
dents of the situation predict that
tne allies will be only too glad to wel
come America into the league on her
own terms.
It is an amazing fact that some of
the most famous statesmen in Kurope
appear to huve negotiated with Presi
dent Wilson in Paris as if the Consti
tution of the United States did not ex
ist. There is no evidence that it en
tered their heads at the conference in
the French capital nor that they
thought of it until confronted with
the actual temporary defeat of the
covenant in Washington. These men.
though they li."|ve democracy on their
ILps almost daily, seem to cling to th?
ideas of the old diplomacy. Confident
that they could commit the British
parliament and people as they liked,
they assumed that President Wilson
could do likewise with the American
people. They are getting seemingly
their firs* lessons in American con
stitutional procedure, and in the devo
tion of Americans to the theory afrid
practice of democratic sovereignty.
Some* of them, though only a few.
take these lessons with ill-concealed
impatience. But they will take them,
nevertheless, because there is noth
ing else for it. Knlightened men and
women in England, both in the gov
i eminent and out of it. are coming to
see that the movement for reserva
tions does not owe its vitality wholly
to resentment against President Wil
son's manners and methods, nor even
mainly to these, but that it springs
chiefly from the determination of the
.American people to keep control of
their affairs, both national and inter
national.
It is most generally felt, and I be
lieve among the working masses uni
versally felt, that America will do
j more for the world by jealously
guarding the forms which preserve
the essence of her democracy tlian by
taking any step in the direction of
empowering envoys or officials of any
sort to go off into conference cham
bers and commit the American people
in matters touching their lives, for
tunes and honor. America will enter
the league, but she will enter it and
behave in it as her people dictate.
This is the view of the best Knglish
opinion, which approves this course.
