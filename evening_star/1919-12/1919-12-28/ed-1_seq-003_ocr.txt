Memory of Col. Roosevelt Today
a Flaming Sword to Followers
By N. O. MESSENGER.
IX talking with republican politicians be
tween here and Boston and New York and
Chicago?republicans of all shades of fac
tional opinion?one suggestion was contin
ually presented to the writer?that the
party managers, the organization and regional
bosses will commit a fatal error if they attempt
"steam-roller" methods in behalf of any candi
date or against other candidates at the national
convention. While apprehension of possible re
currence of former alleged "steam-roller" epi
sodes was entertained more largely by pro
gressives, warning against repetitibn of past
policies was uttered as well by conservatives
and old-line republicans.
An open field and a fair fight is wanted,
with hands off by the national committee and
the organization. The work of the committee
on credentials will be watched as never before,
it was said, and absolute impartiality will be de
manded, insisted upon and required.

The flock of "favorite sons" whose candi
dacies for the nomination have been and will be
entered is warrant for such requirement, it is
claimed. Once the permanent roll of the con
vention is made up and constituted upon a basis
of action which causes no resentment, and leaves
no bitterness or taint of suspicion of unfairness,
the proceedings thereafter will be in the hands
of the delegates. Utmost confidence is expressed
in the pWspective fair rulings of Senator Henry
Cabot Lodge, if. as is now planned, he should
be the permanent chairman. Politicians say
that with the roll honestly made up they will
take the fortunes of war in the subsequent de
velopments.

Another fact stood out aggressively at every
stage of The Star correspondent's journey and
talks with republicans, anil that was the indu
bitable evidence that the spirit of progressiv
ism which was embodied in Theodore Roose
velt still glows at white heat. The memory of
Roosevelt the man is revered and held In grow
ing afTection. but the things which he stood for
are advocated more ardently than ever.
There is a curious psychological phase to
this situation. It is as if every progressive
has constituted himself the torch-bearer, to pick
up the torch which fell from Roosevelt's hand
and to carry it forward as Roosevelt would do.
There appears to rest upon these progressives
a personal responsibility to carry on. Kach man
is jealous of his right and duty, never thinking
of shouldering them upon another, hut eager to
push forward and keep the flame alive. "Let me
do it," is the individual clamor.

The publication of many Roosevelt biogra
phies and the holding of memorial services, giv
lng a closer view of Roosevelt's definite policy
throughout his public life, now that his life can
be viewed in the whole, has seemed to awaken
increased appreciation of the fact that con
stantly in his public career, from police commis
sioner of New York onward, he followed a defi
nite and undeviatlng line of action in the direc
tion of humanitarianism. progressivism and
public and political honesty. His followers are
thinking high thoughts today, appreciating what
he contended for and what he accomplished,
anxious to emulate him.
Everywhere, politicians told the writer that
the leader who can convince the progressive
element of the republican party that he comes
nearer beitlg the Roosevelt type will command
at the outset a political asset of tremendous
value. The asset is latent; it exists now and
needs only to he assembled and applied by a
leader, a person.

Unqestionably the managers of Gen. Wood's
campaign for the nomination realized the ex
istence of this sentiment, this psychologic il
state or whatever one may call it. long ago and
were quick to take .udvantage of it. They pro
ceeded to capitalize it promptly. How far they
will get with it remains to be developed, as
other aspirants for the nomination refute the
claim of a monopoly for Gen. Wood. But there
is no doubt that it is the foundation of the
Wood sentiment now in evidence, furnishing a
nucleus around which to gather increment.
The Wood campaign has been conducted
upon strictly business and along "efficiency"
lines. The first thing the Wood men did was
to establish a bureau to build up an organiza
tion. They chose Henry L. West, formerly of
this city and afterward in charge of building
up the great Security and Preparedness
league, to conduct the propaganda, or rather
it should be said the construction of the edifice
?which is an art.
The Wood league is a separate entity from
the purely political branch of the Wood cam
paign. The league's purpose Is to establish
Wood clubs throughout the country: the politi
cal managers. National Committeeman John T.
King of Connecticut and Col. William C. Proc
ter of Cincinnati, taking care of what might be
called the political strategy.
In twenty-eight states there exist solid
Wood league organizations which have now
reached the point of branching out from mere
state organizations and building up county and
city branches. The Wood clubs are growing
daily. The headquarters of the league, at the
Hotel Imperial in New York city, is a busy
place, and Mr. West's mail runs about a thou
sand letters a day. The statement has been
made by friends of other candidates that the
Wood boom has expanded too suddenly for
healthy growth, and has indeed now reached Its
limit. One of the Wood managers replied to
that by sayins; that they are proceeding upon
the theory of a rolling snow ball gathering
volume, and are not disturbed at its present
size.

Now, there is another sentiment existent
among republicans outside of the professional
political class and which is very much hi evi
dence. It is rated as anti-Wood in effect, though
not necessarily in intent. That is, the widespread
demand in the rank and file for the nomination
of a business man for President. Gen. Wood
has been a pood executive and administrator,
they admit, but say that he does not answer to
the plans and specifications of a "business
man."
They cannot quite explain what they mean
by a business man, in explicit terms. They say
generally thfy mean a man who has had ex
perience with affairs; practical experience as
well as contact with big men in the industrial
:ind financial world, and with big institutions
therein.
These republicans say they are satiated with
theories, utterly intolerant of experiments, and
Vmve no patience either with theorists or pro
fessional politicians. By professional politicians
they say they mean men who depend upon clap
trap. specious arguments or glittering generali
ties and glib speech to further their ends.
They declare they want a "safe" man, leav
ing to one's own imagination the definition of
the term, themselves understanding it to mean
a man who will hug the shore of economic con
servatism closely in piloting the ship of state.
There are several in the field answering to the
description, it is said, and the question will be
to pick the one who looks the best "vote get
ter," so that they can have reasonable assur
ance of electing him after he is nominated.

The plan of Chairman Will Hays of the re
publican national committee to crfeate a com
mitee to assemble ideas for a national platform
in advance of the convention was commented
upon favorably wherever The Star's correspond
ent went. The personnel of the committee will
be announced early in January.
Mr. Hays has been going about the selection
of the committee in a way calculated to please
all factions. He has invited suggestions for
appointment to the committee from all possible
sources, with a view to making it as represent
ative of every phase of economic and political
thought as it is humanly possible to do.
When that committee gets to work it Is ex
pected that every constructive idea latent 4n the
mind of man will be presented in form to be
considered by the committee on resolutions of
the national convention.

state. Born in the little town of
Plymouth and the husband of a Bur
lington girl, his political career has
naturally been watched with inter
est.
This sentiment in his favor has al
ready taken shape in the formation
of a Coolidge Club dn his native coun
ty, the governor's father being the
honorary president of the club. It is
confidently expected that the organ
ized movement will spread to other
counties, and eventually result in the
formation of a state club. Gov.
Clement and several former govern
ors have already pledged their hearty
support.
Wood Is Well Liked.
He would be a rash man who would
venture to predict at this time who
will be Vermont's second choice, but
it may be said that Gen. Wood stands
well up toward the top. If any work
was done for any candidate prior to
Gov. Coolidge's appearance as a pos
sibility. it was done very quietly and
without any tangible results. Ver
mont republicans are not in the habit
of formally instructing their dele
gates to national conventions, al
though the state convention which
elected delegates to the national con
vention of 1916 strongly urged the
eight men to support the candidacy of
Mr. Hughes.
As yet there has been little dis
cussion of the personnel of the dele
gation. The name of Prank L. Greene,
representative from the first congres
sional district, was mentioned, where
upon Mr. Greene wrote that he
thought the honor should go to some
one else. His magnanimous position
has been seized upon by some state
papers as an additional reason why
he should be chosen.
Pears Loss of Seat.
A political matter which is Just
now of particular Interest to Ver
monters is the possibility of the state
losing a representative in Congress
following the apportionment based
on the census to be taken next month.
There are two representatives at pres
ent?Frank L. Greene of St. Albans,
whose service began with the Sixty
second Congress, and Porter H. Dale of
Island Pond, who was first elected to
the Sixty-fourth Congress. If the two
districts remain undisturbed neither
Mr. Greene nor Mr. Dale is likely to
have any formidable opposition next
year. If the state is made into a
single district almost anything may
happen, particularly if the succession
