Swiss Government Control Board's

The Swiss government control board,
which supervises the activity of Swiss
and foreign insurance companies oper-
ating in Switzerland, has published its
report for 1917. The war did not j
diminish the activity of the life in-
surance companies in any European
country, but it strongly changed the
character of the securiites held as guar- j
antee for risks and liabilities, accord
ing to this report which says that at
the end of 1913 the percentage of gov-
ernment securities in the total of assets
of the life insurance companies was:
With Swiss companies. 33.2 per cent;
with German companies, 65.6 per cent;
with Austrian companies, 83.7 per cent;
with French companies, 38 per cent;
with the English companies, 20.2 per
cent; and with the American companiea,
7.8 per cent.
During the war the repayment of
mortgage loans, facilitated by the
strong demand of money for the gov
ernments. transformed the character of
the assets of the life insurance com-
panies. At the end of 1917 Swiss com
panies had 41.9 per cent of their as
sets invested in government securities,
the German 87.9, the Austrian 95.6, the
English 51.6, the French 38.0 and the
American 16 per cent.
It is remarkable that the French in
surance companies did not increase their
holding of state securities. The situa
tion with the Austrian insurance com
panies looks rather dangerous, for there
is no debtor remaining able to fulfill the
obligations for the securities. The
greatest proportional increase or the
state securities held by life insurance
companies occurred in the Anglo-Saxon
countries. Those insurance companies
which held many government issues in
pre-war times endured heavy losses, ow
ing to the depreciation of securities
yielding low interest. But the Swiss
control board Judges these losses less
dangerous than the eventual losses of
Swiss people insured with companies in
countries with depreciated currencies.
