PEKING. China. November 8.?
China seems to bo from one political
crisis to another. andt although each
new crisis further complicates the
political situation and adds to the per
plexities of those attempting to study
this country's affairs, the expected
collapse of China's government ma- i
chine is often averted. Time and time!
again students have said that China j
will be placed in the hands of an in- j
ternational receiver. Up to the pres-
ent it has not happened, but how she j
managed to muddle out of the manj
difficult situations that she has found
herself in during the last six or seven
years it is impossible to tell.
There is something in the national
character of China and her vast pop
ulations that gives her a certain
strength to overcome national diffi
culties and hardships that would
bring other nations to their knees or,
perhaps, to their complete downfall,
but the loosely riveted, badly con
nected and weakly controlled admin
istrative machine of China wobbles
along in an uncertain manner, but
never comes to a standstill.
Country Ha? Two Heads.
The country is divided against it
self. The north has a parliament, so
has the south; the north has a na
tional army, so has the south. Both
governments are divided among them-
selves, and both national armies are
fighting each other, or are ready to
do so. The northern government is
on the verge of bankruptcy: the
southern government has no funds.
The military governors of the prov
inces of both sides are a law unto
themselves: they help themselves to
the provincial revenues for the main
tenance of their troops, and the ad
ministrative bodies have to obtain the
money to carry on the work of gov
ernment from whatever source they i
can. Neither the Peking nor Canton I
governments can make their influence
felt outside of their immediate neigh
borhood, and. although mandates?and
governmental orders are Issued, no
one takes the slightest notice of them;
that is. unless the police are instruct
ed to enforce them, and even then it
is the poor and helpless that are com
pelled to obey.
Opium Still Mold.
An instance of this is the manner in
which opium is cultivated. The north
ern government has issued numerous
mandates, bearing the president's seal,
forbidding the cultivation of the
poppy, its manufacture into opium or
other preparation, and those found
selling or purchasing it are threatened
with heavy punishment, imprisonment
and fines. Tile southern government
has done the same thing, yet opium is
being grown in most of the provinces
today in larger quantities than ever.
In some provinces opium smoking is
publicly carried on, and even in the
large cities the extent to which smok
ing is indulged in is only limited by
the cost of the drug. The poorer
classes in these places are not able to
satisfy their cravings, but the officials
are in a position to smoke the same
amount of the drug as they did in the
days before the prohibition. Among
the members of the government today
there are many who are still addicted
to the habit.
The government is running into
debt monthly to the tune of J7.500.000,
mostly through having to maintain a
useless army of 1,300.000 soldiers.
As each pay day draws near reports
are in circulation that il is impossible
to find the means to pay the soldiers
and open mutiny is sure, but each
month the crisis is tided over some
way or the other. An issue of new
notes is made, a few million govern
ment bonds are thrown on the market
at 20 per cent of their face value, or
money is received from the native
banks and the salt surplus funds are
used, and so the government gets an
other lease of life for one more month.
But still it is a puzzle how it is done,
for common sense leads one to the
conclusion that frenzied finance of
this nature cannot last forever. That
it has lasted as long as it has done is
a mystery to all foreigners. For two
years now it had been generally ex
pected that the culminating point had
been reached and that the end had ar
rived. But nothing of the kind.
China's Kichnnge flood.
Strangest of all. China meets her
obligations, despite the critical finan
cial condition she is in. and her paper
on the foreign exchanges today stands
higher than that of the Japanese.
Moreover, the powers have promised
China that if she will only bring an
end to civil war and become united
again, they will come forward with
a large loan to help put her on her
feet and assist to hcip her national
development.
Considering her present financial
condition, it is strange to see the
financial organizations of the west
having confidence in this country. Al

Causes of the Unrest.
The causes of unrest are multiple,

it givee to middle class politics. There j

in the cortes. and power swings al- I
ternately from one radical taction to
another.
Another Phase of the Tnrmoll.
In the third place, conditions in one
province are quite different from
those in another. What exasperates
the people of Barcelona may not
anger the inhabitants of Madrid. This
militates against a general revolution
but facilitates local troubles. In
Catalonia, for instance, the spirit of
separatism reigns.- Its leaders de-I
mand autonomy, if not independence.
on the ground that their province
is different from the rest of Spain j
in history, language and industry.
Barcelona, and not Madrid, is the
