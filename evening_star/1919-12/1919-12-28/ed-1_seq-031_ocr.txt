By NEWBOLD NOYES.
CHRISTMAS week, Jim and Jane: the
holiday season of the Christian world.
The days ordained for peace on earth
and good will toward man. But these
are swiftly moving times, and the un
seen hand that is shaping the future of America
and of the world has no regard for the mean
ing of that jolly word?holiday. There is no
day, nor any hour of any day. when that hand
pauses: when the cycle of events which, while
but the passing news of today will be the his
tory of tomorrow, is checked. We have given
and received: we have hung the holly and mystic
mistletoe, and filled the stockings, and trimmed
the tree. We have made merry together, seek
ing for the hour to forget the hands of fatu.i
upon our shoulders, ever urging us forward to
whatever may lie ahead. But through it all
there has been the consciousness that there has
been no pause in the march of the events we
follow. So today let us look back, to marshal
those events in memory.

The Senate, it is true, adjourned op tn?
20th for a vacation of something over two
weeks. Wnatever difficulty they may have been
having in the north wing of the Capitol in get
ting together on the variety of matters before
them during the past months, one subject was
ultimately found in which party animosities
could expediently be set aside. The motion to
adjournment met with no dissenting voice.
Depression incident to the fact that the one
thing the Senate found itself able to agree on
was to quit for the nonce need not. however,
be too deep. For the opportunity lor informal
conferences with a vieVv toward compromising
the treaty deadlock was a welcome one to the
leaders of the factions and subfactions of the
upper house, and much headway is believed to
have been made. Born of these conferences we
hear today from all concerned that a com
promise will be reached early in 1920, and the
more optimistic are even prophesying that the
President may be given an opportunity to call
ofT war-time prohibition for a few days prior
to January 16.
Of course, each side in the controversy is
claiming that it is the other which is to crack,
and that the principles for which they have
contended are to triumph. And, of course, each
side knows in its heart that when the final com
promise is drafted, each will have had to give
about as much as each gets. It is refreshing
to hear the gospel of "give and take" preached
at last. Republicans and democrats, erstwhile
"bitter-enders," "don't-dot-en-I-ers" and mild
reservationists: they have all heard the unde
niable voice of Jim and Jane and a hundred
million odd others. They are aware that they

?week's activities give strong assurance that the

Prophets in these days eke out a precarious

actively on the Job in connection with whet ia

ith of January. And the consensus of their

That tht treaty?and the treaty includes the

move any chance of slight to our allies or any

visions as interpreted by the President.
From which faction the ultimate compromise
reservations will evolve is a matter of some
doubt. For It is a true, though pitiful, fact
that, so accustomed have the various groups be
come to regarding the problem before them in
the light of old-fashioned politics Instead of
broad justice, patronage by any democrat or
by any republican who has too flagrantly at
tacked the minority would doom even the most
promising child of compromise to failure
through prenatal influences. There seems to be
good reason for anticipating that the reserva

the mild reservationist group, with whom the
democrats will solidly join, together with a suf
ficient number of wabbling republicans to yield
the necessary two-thirds.

The railroads are to be returned to private
ownership on March 1, 1920. The President, in
drawing up the proclamation to this effect, was
motivated by two major considerations, aside
from the basic determination that expediency
and equity require that temporary government
ownership be relinquished. The first was that
his decision to return the roads upon January
1, as announced in Paris during May, could
not be put into effect without precipitating
chaos in the operation oi" the roads, in the finan
cial markets of the nation and among the pub
lic in general. The second was that some defi
nite date must be fixed lest Congress, lacking
the spur of definition, relapse upon reconven
tion to further dawdling.
There are at present before a conference
committee, composed of members from both the
Senate and the House, two bills purporting to
furnish the legislation necessary for protecting
ail the interests involved. These measures are
known as the Cummins and Esch bills. Kach
was hastily rushed through the chamber in
which it originated. Kach approaches a solu
tion of the problem from an opposite extremity
lo the other. Neither, it may be safely stated,
has progressed any considerable distance to the
common meeting point which must be attained
before March 1.
The conference committee, recipient of the
buck from both houses, has something less than
two months in which to travel the road to that
point. This is none too little time, nor is it any
too great. Widely diverging methods of ap
proaching such vital puzzles as to how railroad
labor disputes are to be handled in the future,
how strict government regulation is to be in the
future, how the roads are to be tided over the
crisis of their first years of return to private

isfactory to the north and the south wings of

With the necessity for having the needed
legislation on the statute books at the time the

promise will be reached.
There seems to be fairly good reason to ex
pect that the President, who assumes the role

express to Congress his ide&s on the problem

for the donkey and the elephant to get together
on the joint task which is theirs cannot be over

The coal crisis has not as yet been definite
ly passed. Just as the leaf upon which was
written the story of a strike which threatened
the nation with industrial chaos was about to

It may very well be that, having received

with the miners were satisfactory to the opera

The operators are definitely on record as
favoring: arbitration of the matters in dispute.
The most salient item of their objections today
is that the commission is to have the power to
fix wages after its investigations are completed.
The inconsistency of this position is apparent.
Arbitration means the hearing and determining
of a dispute. An arbitrator is a person who
has absolute power to decide a matter. The dis
pute between the operators and the miners can
not be settled through the medium of a mere
investigating committee, without power of
award when its investigations are completed.
There is no reason to believe that an agreement

operators subsequent to an investigation through
direct negotiation. The sole hope lies in each
placing their case in the hands of a just tribunal
and accepting with grace the award thereof.
Such a tribunal is the commission appointed
by the President. Its function will be to in
vestigate thoroughly the coal industry and de
termine, among other things, the wages and
working hours of miners for the immediate
future. If the miners are in justice entitled to a
greater increase than the 14 per cent already
accorded they must be given it. If the increase
can in equity be borne by the operators, it must
be so borne. If not, it must be borne by Jim
and Jane.
For Jim and Jane ask no more than that?
nor may the operators ask more and retain their
support.
