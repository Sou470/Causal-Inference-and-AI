The Senate's Minority Leadership.
The Senate's minority leadership
will not be decided until after the
holidays. The postponement was
probably a wise step.
This is an important post. The two
parties in the Senate are almost even
ly divided, and a great deal of busi
ness is on the card. A presidential
campaign approaches, and both par
ties, in the Senate and elsewhere, are
already on the alert. No bets should
be, or are likely to be, overlooked by
either side.
The leader of the Senate majority
is a trained legislator of large and
long experience. He served his novi
tiate in the House. He has been in
the Senate since 1893?a period of
twenty-six years. He, therefore,
knows all the ropes.
The minority is, of course, taking
.Mr. Lodge into account. It must face
I.iiu at every turn. His knowledge of
politics, his knowledge of congres
sional procedure, and the authority
which attaches to his conspicuous
party services are party assets of
value.
Mr. Hitchcock and Mr. Underwood
lire under consideration for the minor
ity's prize. Like Mr. Lodge, each
served his novitiate in the House, Mr.
Underwood rising to the majority
leadership of that body. Both have
shown quality since their transfer tc
the Senate. Mr. Hitchcock was con
spicuous even before taking charge of
the peace treaty, while Mr. Under
wood, by reason of the experience
gathered in the House, has from the
cutset received much attention wheit
ever be has taken the floor in the
Senate. Both are able and likely men,
and highly respected on both sides of
the chamber.
The nev leader, it i* conceded,
should match the old leader. Mr.
Martin was a very capable man, a
thorough partisan when partisan mat
ters were up, but in all other matters
broad and tolerant in his views, nnd
helpfnl in the transaction of business.
He was all alive, and a glutton for
work. Probably no member of the
Senate on either side was more regu
lar in his attendance, or more dili
gent while on duty.
For these reasons the minority does
well to weigh with all care the merits
of the two men presented for its
judgment. The man chosen will have
his hands full. He will find it neces
sary to keep his eyes open, from the
rising of the sun even unto the going
down of the same. All sorts of ques
tions will be coming up, and be
obliged to show the way for his side.
During the coming six months, indeed,
the leaders of both sides on Capitol
Hill will be tested for all they may
know about politics in all of its vari
ous forms, as well as about all that
enters into the government's business
in general.
The President's Strength.
The country, without regard to
party, welcomes the daily news of the
President's steady gain in strength.
It has, of course, sympathised with
him from the beginning of his illness.
So much business was in hand, and
he by nature so eager a worker, it
conld appreciate how great his im
patience must be at finding himself
disabled.
But another consideration now is
that the time approaches when the
President will need full strength to
meet an exacting situation.
When Congress reassembles after
the holidays a legislative grind for
months will begin. A variety of ques
tions will be presented. In many of
them politics will be a factor. A presi
dential campaign, to be conducted on
political lines, approaches. The Presi
dent is a democrat, and wants his
party to win next November. Con-,
gress is republican, and desires re
jmblican suecess then.
8nch a situation calls for confer
ences and concessions. The majority
leaders of Congress will want to meet
the President occasionally, and prob
ably he'them, for the adjustment of
differences growing out of their re
spective points of view and obliga
tions.
Ordinarily, the President is not dis
posed toward conferences with any
body. His party friends have com
plained of his aloofness. He consults
them but seldom. His habit is to
think things ont for himself, and then
announce his decisions.
This disposition has served him very
well sinee he entered the Whit# House.
Fntil recently, his party has been in
control on Capitol Hill, and very
much at his command. It has per
mitted him to shape many of the
measures enacted into law in the past
six years.
The republicans, of course, will in
sist upon a different procedure. Theirs
is the responsibility on Capitol Hill
now, though they lack the votes nec
essary to pass measures over the
President's veto. Whatever is done,
therefore?and much will press for
action?must represent the adjustment
of differences between them and the
President.
Quite recently, the President con
ferred with Senator Fall, a republican
leader, about Mexico. True, he did
not take the senator's advice. He pur
sued a course of his own. But he re
ceived the senator, and heard him
through.
If that may be accepted as the in
auguration of a new policy, the Presi
dent will need robust form to meet
the requirements of the next six
months. The republican leaders, and
the democratic leaders as well, in both
House and Senate, will want to see
him occasionally and sound him on
subjects of business. And a good deal
of business should be transacted be
fore the two national conventions
meet and call their respective sides
into the field.

and his conviction under the espionage
act, a majority of nearly 5,000 votes.
Had all the voters who were registered
gone to the polls Berger would prob
ably have been defeated. There were
about 9,000 of these, and on the reck

fully voted, those 9,000 absentees, in
effect, gave him the verdict, though
they were doubtless opposed to him.
Whatever the figures, the fact stands
that Berger will once more present
himself for admission to the House
and will.undoubtedly be again denied/
probably this time without inquiry by
a committee. The House cannot pos
sibly stultify itself. It must deny
