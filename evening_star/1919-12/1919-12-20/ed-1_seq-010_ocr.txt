BELIEVES EXCESS PROFITS
TAX CAUSES HIGHER PRICES
BY WILLIAM B. COI.VKR.
Member of the Federal Trade Cemmiuion.
(Thiii U Tertmtim report of ipwh niide
by Mr. folvrr Wednrjdej nif ht before the
mfmbrrt of the City Club.)
Whenever I have an opportunity
to speak I am impelled to raise my
voice against the excess profits tax.
I believe it would be a good thing if
the excess profits tax law could be re
pealed tomorrow.
Experience seems to have demon*
strated that the excess profits tax is
one of the foundation stones upon
which the present intolerable price
structure has been raised, and which
works such a hardship upon the con
suming public, and so, of course, upon
business concerns.
Between $4 and $5 is taken from
you and me and the man on the street,
as ultimate consumed, in excess
prices for every dollar that eventu
ally gets into the public Treasury. I
think that is going it little bit stiff.
If you knock the whole thing out. the
price structure would come down, 1
think, two or three stories at least,
and nobody would be hurt. Of course,
the government would lose this big
revenue.
What would we put in the place of
this tax? Plenty of devices have been
suggested for the raising of revenue.
For instance, a reasonably small di
rect tax that everybody can see, evi
denced perhaps by a stamp, on gross
sales. It would be something that
would stand right out something
that everybody could see. That same
tax would apply on everything?lux
uries and necessities and everything.
We would be a nation of taxpayers,
of course, and we would yell about
it; but we are a nation of taxpayers
now, and we are paying $5 for $1;
so, why not put it out where we can
see it. and take it and pay it, and
kick about it and have done with it?
Set Reveaae Device.
In the beginning, the excess profits
tax, as I understand it, was not a rev
enue device at all. During the war it
became necessary to fix prices of vari
ous commodities to keep the market
from going wild. When it came to
fixing prices on any given commodity,
no matter what it was. it was found
there was a very wide range In cost
of produotion of the same article in
the various processes of manufacture
in factories or mines or mills. So
long as the theory of fixing a single
price waa adhered to, it was necessary
that the price should be fixed high
enough to bring-into production the
amount, of the commodity that was
needed to supply the wants of the
war time. So the price was predi
cated on the cost of production based
on the high cost of operation then
existing.
The excess profits tax was proposed
as a means whereby the undue profits
which might.accrue to the low-cost
operations could be gathered back
into the Treasury to provide in part
the money with which the government
could pay the high fixed price, because
the government then, as you know,
was the biggest customer for every
thing. So they put a price on oopper
high enough to provide the last need
ed pound of copper, which was going
to be scandalously high with respect
to the lowest-cost copper mined; but,
since the government was going to
buy all the copper anyhow, the idea
was to take back the excess profits
that went to the low-cost plant so
that the cost could be recouped and
still pay the high fixed price which
was necessary to bring out the neces
sary production.
Tkrery ui History Law.
That is the theory of that law, and
that is the history of it. In other
words, the theory was?and I never
agreed with that theory for a min
ute?that by taking back SO, or 50.
or 60. or 80 cents on the dollar of
undue profit, a condition of private
profiteering would be avoided. I do
not think it has worked out that way
at all. It seems to me that it has
been an attempt to lift ourselves by
our boot straps, and that we have
been losing 7# or SO or 40 or 20 per cent
of the energy we have been putting into
the lifting process.
The inevitable result has been the
creation of a price structure which
has been, and is, intolerable and abso
lutely indefensible, affecting both the
producer and the consumer. The pro
unc?r is hurt just as much as the
consumer is: for he himself is the con
sumer of every other thing except
the one little thing he may produce.
Nobody, in other words, is playing
even on the game.
Since, as a matter of fact, the ex
cess profits tax was created as an
equalizer, in cases of price fixation,
and for no other reason, and since
prices are no longer being fixed, even
that reason for its continuance is
gone: and I am waiting for some one
to give me a reason for continuing it.
Nobody can say that it continues be
cause we need the money: for certain
ly we can find ways to provide the
money without losing 80 per cent of
the money that is collected from the
public as a tax.
SkralM K?t Be Tolerated.
l.eaky as tax methods may be. I do
not go along with this 80 per cent loss
in getting 20 per cent Into the Treas
ury, thus skinning the public?you
and I and all of us?out of 80 per
cent in the process. That is all wrongj
A price structure built upon such a
theory as the cannot and should
not be tolerated by the people of the
country.
Aside from the impossibility of such
a principle, viewed from the public
standpoint, it has a vicious effect upon
business itself. The reflect shows it
self in tax evasions, and, worst of all.
in inviting and encouraging wasteful
and extravagant business operations.
When a business reaches the point that
its excess profits tax will operate to
take away a considerable part of its

I think it would be a great blessing
and a great step toward the reduction
of the high cost of living if at this
moment the whole scheme of excess
profits tax could be abolished. There
never was so bad a device in business
as the device called "cost plus." and
the excess profits tax is in its essence
a "cost-plus" scheme.
Caa Increase Proflta.
Under a cost-plus arrangement, say,
of 10 per cent on cost, one can in
crease his profit by one dollar only by
wasting nine other dollars, and we
have that curious and impossible
situation of attempting to create
wealth by wasting it. In the excess
profits tax. when a concern has
reached beyond what is considered to
be its normal in profitableness, the
surplus is partially taken away from
it by increasing percentages. What
follows? Waste and extravagance, or
an effort to make as much profit with
the excess profits tax in operation as
though it were not in operation, and
that is done by charging excess profits
tax to cost. It means the collection
from all the people, when the 60 per
cent excess profits tax rate has been
reached, of a dollar, unnecessarily, in
order that 40 per cent may be added to
divisible profit.
And the excess profits tax or any
other tax is not paid by the taxpayer.
It is passed on?it must be passed on
?in the price of the product to the
purchaser of the product, and as it
passes on and on it grows, like a
snow ball rolling downhill, until it
reaches the public. And the public,
whether represented by railroad pres
ident, a skilled mechanic, a farmer, a
merchant, a common laborer, or a
financier, having had the composite tax
bill of all his predecessors parsed to
him. haises the price of his own labor,
whether that labor be stored up labor
in the form of capital, labor of the
hands, or of the mind, to meet the new
cost of living. So the distressing proc
ess. having made a complete circle,
starts again the endless spiral up
ward.
Tho excess profits tax boosts costs,
raises prices, halts production, breeds
waste, extravagance, and inefficient bus
iness methods, and raises the cost of liv
ing. Such a vicious law should be
I wiped off the statute books before an
other day passes.
