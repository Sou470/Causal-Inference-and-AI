In the first article of this series I
presented a brief summary of the law.
It was of such general application
that a number of explanations are in
order.
1. Cash vs. Accrual Basis?Taxpay
ers are privileged to render a report
of their net income on the basis of
either cash or accrual methods^
By cash method is meant a return
which takes into consideration the
cash received and the cash disbursed
during the taxable year. Ey the ac
crual method is meant a return pre
pared on the basis of income appli
cable to the period, whether actually
received or not: and expenses appli
cable to the period whether disburse
ments were reference thereto have ac
tually been made or not. A few ex
amples will suffice to mako the dis
tinction clearer.
(a) The owner of a tenement buys
ten tons of coal in November at $10
per ton, $100. If he reported his in
come and expenses on a cash basis, ho
would consider $100 as his expenses
for the year. If his report were on an
accrual basis, he would Inventory his
coal at the end of the taxable year
and consider as expense for the year
only the cost of the coal actually con
sumed.
(b) At the end of the taxable year
certain bills for telephone service, gaa
and electric light, etc.. supplied dur
ing the taxable year, may be unpaid<
If the return were filed on a cash
basis these unpaid items would not ba
taken into consideration. On the other
hand, if the return were filed on an
accrual basis, the unpaid bills would
be regarded as an expense, despite
the fact that they were as yet un
paid.
(c) In reporting on a cash basis, in
ventories of merchandise, etc., are not
taken into consideration. On an ac
crual basis, inventories are essential.
2. Bookkeeping methods?The law
does not prescribe the method of book
keeping which a taxpayer must em
ploy. It contemplates, however, that
some bookkeeping method is in use,
and that such method reflects the true
net income of the taxpayer. In such
cases it is presumed that the tax
payer's return will be filed on the
basis of his books.
3. Taxable year?The taxpayer's re
turn is presumed to cover, in general!
a period of twelve months ending De
cember 31. He may, however, render
a report on the basis of any yearly
period ending on the last day of Any
month. In fact, if his books are kept
on a fiscal year rather than a calendar
year basis he is expected to make al
report in accordance with his books.
4. Returns.
(a) Individuals: In my flr?t arti
cle I indicated to what extent indi
viduals are expected to render tax re
turns.
(b) Partnerships: Partnerships or
firms as such, are not subject to tax.
The individual members of such firm
are, however, subject to taxes on the
basis of their share in the net profits
fron* their Arm. regardless of whether
or not they have actually received the
share of profits to which they are en
titled. Despite the fact that partner
ships are not subject to tax, they
must file tax returns, disclosing their
income and expenses, at the same tim?
and in the same manner as Individ
uals.
(c) Corporations?Every business
corporation, regardless of whether it
has any net income or not, must file
a return. Certain classes of corpora
tions are exempt from tax and are not
required to file returns. The complete
list of tax exempt corporations is
given in section 231 of the revenuS
law.
(5) Information at the source?All
?individuals, partnerships and corpora
tions making certain payments
amounting to $1,000 or over are re
quired to furnish information of such
payments to the government. Section
256 of the law reads as follows:
"That all individuals, corporations
and partnerships, in whatever capac
ity acting, including lessees or
mortgagors of real or personal prop
erty, fiduciaries and employers, mak
ing payment to another individualj
corporation or partnership, of inter
est. rent, salaries, wages, premiums,
annuities, compensations, remunera
tions, emoluments or other fixed, or de
terminable gains, profits and income
of $1,0(00 or more in any tax
able year shall render a
true and accurate return to the com
missioner, under such regulations and
in such form and manner and to such
extent as may be prescribed by hira(
with the approval of the Secretary,
setting forth the amount of such
gains, profits and income and the
name and address of the recipient of
such payment."

from the ship by a Newfoundland
dog after their vessel piled up on
Martin's Point.
Boats could not make the hazardous
passage from the stranded steamer.
An effort to shoot the line ashore
failed when It became caught. Men
did not dare attempt the trip through
the waters, and so the dog was put
overboard.
Directed by officers of the Ethie, the I
intelligent animal succeeded in re
leasing the rope and, holding it tight
ly in his teeth, fought his way
through the breakers to tlje shore.
With block and tackle the Kthie's
crew, aided by fishermen on the shore,
rigged a life-saving device, using a
boatswain's chair for a carriage. One
by one in this chair ninety-one of the
ninety-two persons aboard were safe
ly hauled to shore. A baby eighteen
months old was pulled ashore in a
mail bag.
The Ethie, which had beeq. engaged
in the coastaJ service between Curl
ing and Labrador ports, went aground
last Wednesday during a gale, while
bound south. The wreck was not re
ported here until the shipwrecked
passengers and crew arrived from
Bonne Bay, all wires having gone
down in the storm.
