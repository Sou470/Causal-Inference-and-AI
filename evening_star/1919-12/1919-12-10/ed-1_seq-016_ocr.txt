Pointing to the fact that the inter

Lost Money Daring War.
Uncle Sam, during the war, it was

thousands of dollars, owing to th?

the Post Office Department lost so

North Capitol and Eckington
Citizens Would Check
Reckless Driving.
Measures for making the streets of
Washington safe for pedestrians,
especially children, formed the chief
topic of discussion at a special meet
ing of the North Capitol and Ecking
ton Citizens' Association last night, at
the Emory School. The meeting was
called, as explained by President Sel
den M. Ely, to finish up the work of
the association in the membership
drive. Two hundred and thirty-nine
new members were admitted to the
organization.
Mr. Ely and G. M. Koockegey, chair
man of the membership committee,
urged that the purposes of the mem- j
beiship drive be not forgotten, and
asked a pledge of every member of
the association to continue the can
vass for new members.
"The drive has exceeded my high- i
est hopes," Mr. Ely said, "but I liave
learned that the neighborhood has not
been thoroughly canvassed, and I urge
that the campaign for increased mem
bership be kept up until we secure 100
per cent membership in this section
of the city."
Discussion on the Question of mak
ing the streets of Washington safe
centered in a resolution proposed by
G. A. Lyton, which was referred to the
executive committee for action. The
resolution proposes that the associa
tion petition the District Commission
ers to urge upon Congress enactment
of a law prohibiting the taking of
collateral for automobile speeders,
and that an assured penalty be given
to any driver causing the death of any
person.
In offering the resolution. Mr. Lyon
said that scarcely a day parses with
out the death of some person being
reported as the result of a traffic ac
cident. and that some drastic meas
ure will have to be taken to stop
reckless driving. He urged that the
association take up an active study
of safety conditions and that the
executive committee appoint a street
safety committee empowered to act
with the police department.
J. L. Feeney urged that the board
of education take up the subject of
safety on the streets and that teach
ers instruct their pupils every day
in some of the principles of making
the streets safer.
William G. Henderson urged that
reckless driving in the streets be
stopped, and said that the police de
partment should co-operate with the
safety organizations by curtailing
fast driving. F. M. Jarvis said that
the examinations for automobile
licenses are not stringent enough and
urged enactment of a law requiring
prospective drivers to take rigid
tests to determine their fitness to op
erate an automobile.
In the city of New York. H. S. Har

are schools are patrolled by police
men during the h->urs when the chil
dren are out of school and vehicles
are held up by these policemen to
permit the children to cross the
street. He said some such procedure
should be followed in Washington.
