Woman Association Members.
One of the most gratifying features
of the citizens' association member
ship drive just conducted has been the
admission to many of the associations
of women. Some of the associations
have found to the astonishment of
members that their constitutions had
never prohibited the election of women
to the ranks and have warmly wel
comed them. In some instances eon
stitutional changes may be necessary.
In a very few cases opposition has
developed to woman members, but the
prevailing sentiment is strongly in
their favor.
The women of Washington can and
should do good work in the citizens'
associations. They are vitally concern
ed in all the questions that are dis
cussed and acted upon by them. They
have as much at stake in the correc
tion of untoward conditions as the
men. They are as keenly concerned
in the welfare of the schools, in the
efficiency of the police, in the ade
quacy of fire protection, in the state
of the sidewalks and pavements, in the
matter of street safety, in the in
numerable matters of municipal ad
ministration over whicl^ the associa
tions are exercising a sort of public
supervision.
It is in keeping with the prevalent
spirit throughout the country that the
women of Washington should be tak
ing their places in the ranks of these
organizations. Citizenship is now
equally vested in men and women in
many of the states, and in a short time
it is expected that the entire nation
except the District of Columbia?will
by federal amendment be put on an
equal suffrage basis. It is only right
that the women of Washington should
be aligned with the men in such a de
gree of citizenship activity as these
associations afford while waiting for
the day of enfranchisement which will
grant to both men and women full
American rights at the capital.

Dry Ratification Questioned.
A petition for mandamus to compel
the Secretary of State to show cause
why the prohibition proclamation
should not be set aside on the score of
the lack of referendum votes in states
recorded as ratifying has been denied
by the District Supreme Court, and an
appeal has been taken to the District
Court of Appeals. This sets in mo
tion the procedure for determining
the validity of the proclamation which
tixes January next as the date for
the operation of the federal amend
incut. It is held by opponents of fed
oral prohibition that the failure in
certain of the states to refer the rati
tication question to the people bus
ponds the effectiveness of ratification
in those states. In the case of Ohio
the ratification was reversed on a ref
erendum by a very narrow margin.
The proclamation of ratification was
issued undoubtedly without reference
to referendums, inasmuch as the Con
stitution provides that when a pro
posed amendment is accepted by the
"legislatures" of three-fourths of the
states it shall become part of the Con
stitution. The State Department is
not officially cognizant of other
methods of ratification. If the Con
stitution provided that ratification
should be by vote of the legislature, or
otherwise, as the state may elect, the
case would be different, as in every
instance of a change of mode the par
ticular method adopted by the state
would be established with the official
cognizance of the State Department.
The case turns upon the question of
whether the Constitution means lit
erally that ratification shall be by the
legislature or by any other method
which the state may propose. This
question must be answered by the Su
preme Court before it is finally set
tled. T'nless it is settled to the con
trary before January 16 the proclama
tion will be viewed as valid and the
federal prohibition will go into effect.
In view of the length of time that lias
been occupied in consideration by the
Supreme Court of the validity of the
war-time prohibition act there is little
likelihood of a decision on the major
question within the month now re
maining before general prohibition is
effective.
