Strikes and the Public.
The auti-strike provision of the Cum
mins railroad bill now under consid
eration by the Senate is a very inter
esting feature of that measure. It is
warmly opposed by organized labor.
The House refused to incorporate a
similar provision in the railroad bill
it passed. The sense of the Senate
on the question is yet to be taken.
Rut even if the Senate approves, still
another contest will come in confer
ence. So that the final result is much
in doubt.
The whole strike subject is very pat
today. Everybody is discussing it.
Everybody has some relation to it.
Those who art! not striking are in
some way struck. The coal strike has
brought the climax.
Since the subject is so pat, there
fore, and everybody at attention, the
Senate will be well advised if it goes
into it fully and in a broad spirit. It
has grown to be a broad subject. It
has angles and ramifications un
dreamed of at the start. It has been
pushed far, and there are signs that
if a check of some kind is not insti- j
tuted it will be pushed across the i
limit, and very grave trouble brought!
on.
The proposition that a man may
leave his employment if he does not'
like the wages paid or the conditions!
surrounding the labor required is in
disputable up to a certain point. The
point is that there are employments
and employments. In certain em
ployments, men take actually, though
not in terms expressed?not formally
?an obligation to the public. Coal!
miners, for an instance, offend against
public interests and good citizenship
when they extinguish their lamps and
throw down their picks at the begin
ning of winter when such action is
certain to produce widespread suf
fering. They are guilty of supreme
selfishness. Complaining of injury
themselves, they show a willingness
to inflict injury on others in number
a hundred times greater than their
own number.
Railroad trainmen are in this same
category. They owe much to the pub
lic. A paralysis of rail transporta
tion would produce insupportable, in
describable, conditions throughout
the country.
Both the miners and the trainmen
perform services of great value to the
public; and that fact should enter
into the question of their wages and
working conditions. They should be
well paid, and well protected while at
work. But so should the public in
some way be protected against any
abuse by them of the power they
possess.
There is so much in the subject of
interest and value to the public, it is
to be hoped the Senate debate may be
I both full and illuminating.
