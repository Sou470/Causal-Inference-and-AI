THE STORY THE WEEK HAS TOLD
By NEWBOLD NOYES.
THEHE is a good deal more satisfaction
to be derived through looking back
upon the seven days that have just
drifted past than was the case last
Sunday. For a week ago Jim and
Jane, seated side by side of a holiday
morning to review the affairs of the nation,
found things looking moderately dark. There
was the coal crisis and the Mexican crisis and
the crisis incident to the hesitancy of the Ger
man government to affix the required signatures
to the peace protocol. The crisis business was
being somewhat overdone.
And then there was the fact that the fate
of the peace treaty looked none too propitious.
To Jim and Jane it must have seemed that this
business of keeping posted on the trend of
events was a rather disheartening proposition.
Things are better this week. Things have a
habit of breaking that way for Jim's and Jane's
Uncle Sam. His nephews and nieces, without
going to the extreme necessary of a bona tide
optimist, should at least have reached t?ie point
where they may gaze upon transient clouds
upon the national horizon with the undismayed
vision of a mcliorixt, to coin a needed word.

The coal strike is ended. The national crisis
precipitated through the announced determina
tion of less than one-half of one per cent of
the population to starve and freeze the Ameri
can body politic to a gratification ol' their ambi
tious has been passed.
President Wilson's proposal, which formed
the basis for the agreement under which the
striking mine workers return to their jobs, was
briefly to this effect: The immediate resump
tion of operations, the workers to get a 14 per
cent increase in wages, the cost of which is
not to be borne by the public; the appoint
ment of a commission by the President to study
wages, profits and all conditions surrounding
the industry. When this commission shall have
reported a further revision in the workers'
wages, hours, etc.. may be put into effect. The
possible future revision would* be retroactive
to the date of resumption of work by the
miners.
Settlement upon this basis is hailed by the
workers as their victory, by the operators as
theirs and by the public as theirs, a fact which
speaks well for the diplomatic sagacity of the
President and the Attorney General. Concede
the victory as between operators and workers
where you will, there can be but one viewpoint
as to the greater victory?that of Americanism
over the doctrines of Lenin. An additional
blow has been struck upon the battered jaw of
that wabbling pork-and-beaner. old Radical
I^abor, and he is sagging toward the ropes. It is
a splendid fact that Uncle Sam, with a hundred
million patriots back of his corner, packs a wal
lop in each fist that no indiscriminately bearded
red can stand up to.

The criminal contempt proceedings against
those officials of the recalcitrant miners who
disobeyed the injunction forbidding the further
ing of an illegal strike have been, for the time
being, dropped. But the prophecy may be safe
ly ventured that it will be a long, long time be
fore they or any others contemplating similar
defiance forget that there la only one supreme
will or supreme power in these United States,
and that that will and that power, vested in the
government by every decent man and woman
in the nation, may not be defied with impunity.
The despotism of the law over the lawless is a
happy subject for contemplation.

On Friday the announcement of Fuel Ad
ministrator Garfield's resignation was made, on
the basis that the terms of settlement of the
strike as finally accepted contravene his con
ception of the main principle involved. The
main principle, as seen by the fuel administra
tor, was that the public should not be requested
to bear any part cf increased operation ex
penses incurred in meeting the demands of the
mine workers. The public, already staggering
under the heavy burden of living expenses, is
heartily in accord with Dr. Garfield in this
sentiment. The pity of his resignation lies in
the fact that while there is nothing in the im
mediate provisions of the settlement which
runs contrary to his expressed determination,
his assurance as to what future developments
may bring forth cannot but serve to give con
fidence to any who may at present be planning
to gouge the public.
The function of the commission as provided
fnr is to investigate and see that justice is done.
The public asks no more than this. Dr. Gar
field's resignation would have been better
timed had it been offered when the gouging
he anticipates had become a fact and not a
mere speculation. So timed it is more than
possible that he might never have had cause to
proffer it.

The peace treaty, than which no subject is
more prominently in the public mind these
days, continues to lie untouched on that mythi
cal table of the upper house of Congress. Presi
dent Wilson, claiming that the responsibility
has been shifted to other shoulders, expresses
a willingness to have it "lie there a while." The
republican Senate repudiates this responsibility
and indicates that the next move is up to the
President. And while Europe retches in the
sickness of uncertainty, and the baneful effects
of delay work untrammeled in America, political
hands are rubbed in the faulty confidence that
a ?ood job has been done in the old, old game
of passing the buck.
The time is not one for playing politics,
and even if it were, the political acumen of both
republicans and democrats were sadly at fault,
lit public wants the peace treaty disposed of.
AVhatever its final destiny may be. they want
that destiny decided. They are conscious of the
dangers of delay already too far prolonged.
Whatever political prestige is to be drawn from
the existing deadlock will accrue to that party
which can iu the next campaign say truly to
the nation: "We took the treaty from where
it lay, insisted that it be disposed of, and ar
ranged the compromise that brought you the.
established peace you enjoy at present."
The treaty must be settled. If the present
document is to be accepted under any condi
tions, the necessary compromise must be ef
fected and the ratified document forwarded to
Paris. If it is to be rejected, rejection must be

of battledore and shuttlecock, so hotly con
tested between the two major parties during the
past week in connection with treaty responsi
bility, should be abandoned. There is but one
responsibility incident to the treaty in which
the public is interested, and that is the joint
responsibility of their servants, the executive
and administrative branches of government, to
serve their will and place this nation on a peace
basis in double-quick time.
Open your eyes, gentlemen.

Machinery the World's Need.
BY WILLIAM JK\M\(;S UK VAN.
(Written fur The Sunday Star.)
MACHINERY is the means
by which the laws of na
ture are turned to the
service of man. Nature
has always been anxious to help
man, but can do so only when he
invents machinery through which
nature can work.
From the beginning the wind
has been blowing where it listeth.
but it was a Ions time before man
invented the sailboat and accept
ed the invitation to travel before
the breeze. Afterward man invent
ed the windmill, and r.ow the tire
less wind raises the water from
the hidden streams within the
earth. More recently man has in
vented the airship, and now rides
through the air "like swallows on
the wing."

The water has been tumbling
down the mountainsides for ages,
eager to fftrve, but how could it do
so until the genius of man devised
the water wheel? Now the energy
that once wasted itself in fretting
and in foaming furnishes power
for the mill.
Steam has been rising from boil
ing water since man came upon
the earth, but Its strength was dis
sipated because he did not know
how to harness and direct it. Now
it draws the seemingless endless
train across the prairies and makes
the steamship plow the fathomless
main.
The electricity that has always
surcharged the air formerly found
the lightning its only means of
manifesting its presence, but man's
arm has drawn it from the clouds
and man's mind has imprisoned it
in a slender wire so that, at his
command, it lights our homes and
supplies energy for myriads or
machines.

It was the machine for which all
these forces waited; it is the ma
chine that enables us to multiply
the strength of the human arm;
it is the machine that makes man
but "little lower than the angels"
in his wonder-working.
And so in government it is ma
chinery that translates public sen
timent Into constitutional forms
and needed legislative enactments.
It is machinery that enables the
public will to guard the public
welfare and to advance the public
good. Patriotism without ma
chinery through which to express
itself Is as impotent for good as
the wind, the water, the steam or
electricity. The great need of the
country and of the world today is
machinery. Our nation has taught
the world how to build a machine
that will put the people in control
of their own government and how
to make that machine responsive
to the people's will.

But much remains yet to be done.
The American people desire peace in
industry; they protest against a con
dition which leaves employer and
employe impotent to protect their
rights and interests except by resort
to the lockout or the strike. We
need machinery for the settlement of
industrial disputes before they reach
the acute stage.
Is it not strange that an intelligent
people, even in the presence of a
great emergency, are so slow to con
struct the machinery necessary for
the prevention of war between capi
tal and labor? A commission or
board, empowered to investigate at
the request of either side or on its
own initiative, and to lay all the
facts before the public Is much easier
to build than the machines that have
made man the master of the forces
of nature.
"Give us a machine," cry the peo
ple, "through which the high purpose
and the good intentions of the public
may find expression; give us this
machinery for the protection of em
ployer and employe, as well as for
our protection." Truth clamors for
expression; give it the means by
which it can express itself through
lawful forms by peaceful means and
thus silence the turbulence that fills
our days with anxiety and makes
every lover of his country solicitous
about the future.

NEW YORK, December 13. The
cure for social unrest in this country
is "a fuller knowledge of American

son in an article written for the cur
rent number of the Independent.
"Men today are blessed with a new
curiosity about their governments,"
says the President. "Everywhere they
are demanding that the doors behind
which secret policies have been incu
bated be thrown open and kept open
henceforth. The doors that do not re
spond to the keys the people hold willj
be battered down and free passage
ways erected in their stead.
"Autocratic governments of the
past have lived by concealment; free
government must live by understand
ing. In the new day that is dawning
only those governments that have no
secrets from their peoples can long
endure. I do not say that such a gov
ernment will make no mistakes, but
I do say mistakes will be fewer and
more easily corrected when all gov
ernments are guided by well informed
public opinion.
"Unrest is evident everywhere
throughout the world. It is not of
itself a disease, but a symptom of dis
ease. In our own country the disease
lies principally outside the govern
ment. Those who think otherwise are
mostly the newcomers and the men
they have influenced. The cure for
their disquiet is a fuller knowledge of
American institutions. In this natica
the people have in their ballots the
Instruments of peaceful chance.
"We can know if change is desir
