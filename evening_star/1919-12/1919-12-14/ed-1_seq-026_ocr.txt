RADICAL CHANGES
FOR LAWNJENNIS
National Association Pro
poses to Rearrange Gov
erning Code.
NEW YORK, December 13.?Radi
cal changes in the scoring rules, foot
fault code and In the association
form of voting will be proposed at
the annual meeting of the United
States National Lawn Tennis Associa
tion to be held in this city next Feb
ruary. These suggested alterations
were rounded into legislative form at
a meeting of the executive committee,
which did not end until early hours
this morning. The fact that the vari
ous proposals will go to the annual
meeting with the general support of
the committee, is thought to insure
their adoption by the organization as
a wholt.
The most striking change is the
proposal to eliminate the old system
of scoring entirely and provide a
1-2-3 method that will piake such a
score as "15 love" only a memory.
The proposed rule follows:
"If the server wins the first point
the score is called 1-in; if the re
ceiver wins the first point the score
is called 1 -out. If each player wins
1 point the score is called 1-all. If
the server wins the next point the
score is called 2-1; if the receiver
wins it the score is called 1-2, and
the.scoring is continued in this man
ner until either player has won 4
points, when game is scored for that
player, unless the other player has
scored 3 points: when the score is
called 4-3 or 3-4, as the case may
be, and game is scored for the player
who first thereafter gains a lead of
2 points."
The committee has swept aside the
old foot fault rule and proposes in
stead the following:
"Before commencing to serve, the
server shall stand wttn both feet at
rest behind: that is, rurther from the
net than the base line, and within the
limits of the center mark and the
side line. Both feet shall be kept be
hind the base line until the racket j
strikes the ball."
The present methoA or handicapping
is to be revised by the following rule
and simple table:
"The method handicapping shall
consist of allotting to each player
I one or more points in a set, these
points to be added to the points scor
ed by each player in that set. The
points scored shall be recorded at
the end of each game, and at the con
clusion of the set the allotted handi
caps shall'be added to this total. The
'player then having the greater num
ber of points wins the set, regardless
of the number of games won or lost."
A new plan of voting recognizes the
I playing membership of the clubs as
the basis for votes. Thus a club of
100 members or less would pay annual
dues of $10 and cast 100 votes. Larger
clubs would vote according to addi
tional membership, increasing the
voting power pro rata and the dues
by $2.50 for each additional twenty
five members. Clubs affiliated through
subassociations cast their full vot
ing strength. Country clubs, athletic
associations and organizations fos
tering tennis only in connection with
other sports will pay J10 dues and
cast 100 votes.
The next important change carries
out the association'^ policy of promot
ing "home rule" by requiring a club
in a section which has an organized
sectional association to obtain its
membership in the national body
through that suhassociation, instead
of jbining the U; S. N. L. T. A., direct
ly, as at present. This provision will
apply only to the California and west
ern association where the sectional
organization has reached its highest
development.
Regarding the Davis cup. the com
mittee recommended that all details
incident to this competition be re
ferred to the executive committee of
the association, with full power and
instructions to delegate its authority
as it may deem wise.
