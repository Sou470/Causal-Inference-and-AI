The question may be asked: How
are these eclipses foretold'' Astrono
mers have been carefully observing
the motion of the sun and moon for
ages and have learned from these ob
servations, not only how the moon
moves at present, but also how it will
move for ages to come, and have
learned one leading principle about
eclipses?that is. those occurring this
year have no obvious relation to the
eclipses occurring last year or to
thosit that will occur next year. Yet,
an extended view of the sequence of
these phenomena shows a very defi
nite principle in the physics of the
heavens, for if we observe all the
eclipses in a period of eighteen or
nineteen years, then we can predict
to at least an approximate truth all
the future eclipses for all the com
ing years.
It is only necessary to recollect that
in 6.5S5 1-3 days after one eclipse a
nearly similar eclipse follows. There
was an eclipse of the moon Decem
ber 5, 1881. If we count back 6.585
days from that date?that is, eighteen
years and eleven days?we come to!
November 24, 1863, when a similar!
eclipse of the moon took place. It
is this rule which enables the as
tronomer to predict the recurrence of
this phenomenon; and as long as the
sun and moon revolve in their present
orbits under the fixed laws of gravi
tation this phenomenon can be pre
dicted.
