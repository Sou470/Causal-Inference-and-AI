Warring Factions of the G. O. P.
Appear to Have Buried Hatchet
By N. O. MESSENGER.
ONE of the outstanding features of the
assembling of republicans of all fac
tions at the meetings of the national
committee in Washington last week
was the absence of aforetime friction
between the so-called progressive and conserva
tive factions. To all intents and purposes, no
such factional division is now discernible; if it
existed it was not in evidence.
Chairman Hays, speaking as one who should
know, declared the meeting to be the largest
assembly of representative republicans at any
national committee session ever held. There
were close to 500 people, man and woman work
ers, from all sections of the country, at the
meeting, and they included every type of for
mer factional classification, from erstwhile ram
pant bullmooser to the hard-boiled "Yeactlon
ary." But the point is they did not exploit
their former differences; they were republicans,
united for what they believed will be a smashing
victory in 1S20 if they stick together.

Another dominant note of the gathering was
the enthusiasm and energy developed, the busi
nesslike method of procedure adopted and the
unmistakable evidence of determination to go
ahead in confidence and unity with a presi
dential campaign upon lines which promise to
be broader than ever before pursued by the re
publican party and to get out and away from
restricting partisanship to a notable degree. A
neutral observer, familiar with past perform
ances, could not but be cognizant of this trend
of the meeting.
Women were welcomed sincerely In the work
of the committee and took their places natur
ally, as to the manner born. The policy of the
republican management is to treat the women
not as an auxiliary element in tho campaign
and the voting, but as component part thereof.
Theirs will be no ladies' aid society, but they
will work in harness with the hard-headed, old
"practical politicians."

The more the assembled party managers
analyzed the innovation in committee work pro
posed by Chairman Hays and enthusiastically
adopted, the greater were the possibilities per
ceived to be contained in the plan. They were
pleased with the idea of assembling and co
ordinating the best thought of the republicans
of the country to be used in the construction of
a platform next June. It was readily under
stood that this is no plan to foreclose the ef
forts of the committee on resolutions of the
convention, which will be composed of men se
lected by and from each state delegation, but to
present that committee with material. For the
next six months "the best brains of the repub
lican party of the country." as Chairman Hays
put It, will cogitate constructive measures to be
incorporated into the party's declaration of
principles, aims and desired legislation. It was
claimed that voters of the country in sympathy
with the republican party will demand at the
hands of the party a great legislative and ad
ministrative program. The managers want to
go further than to satisfy this 'icinand; they
aim to present, if possible, such a program as
will attract the great independent vote of the
land and possibly draw reinforcements from
the opposition party in men to whom such a
program would appeal.

The creation of an advisory council, to con
sist of twelve members of the national com
mitee and twelve representative republicans at
large, was declared to be an important step in
the direction of maintaining harmony among re
publicans. It gives, at the outset, republicans
of all shades of previous factional opinion a
place in the national councils of the party, from
which their views and opinions can be heard and
communicated to the national managers, to be
embodied in action. In the selection of mem
bers of the council from the committee, as well
as in choosing them from the body of republi
cans generally. Chairman Hays was careful to
accentuate this policy by placing upon the com
mittee men and women recognized as prominent
in the one-time factions. Murray Crane of
Massachusetts and George W. Perkins of New
York, Boies Penrose of Pennsylvania and Ray
mond Robins of Illinois will be side by Bide as
representative of the extremes of factionalism
heretofore militant, but in the future, it is
hoped, to be submerged to the general party
welfare.
It remains for the future to demonstrate
how the plan will work out. It was entered
upon with hearty good will and general ap
proval of Chairman Hays' intent.

No state, national or domestic questions
were touched upon in the proceedings of the
committee. Views of attending members were
obtainable only in conversation and conference,
"out of court." In that realm of discussion
there was developed the seeming tendency of re
publicans to the opinion that, touching the league
of nations, it will be essential for the republican
Senate to dispose of the treaty of peace and the
league of nations; to dispose of it in some way,
the manner and method to'be left to the discretion
of senators, but a disposition to be reached with
out fail. There were diverging opinions as to the
scope of reservations, but reservations were
classed as secondary in importance to the main
Issue of "getting the treaty out of the way." On
?very side apprehension was voiced of the danger
of allowing the treaty to be still pending when the
campaign comes on. It was feared that the re
publicans would be held responsible for the dis
quietude expected to result from delay. The re
publican visitors were agreed in the main that
the country is sick and tired of the war and Its
aftermath, of the treaty and all talk, and wants
an end to it. "Do something with it, and do it
soon," was the finality of utterance in all discus
sions.

Talk of candidates? Yes. naturally. Th?re
were men and delegations on hand whose busi
ness it was to make talk and keep it going. But
it didn't get anywhere. National committeemen
were gunshy on the candidate question as a rule,
beyond a more or less perfunctory Indorsement
of their "favorite sons."
All the big leaders in the gathering were of
opinion that the convention will name the candi
date, and that there is little likelihood of any of
those who are now mentioned going to Chicago
with the nomination assured in advance. The
old war horses of the party who were present
told the youngsters of the hot fights in conven
tions of former days, when roll call after roll call
was necessary when delegations traded off and
shifted between roll calls, when there were dick
ering and bluffing and scheming, and they wanted
to see the old times come back. The existence in
so many states of primary laws for the selec
tion of delegates to national conventions will
work in favor of an open convention, as delegates
will be sent with instructions or preferences of
choice from many states and unhampered by in
surrections in others.

The formal launching of the Harding presi
dential boom and annihilation of the Watson pres
idential boom attended the gathering of the clans.
When the Ohio men realized that Gen. Wood and
Gov. Lowden had campaign committees actively
at work, with their candidates formally in the
field, the Ohio delegation in Congress met and
indorsed Senator Harding. The Indiana delega
tion wanted to do the same for Senator Watson,
but the interposition of the ambitions of Gov.
Goodrich of that state interposed and prevented
unanimous action.
In the Indiana case the net outcome, as
viewed by practical politicians, is that Senator
Watson is at least assured of the nomination for
the senatorship, while Gov. Goodrich still has
to establish his ambitions, barring the senator
ship, and take Iris chances for what he may be
ablo to get. The politicians think Gov. Goodrich
wants the vice presidency, but if there is any
prospect of a factional fight ensuing in Indiana
as result of the Watson-Goodrich conflicting
claims for preferment the convention may turn
elsewhere, it is said, for a choice for second place,
in which even Senator Watson will be found to
have picked the only plum in the Indiana pie.
