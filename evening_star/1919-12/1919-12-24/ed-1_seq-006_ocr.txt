Leave the Schools Alone!
A general survey of the public
schools of the District is proposed,
to be conducted by the bureau of edu
cation under the auspices of the
board of education. The prospect is
that this survey will aim at a general
reorganization of the school system,
going deep into the methods of teach
ing, the relationship of school parts,
the division of duties and the classi
fication of teachers.
The teachers of the schools are
naturally distressed at this prospect.
They have been harried by changcs
for some time past, have been bur
dened sorely with extra duties as the
ranks have been depleted in the
yielding of many to temptations to
CO to higher paid employments, have
been compelled to care for more than
the normal number of pupils, have
been hampered by inadequate school
room and constantly fretted by the
lack of proper compensation and the
uncertainty of relief.
If the schools of the District need
one thin* more than another at thia
time it is respite from change and
menace of change. A survey such as
ia proposed cannot possibly disclose
conditions that are not fully known
Row to the authorities. Before any
thing else is undertaken in the di
rection of effecting betterments in
the educational methods and organi
zation there should be more build
ings, a larger teaching force and a
better pay scale, together with a re
tirement system that will guarantee
the faithful members of the force
provision for their declining years
when they have passed the point of
maximum usefulness.
Reorganization, such as is pro
posed in the shape of "reclassifica
tion" plans or such as may be de
veloped through a survey, cannot
tore the fundamental inadequacies
l'rom which the system is suffering
today. It cannot add to the physical
equipment or increase the pay scale.
It will not reassure the teachers and
give them the quiet of mind that
is necessary to the end of good class
room work.
Give the schools a rest for a year
or two and allow them to repatn the j
balance that has been diafutbed
through months of agitation and
change suggestions. The District's
system ia fundamentally sound, the
teaching staff is excellent and faith
fnl and the organization is adequate,
with perhaps the addition of an offi
cial to assume the responsibility of
business management, to relieve the
superintendent of a burden that
should never be laid upon his shoul
ders. If the schools of the capital
art to be used as an experiment sta
tion for the trying out of theories
and essays in new methods there will
be no end to the disturbances and
discord that are so fatal to good teach
in* and so harmful to the welfare of
the pupils.

The Day Before Christmas.
The last day before Christmas!
The belated shopper is rushing about
finding the things he has tardily
thought to buy. The last-minute
mailer is hastening to the post office,
to find hundreds of others like liim
standing in line before the weighing
scales, to mail parcels that should
have gone a week ago. The cars are
packed with bundle carriers, trying
to take home in one load the goods
tha? should hare been transported
bit by bit long since. It is a time
of general hustle and repentance for
failure to obey the injunction to
"shop early."
Every year we make good resolu
tions to do our Christinas shopping
in fair season, and every year we
forget to clean up our lists until the
last few hours. It is not alone a mat
ter of Christmas preparations. It is
so in many other matters. We fail
to profit by our experience and pay
the penalty of neglect and inatten
tion.
In a few hours it will be over, the
last gift bought, the last bit of green
obtained, the final touches for the
Christmas dinner provided. And at
that in many households there will
be some arrears of things that might
have been done had there been time.
But, even so, it is a wonderful sea
son. It is well worth the rush and
hurry and fret of planning and figur
ing and dividing and subdividing
funds. For there will be gladness on
the morrow. There may be a few
who will not have a merry Christmas,
whose homes are sad with want and
need and suffering. But for these
there is the cheer of the thought that
the Christmas spirit is with them,
even though it takes no tangible form
in tokens of remembrance or gifts
of affection.
On this last hurry homeward drop
a coin into one of the kettles and
pots of the organizations that are
providing Christmas cheer for those
who will otherwise be neglected sKd
forgotten on the day of days. Even
if it is the last coin left, let it go to
carry Christmas to the poor, for whom
He pleaded whose day dawns on the
morrow. I

Kansas Mine Workers' Union, has
seen the light. Facing a prison term
for defying the mandate of the fed
eral court at Indianapolis requiring
him, with other miners' officials, to
call off the strike, he has weakened
in his resolution to go to jail as a
martyr and has announced that he
will obey the order of the court.
This is the sensible thing to do.
Judge Anderson, in pronouncing I
judgment on Howat's case the other
day, declared that the issue was
whether he should abdicate to the
Kansas leader. The Kansas miners,
to some extent, stood by Howat and
left the mines in protest against his
treatment in court, but his own re
cantation leaves them no alternative
but to return to work.
Had Howat been permitted to re
main in defiance of the court with
out punishment there would have been
no end to the conflict. Such defiance
of the law could not have been tol
erated. The Palmer-Lewis agreement,
challenged now by the operators,

of paper." for there conld be no dis
tinction between the miners in Kan
sas and those in other states. The
Kansas contention was that the strike
there had begun before the general
<-!rike, and was based on different
grounds from those underlying the
general suspension of work. The
court could sec no difference between
strikes.
