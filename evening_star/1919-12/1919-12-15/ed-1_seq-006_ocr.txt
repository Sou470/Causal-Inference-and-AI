Bolshevism and Education.
Addressing the Lawyers' Club of
New York Saturday. Senator Kenyon
said: "You can deport an individual,
but you cannot deport an idea. The
ruri' for bolshcvism is Americaniza
tion. and the road to Americanization
is education."
The Iowa senator i> a force for good
in the contest now in progress for up
holding American institutions and tra
ditions. His actions tit his words. It
was he who moved, and presided over, j
the Senate's investigation of the steel
strike. Recognizing a serious situa
tion. and the necessity for full light
on the controversy, he asked for and
obtained an official investigation. He
did not impute wrong or wrong inten j
tions to either side, but demanded the j
facts. He took the wholesome Ameri
can ground that the public was entitled
to the facts.
He is on safe and solid ground now.-, j
Education?the real article?is the
remedy for many of the ills with
which the country is afflicted today.
The enemies of America living within
the gates must be taught what Amer
ica is. and what its line of progress
is, if they are to be turned from their i
present views and made good citizens.
Not all of them are foreign born.
But what of the teachers? They are
not agreed among themselves about
what America is, or what the line of
progress should be. There are men
who reprobate in the strongest terms
the proposition to overthrow the gov
ernment by force, and yet in order to
carry a selfish point are willing to
paralyze the country's industries. Ma
jority rule and the public welfare seem
to have no meaning for them. They
are of. by, and for a class?their own I
class?and active only for class ad-
vantages.
The foreign-born malcontent is less
censurable than the native-born of the
species. As a rule, the former, from
his early experience, associates gov-
eminent only with force. When he
thinks of a change it is in connection
with force. That weapon occurs to
him instantly, and, one may say, al
most naturally. But the latter, born
and reared under American institu
tions and processes, has all his life
known better things?the best of gov
ernmental things known?and is to be
held to a far higher and more rigid
accountability.
The famous adjuration, "Physician,
heal thyself,'' should be adapted and
applied in the present case. "Teacher,
teach thyself." If we are to teach ef
fectively, we must come to an agree
ment among ourselves about what is
to be taught. We must all teach the
same thing: That is, respect for au
thority, and the paramount duty to
employ only those agencies when
changes in the law are desired that
are provided for in the great charter
?the Constitution.
New Orleans doubtless has its local

Canvassing for the Presidency.
Senator Johnson has the courage of
liis aspirations. He wants to be Presi
dent, and announces the fact. He is a
candidate for the republican nomina
tjon to that office, and will soon begin
a tour of the country?a swing around
the circle?to discuss the subject with j
the voters at their homes. He will
visit every state. A large purpose,
and a large program.
In this way, the senator will gather
a good deal of information about the
desires and expectations of the people
respecting the presidency, and they
be able to appraise from face to-face
meetings his qualifications for the of
fice. The plan thus will yield some
thing to both the seeker and the
bought.
At the outset of his tour the senator,
perforce, will discuss the issues that
now present themselves and are under
general discussion. The people at the
moment are most interested in them.
Their curiosity attaches to what ex
ists rather than to what may exist.
But as time passes and other issues
arise, the senator will have to tackle
them as they arise. Other issues are
certain to arise. New issues are in
the air, so to say. The period in that
of development. Things do move, and
parties and politicians must move with
them.
Whether the California senator's ex
nmple will be generally copied is a
question. It is out of the ordinary,
but the ordinary is not in fashion
these days. New times, new ways. The
old order has changed.
As an illustration, take the case of
Mr. Bryan. His tours about the coun
try for a year or so prior to the Chi
i ago convention of 1896 were not an
nounced as having a presidential
|nomination in view by him. And yet
they had a very heavy and distinct
bearing on his subsequent fortunes.
On his tours he had discussed issues
then ripe or ripening, and given the
people a clear idea of where he stood.
As a consequence, when he rose in
that deadlocked and impatient national
convention and electrified it with a
rattling stump speech, his words were
familiar to many who had heard him
in similar deliverances at their homes;
and, freshly impressed, they now
plumped for him for leadership. The
j bread he had cast upon the waters re
turned to him.
There is plenty of time for such a
canvass as the California senator has
laid out for himself. The republican
convention is si* months away. The
railroad service will soon Ite normal
aeain; and candidates can be whisked
in and out of states at top speed. Peek
ing offire has general approval now;
and the practice reaches to the peak.
The presidency itself is sought, as are
places of modest caliber.
The Collateral Farce.
I?ast Saturday forty-five persons for
feited collateral in the Police Court
when their cases were called. All of
them had been arrested for various
violations of the traffic rules. Their
forfeitures ran from $2 to $10. Two
of them had been arrested for break
ing the rule requiring the stopping
of a vehicle fifteen feet behind the j
entrance of a halting street car. One i
of them forfeited $5 and the other
$li>. One defendant, charged with a
like offense, answered the call in
court and was fined *5 and placed on
probation. The variation between the
two collaterals forfeited for the same
offense is not explained, but it serves
to disclose the absurdity of the pres
cnt system of taking cash bail upon
arrest. There is evidently no uni
fonnity about the matter. Nor is there
any relation between collateral taken
and punishment inflicted.
The whole collateral system is wrong
and should be revised. When forty
live rule breakers can escape in one
day with the payment of from $2 to
$10, with the discomfort merely of a
few minutes of delay and a vijit to a
station house, there will be no cessa
tion of violations. No headway can
be made by the traffic squad against
traffic law defiance when the sole pen
alty faced by those arrested is this
trifling payment. Apparently there
is no method of keeping tab on the
number of times an individual is ar
rested, perhaps for precisely the same
offense. There can be none, with the
collateral system in vogue, practiced at
thirteen different places?eleven pre
cinct stations, one substation and
headquarters. There is no way in
which the license numbew of offend
ers can be kept on record so as quick
ly to determine whether a person ar
rested is an old or a new offender.
If the Senate District committee
wants to make Washington safer it
should take into consideration this
matter of collateral, together with the
question of establishing a traffic court,
with the penalties for rule breaking
made so stiff that they cannot be
evaded with impunity or paid with
out flinching. Let it be once estab
lished as the law here that a breach
of the traffic rules means an assured
response to court, with the prospect of
a heavy fine for first offense, for
feiture of license for second and im
prisonment for third, and there will
quickly be relief from the "accidents"
that are now making Washington in
famously unsafe.

A youth with a gun, bent on robbery,
took a life in this city yesterday and
dangerously and perhaps mortally
wounded a policeman who later ar
rested him. The gun was bought in
Krie, Pa. It might as readily have
been bought here. Kor in scores of
shop windows and showcases in this
city pistols are displayed for sale, and
can be purchased by anybody with the
price?a comparatively small price?
without the least difficulty.
Though this case is not one of the
numerous kind that points so plainly
to the ease with which killing weapons l
are obtained here, it nevertheless ac- j
cents the need of legislation that will
protect the people of Washington, as:
far as this jurisdiction extends, from
Ithe hastily obtained pistol. The pres- j
ent law forbidding the carrying of
'weapons is absolutely worthless. It
cannot be enforced until after the fact
of gun use. save in the rare instances)
wh*n a pistol carrier is arrested for I
some other offense and the gun is
found on him.
Gun carrying is an evil that menaces
every life in the capital. Repeatedly
recommendations have been made to
Congress for the enactment of a law
which will provide a severe penalty
for the sale, gift or exchange of pistols
save upon presentation of a permit
obtained from some designated au
thority. Xo action has ever been taken
on these urgings. They constitute a
remarkable file of ignored pleas in tlie
archives of the House and Senate Dis
trict committees.
The record of lives that have been
sacrificed because of the ease with
which murderously inclined persons
can arm themselves is appalling. It
calls for attention. Will not the Dis
trict's legislature take cognizance of
this evil and quickly move to put an
end to a traffic that aims at the lives
