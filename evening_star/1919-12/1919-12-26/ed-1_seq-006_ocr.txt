Chronic Criminals.
Four men were released from the
Toledo, Ohio, jail early Christmas
morning by a gang of half a dozen
confederates. Every one of these
prisoners had a long criminal record.
One man had served part of a term
for safe robbery and had been par
doned, but recently was convi*.ed in
Toledo of manslaughter and sentenced
for twenty years. Another is known
to the police of many cities as a thief,
having at least six aliases; he was in
jail for stealing a quantity of silk, j
A third man had served terms for
various crimes in different parts of
the country and was held in Toledo
for carrying concealed weapons. The
fourth had, perhaps, the most unsav
ory record of the quartet; ia 1917 he
was given a nineteen-year sentence
in New York for holding up a gam
bling resort, served a few months and
escaped; he was later involved in a
crime in Chicago and escaped, being
eaaght in Toledo and returned to Chi

went back to Toledo, where recently

An organization has existed for a
number of years known as the Police
Chiefs' Association, which promotes
the interests of justice in the identi
fication of fngitive criminals and the
speedy extradition of a "wanted" man
to tho jurisdiction of the charge
against him. It has worked well and
proved the need of the closest co
operation to fight crime in this coun
try. Identification methods have
been standardized. Records are ex
changed. It is hard for a man who
breaks the laws to get away for long,
within the confines of the country.
Set, as this Toledo jail delivery shows,
men are able to defy the laws and to
remain freo after escaping from
prison. Their successive crimes do
not count heavily against them. The
man who "jumped" Sing Sing and
later was sent to Chicago from Toledo
was released on bail, which he vio
lated.
An interstate judicial system is
needed, supplementing the unofficial
Police Chiefs' Association. Courts
should scrutinize the criminal records
of all brought before them, to deter
mine whether or not they are chronic
lawbreakers. Such men should be
put away for long terms. They are
a menace to society. Prison does not
cure them, evidently, nor is parole
effective. If they cannot be cured
of their criminal habits they should
be kept in close custody, and the laws
bhould be so written in the states as
to permit the courts to fix the terms
of their sentences to accord with the
length of their criminal records else
where.
