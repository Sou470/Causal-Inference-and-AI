Engliih Delegate Believes Plan
Would Stali lixe Foreign
Exchange.
A proposal for the establishment of in
ternational currency as a means to
stabilize foreign exchange has l>een sub
mitted by Marshall Stevens of Manches
ter. Kngiand, to members of the United
States Chamber of Commerce, now giv
ing attention to international finances.
Mr. Stevens was one of the British
delegates to the international trade con
ference. which met in Atlantic City
the latter part of October. It is un
derstood that his plan for an interna
tional currency will be considered along
with other plans by the national com
mittee on Kumpean finance, now in the
process of appointment by the Chamber
of Commerce.
The committee is headed by Harry
A. Wheeler, vice president of the
Union Trust Company of Chicago, and
?lames K. Alexander, president of the
National Bank of Commerce of New
York and 'chairman of the executive
commit tee.
Mr. Stevens, it is understood, has
discussed his plan with bankers and
business men in this country to a
considerable extent. He proposes the
issue by the allied governments of a
guaranteed currency. This currency,
he suggests, should he placed In cir
culation by the purchase of war bonds
of the allies.
Mr. Stevens' idea is that such a cur
rency would standardiie payments for
international transactions, and that
it would be supplementary to any
plans for extending international
credits. It would supplant the exist
ing exchange business without elimi
nating it.
The guarantee per unit of such an
interallied currency would not, he
contends, depend upon the value of
the war bonds, but upon the definite
amount of commodities, distributed in
various countries for a definite
amount of currency.
