Slower Speeds, More Safety.
Motor speed, which causes most of j
the so-called aeeidents in the streets,
is not necessarily a matter of "scorch
ing." The swiftly rushing machine is
highly dangerous, to the driver and
occupants as well as the pedestrians
and other motorists. But there is a
slower speed that is also dangerous to
pedestrians, one at which the machine
cannot be quickly brought to control
or halt. That is the speed at which
machines figuring in mishaps on the
streets are commonly propelled. In
the majority of cases the motorist is
not actually violating the speed rules.
Yet when a child suddenly starts from
the curb, or an elderly person steps
off into the street, directly in front
of the car, it cannot be halted in time
to avoid a collision.
The difference between running a
car at a speed that is actually safe
and one that is dangerous is very
slight in terms of the time consumed
in the trip. In only the rarest cases
is hurry necessary or excusable. A
physician responding to an emergency
call, an ambulance answering a sum
tnons, the fire apparatus going to a
blaze, these are justifiable cases of
speeding. But the other street, users
do not need to g? so fast. They do
not need to go beyond the pace at
which the car can be brought to a
halt to avert a collision. There is no
necessity for a sharper pace.
If every car that is tub in the
streets of Washington were driven at
such a pace that it could be halted
within its length there would be a
reduction of accidents by moreMhan
half. And nobody would be the
worse for such a slackening of speed.
Each run would be lengthened in time
by only a very few minutes. Instead
of getting to destination in five min
utes it might be necessary to allow
ten. And at the. end of a year the
accumulated differences in time would
stand as the contribution of the motor
ists of Washington to the city's safety.
At every corner there is too much
speed. At every crossing the cars are
run too fast to enable pedestrians to
get over the way in safety. In pres
ent conditions they must, be on the
jump, looking both ways at once and
taking their chances, unless they hap
pen to be at one of the "Go?Go"
points. Give the pedestrian a bet
ter chance for his life and all Wash
ington will 'be the gainer and the
motorists of the city will have less
burden on their consciences.
