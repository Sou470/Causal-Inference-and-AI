Small Pay a Factor.
Not only the enlisted personnel of
the Navy, but that of its officers as
well, has been seriously affected by
the drain imposed by the higher
wages paid by the United States
Shipping Board and in civilian life for
men of their equipment and experi
ence. Their emoluments, stationary
since 190.x, have made no progress in
the direction of keeping pace with the
tremendously increased costs of liv
ing.
In a summary of the situation as it
involves officers submitted to the com
mittee on naval affairs. House of Rep
resentatives. by Frank J. Sprague.
member of the naval consulting board.
November 19, comparisons of the pay
received by Navy officers is made with
that received by mechanics and even
unskilled workmen in civilian life.
Comparison Made.
It is set forth that the pay of a lo-
eomotive engineer at present equals
that of the captain of a vessel; that
the conductor of a freight train re
ceive as much as a commander in the
Navy: that the driver of a city milk
wagon is paid as much as a lieuten
ant. and that any mechanic who can
speak English receives as much as an
ensign.
legislation pending in Congress
under the Stiness and Wadsworth
bills contemplates an increase of 50
per cent in the pay of enlisted men
in the Navy and 30 per cent in that
of officers. If passed it is believed
this legislation will have a tendency
to stimulate enlistments, although
even then the wages of enlisted men
will not equal the emoluments of
similar work for the United States
Shipping Board or numerous privately
