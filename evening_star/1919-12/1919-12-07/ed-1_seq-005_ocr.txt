iSENATE BILL CHANGES
D. C. CAPITAL PENALTY
Electrocution would be substituted for
hanging in cases of capital punishment
in the District of Columbia under' the
terms of a bill which passed the Senate
yesterday afternoon. The bill was in
troduced by Senator Dial of South
Carolina and had the approval of the
District Commissioners.
In asking the Senate to pass the bill
Senator Dial argued that electrocution
was more humane than hanging and
called attention to a considerable
number of states which have adopted
this method of Inflicting the death
penalty upon criminals.
Senator King of Utah took exception
to this statement, saying that he be
lieved shooting or hanging would be
more humane than death in the elec
tric chair. "He said that he could
imagine nothing more frightful than
the adjustment of all the mechanism
of the electric chair before the cur
rent is turned on to kill the condemned
criminal.
Under the terms of the bill electro
cution would take the place of hang
i ing in the District after March 1, 1920.
