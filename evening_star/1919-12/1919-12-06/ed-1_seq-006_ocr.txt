Jenkins' Release.
Release of Consular Agent Jenkins
from jail at Puebla lias quickly
changed the situation and greatly
eased the strain between this country
and Mexico. It is of no moment for
the present whether Jenkins was re
leased by the government of Mexico
at the instance of the TTnited States,
or upon the tender of bail. He is now
at liberty and the primary cause of
the immediate friction between the
two countries is removed.
If Jenkins was released on bail it
was evidently without his initiative
or desire. He has heretofore refused
to apply for release on that condition,
on the ground that to do so would be
to admit the right of the Mexican au
thorities to arrest him. If somebody
else has tendered bail for him it may
hare been at the secret instance of the
government at Mexico City. Such a
trick would not be unthinkable.
With the Jenkins ease removed to
the category of discussion there re
mains no acute issue between the two
countries. The cause of the repeated
affronts and indignities, however, is
not cured. Mexico is hostile in spirit
toward the United States and toward
all Americans. The future holds no
guarantee of relief from these con
stantly recurring occasions for protest
and demand.
Charges are made that the govern
ment at Mexico City has connived at
the introduction of radical agents and
propaganda literature into the United
States over our southern border. That
will be a difficult thing to establish,
and when established, if that is pos
sible, it does not necessarily stand as a
cause of intervention in the troubled
affairs of Mexico. If it develops as a
distinctly "unfriendly act? on the part
of the Mexican administration it
stands as cause for specific protest and
possibly may develop into cause of
action. At present it merely adds to
the irritation.

Congress and the Mandate.
What was the mandate the repub
lican party received from the people
in November, 19181 It did not cover
the peace treaty or the league of na
tions, because at that time they were
not in existence. It did not cover spe
cifically the case of the "reds," because
at that time there had been no such
talk from that source about revolu
tion as has since been indulged in by
soap-box spouters and others of that
kidney. It did not cover the Army
question as now presented, because at
that time the war was still in progress.
The reference to the railroads was that
they should be returned to their own
ers, but no details were suggested.
The mandate was something like
this, if it is permissible, to imagine
the people addressing the republican
party in general terms: "The war ap
pears to be drawing to a close, and at
the close a big job will present itself.
Reconstruction will be a, very large
?rder, and we want you to fill it. It
will have to do largely with business
questions, and your reputation rests
upon business achievements. You are
proud of the designation of being the

i best. Give us workable measures that
i will enable us to keep abreast of the
j new times, which are iikelv to test
'the capacity of all the nations. And
jdo not forget that we belong in the
front rank of the, nations."
The party in control on Capitol Hill,
therefore, is not in the toils of cut
and-dried instructions. If enjoys a
good deal of leeway. It is to exercise
its best, judgment on questions as they
arise, giving to each the examina
tion, the reflection and the discussion
its importance demands.
The appeal is to the party in its
I best estate for its best work. It has
I a reputation to sustain. When united
it has never failed to score, and in
scoring has served the country well
in some grave emergencies, financial
and political. Such an emergency ex
ists today?the greatest since the civil
war. The party is united today, and
well led in both House and Senate. It
could not hope lor a better oppor
tunity or a higher inspiration to show
class and quality in legislative labors
j If it scores this time, it will score I
heavily. The bulk of the work done
in Congress at this session will be a
feature in the presidential campaign.
The country is at attention, and Con
gress should attend to the country.
President Wilson's Health.
President Wilson's reception of a
delegation of two senators yesterday
in conference on the Mexican situa
tion has set at rest reports regarding
his condition that for some time have
been in circulation, greatly to the un
easiness of the public. It would ap
pear from the accounts that have been
given of the interview at the White
House that the President is perfectly
capable of conducting the affairs of
his office; that he is fully cognizant
of national and international condi
tions and that he is progressing toward
recovery. If he were as ill a man as
the sinister rumors have suggested he
would assuredly not have been able to
spend forty minutes in animated and

sicalities respecting the currency of
suspicion concerning his convales

be a cessation of rumor-spreading and
whispering and gossiping about him.

from the White House on the author
ity of those in medical attendance

ment that the President's illness at

the fact that his restoration to health

upon himself have naturally been rele
gated to others.
The presidential office is being dis

Mr. Wilson has suffered. The federal
administration is functioning, and
there is no ground for pubtM uneasi

the government in any emergency
