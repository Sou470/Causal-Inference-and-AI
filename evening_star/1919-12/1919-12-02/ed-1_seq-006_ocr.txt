Opportunity and Responsibility.
A great opportunity'' lies before, anc
a correspondingly great responsibility
rests upon. President Wilson's second
industrial conference, which held its
opening session yesterday nfternoon.
The personnel of the conference is
of a caliber to inspire belief that it
realizes fully the responsibility and
will measure up to the opportunity.
This second conference is fortunate
in that its way is charted, at least to
the extent of the specific dangerous
rocks and reefs upon which its prede
cessor came to grief. It has also in
the President's letter of invitation to
the conferees a set of sailing direc
tions. something the former confer
ence lacked and which It sadly missed
Carrying farther the simile of naviga
tion, the crew of the second craft hap
been more happily chosen, and the
"articles" they have signed are more
calculated to inspire good will and
teamwork. And this second ship, in
stead of three, is to fly a single flag
the flag of the whole American people.
It is to be hoped that the navigators
will steer tho conference on a course
safely away from the borders of that
Sargasso sea of wrecked Utopian
dreams, where the derelicts go end
lessly round and round, and will keep
it headed straight toward such prac
tical things as have a chance of being
realized in time to help the world get
well of some of its present ailments.
What the situation calls for is a
broad declaration of principles, fair
to those who work for hire, fair to
those who hire them, and fair to the
great consuming public, which must
pay the hire of the one and the profits
of the other. And when these prin
ciples have been conceived and de
clared, there should be devised the
machinery, largely-planned and im
pressive in its' character to make them
effective.
Those whom the President has called
to sit in the conference are practical
men. skilled in large affairs. Some
of them are conspicuous in world',
renowij for practical altruism?ingre
dients in which there is hope a cure
for social and industrial ills may be
found. Practicability alone will not
suffice; altruism alone is a futility.
In the two there is promise. May the
industrial conference find the proper
combination.

The Attorney General's Appeal.
Attorney General Palmer appeals to
the people. He adjures them not to
be stampeded, but to support thd au
thorities in the matter of the coal
strike.
The appeal is entitled to considera
tion on two scores. (1) It comes from
an official who understands the ques
tion from investigation. Ho has lis
tened to both sides. Kepresentatives
of the miners and representatives of
the operators have conferred with
him. Moreover, as a public official,
charged with heavy responsibilities, ho
has kept the public's interests in view.
He has thus qualified for the role of
adviser, and, as we may all believe,
speaks now, not only from informa
tion, but from a sobor appreciation of
what the country faces.
(2) He has carried himself tljus far
in the business with firmness: and
courage. Ho has not taken sides as
between the miners and the operators.
His only aim has been to serve the
public, whose representative he is. He
wants coal nfined in quantities to in
sure the comfort of the people and
the continued operation of all their
industries that depend upon fuel. He
wants the miners to receive good
wages for their labor, and the opera
tors good dividends on their invest
ments.
The miners luiV'.' been rf?c*?ivlii*?
good wages?riot as well adjusted to
the changing times as should have
been, but an increase is offered in
sum sufficient to meet present needs.
The operators have been receiving
good dividends?better, it is asserted,
than always deserved.
The high cost of living enters into
the equation, as it does into every
equatirtti of the day. It is a very real
thing. It affects all classes and con
ditions. The aim, the keenest desire,
of all men in public place who may
assist is to lower the figures?bring the
necessaries of life in particular in
sustaining quantities within reach of
the average purse.
But, important as that question is.
there is one still more important.
While endeavoring to lower the cost
of living, we must endeavor to
heighten appreciation of our gov
ernment and what it stands
for. It is a "government of the pen
pie, by the people, for the people."
The majority rules, but the rule must
be for the benefit of nil. The minor
ity is not to be oppressed or neglect
ed. It lias its rights, and the right
to be heard about its wrongs.
But everything in reason, and by
prescribed and accepted means. No
minority, large or small, may lawful
ly employ other means to enforce its
wishes or advance its fortunes; and
whenever it adopts the stand-and-de
liver process of action it steps out
side the pale, and forfeits the sym
pathy and respect of all good citizens.
