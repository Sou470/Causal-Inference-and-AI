ROME. Italy, December 3.?The lat
est number of the Bulletin of Emigra
tion, published by the Italian govern
ment, contains a thorough study of
labor and migratory conditions in the
United States, made by the royal in
spector of emigration in Washington,
Signor Pancrazi. He states that Amer
ican farmers do not pay their hands
sufficiently well and maintain land
values at a high figure, so that
therefore few foreigners in America
go on the land. Instead, help is badly
needed in the building and steel
trades and a scarcity of afl common
or unskilled laborers will continue.
The writer traces the formation of
two currents in ^America?one to re
strict immigration altogether, and the
other to Americanize the workers and
persuade them to remain. He declares
that tlfe Italian view that workers are
entitled to spend their earnings ac
cording to their desires is contrary
to the American opinion that money
earned in America should be spent in
America. In summing up the reasons
for the return of Italians to Italy,
Signor Pancrazi. who should be well
informed, adduces seven, as follows:
1. Italian emigration to America is
temporary and was prolonged by the
war.
2. The emigrants have sa'ved money
in America which, as always, they in
tend to spend at home in Italy.
3. The Italian government has pre
vented their families in Italy from
joining them.
4. Propaganda of steamship agents
anxious to make up for losses during
the war time.
5. Uncertainty of work with possible
loss of jobs.
6. Local pressure to persuade them
to become American citizens.
7. Hope that at home they will find
i work under conditions as good as
elsewhere.
Reasons 1. 5 and 6 should be studied
by those Americans who will make
the immigration laws of the future.
In conclusion, Signor Pancrazi hopes
that the American labor market will
remain open to Italian workmen.
