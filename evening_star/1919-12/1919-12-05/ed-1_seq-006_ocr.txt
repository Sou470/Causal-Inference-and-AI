Coal Conservation.
Coal conservation is urged upon all
consumers as a measure of precaution
against actual famine in fuel. Gen
eral response to this request is to be
expected. The necessity comes at a
most difficult time. Not only is there
need of coal for heating and lighting
incidental to the winter season, but
the days are now nearest the shortest
range and the need of light is great
est. Furthermore, business is at its
briskest point on the eve of the holi
days. The large retail establishments
must, in order to meet the public de
mand, maintain full service.
Measures to speed up the production
of coal are developing in the west with
some success. Coal supplies are not
being depleted as rapidly as before,
although the production is still far
below normal demand, and, in truth, j
below the point of safety in terms of
sharp economy in use. It. will re
quire the services of at least 200,000 j
more miners than are now at work to j
meet the most rigidly conservative de- j
mands of the country.
Already serious j*3ult3 from coal
shortage are being reported. Indus
tries are being suspended for lack of
fuel and many thousands of men have
been thrown out of work. Great suf
fering is certain to ensue from this
enforced idleness, particularly at this
time of year. A continuation of the
process of shutting down is certain
to bring the country to a state of ca
lamity in a short time.
Coal conservation is in no sense a
cure of the evil. It is merely a pal
liative of the ill-effects of that evil.
Yet it is compassed within narrow
limits, and even when all luxuries,
amusements and not actually neces- j
sary services are curtailed there still
remains a need for coal that must be
supplied else the country is plunged
into disaster worse even than war.
