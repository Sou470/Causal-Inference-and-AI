A. L. Sinclair Urges National
Representation for D. C.
Before Anthony League.
"Since the four hundred thousand
and more Americans of the District of
Columbia pay national taxes, obey
national laws and go to war in the
nation's defense, they are entitled, on
American principles, to representa
tion in the national Rovrrnmeiit,
which taxes them, which makes their
laws and which sends them to war.
said A. Leftwich Sinclair, Washing
ton attorney, in an address last night
on "National Representation for the!
Citizens of the District," before the I
Anthony League, at 2007 Columbia
road.
Quotation Winn Applause.
Mr. Sinclair quoted opinions of
many of the great statesmen of the
past on the subject, and elicited ap
plause 'when recalling those stirring
words of Representative John Ran
dolph, jr., of Virginia, when, in Feb
ruary, 1S03, he said:
"I could wish, indeed, to see the
people within this District restored
to their rights. This species of gov
ernment is an experiment how far
freemen can be reconciled to live
without rights?an experiment dan
gerous to the liberties of these states:
but inasmuch as it had been already
made, inasmuch as I was not acces
sory to it, and as at some futur* time
its deleterious effects mayxbe arrest
ed. 1 am disposed to vote against the
resolution."
Mr. Sinclnir'a Argument.
The reason why the District was not
given representation in Congress at
the beginning was obvious, said Mr.
Sinclair; "it's population was .then too
small.
"But now the population of the Dis
trict exceed^ that of six of the states
?Nevada, Wyoming, Delaware, Ari
zona, Idaho and New Mexico. Each of
those states is represented in Con
gress by two senators and one or two
representatives, while the District is
unrepresented.
"The denial of this representation is
inconsistent with the principles and
provisions of our free institutions."
Mr. Sinclair then quoted President
Woodrow Wilson, who said:
"We are glad to fight for the ulti
mate peace of the world and for the
liberation of its peoples, the German
people included. The right is more
precious than peace, and we shall
light for the things which we have al
ways carried nearest our hearts?for
j democracy, for the right of those who
submit to authority to have a voice in
I their goverjjments."
HI* Plea for the District.
And in conclusion, he said:
"Let me remind you that among
those 'who submit to authority' are
the 400,000 Americans of the District
of Columbia.
"The recent1 war had for its object
the substitution of democracy for
autocracy throughout the world.
"Let us now address ourselves to the
substitution of demo?racy for autoc
racy in the District of Columbia."

Two special schools, the Hamilton
ami tho Harrison, equipped for chil
dren excluded from the regular
grades on account of threatened tu
berculosis, will be among the first
local institutions to benefit by ap
propriations from the fund obtained
from the sale of Christmas seals now,
for the first time since 1917, in prog
ress here.
This announcement comes from of
ficers of the Association for the Pre
vention of Tuberculosis, who state
they are informed that owing to lim
ited appropriations for the complete
equipment of these school buildings
and for the maintenance of the spe
cial food supplies which ate a neces
sary adjunct to the work carried on
therein, the pupils are in lack of cer
tain things which both the associa
tion and the school authorities agree
they ought to have. As soon as such
children are restored to their nor
mal physical condition they are al
lowed to return to their regular
grades. Inasmuch as the tuberculosis
association was one of the most ear
nest advocates of the establishment
of these schools it thereby feels a
responsibility for their adequate equip
ment.
Krrmi-Air Classes Wanted.
In this connection the association,
which is to administer the funds
raised by the sale of seals and of
"health bonds." announces# that it, is
exceedingly desirous of the establish
ment of more fresh-air classes for
normal youngsters in other schools.
At present only two have them, the
Blake and Stevens schools, and as
there is no appropriation available
for luncheons for the class members,
the association has also assumed that
work, spending last year more than
$300 for the purpose.
Demand for both seals and bonds
continues active at the twenty booths
maintained at various points by cam
paign managers, and already many
pieces of Washington mail are dec
orated on the reverse side with the
little stickers. Seals to the number of
56,000 have in the past twenty-four
hours been distributed through gov
ernmental departments and organiza
tions making special requests for
consignments of varying sizes. Al
though the demand for health bonds
of lower denominations has been ac
tive, no one has yet broken the ice
with a $100 bond.
Indorsed by Gen. Pershing.
Gen. Pershing, in a letter to the Na
tional Tuberculosis Association, in
dorsing the Red Cross Christmas seal
sale, says: "It gives me great pleas
ure heartily to indorse the Red Cross
Christmas seal sale for 1919. It is de
serving of loyal support and every
