THE year
1920 looms
with more
than that promise of change
which human nature so con
fidently ascribes to periods at
their dawn. Never was the outlook for pros
perity so bright as in the dawn of this New
Year. Industries are humming?a great scarc
ity must be filled. People are busily turning
to the problems that a new world situation has created and are
learning the great lessons of co-operation and service. It is im
possible to review recent events, without feeling that there is an
assurance of heightened power in America.
The constructive problems of peace are more fascinating than the problems
of war. They are problems of living, not of death. Our present civilization
depends on their correct solutions, for it can no longer endure the clash of
divided interests.
We are optimistic about the future. We believe in the American people. We
believe that there is common sense and courage enough in this country to bring
about the necessary changes and re-establish our social relations in accord with
our democratic aspirations. A great year is before us. And young 1920 will
have the greatest chorus of special good wishes ever lavished on an heir to
opportunity. _
