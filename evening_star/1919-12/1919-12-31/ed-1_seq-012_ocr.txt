Reserve Board Believes
Crisis Past, But Urges
People to Economize.
BY DAVID LAWRENCE.
Generally speaking. the public?
which is largely responsible?has not
been acutely conscious of the deli
cate financial situation tn which the
nation found itself in the holiday sea
son. While the Federal Reserve
Board believes the crisis has passed,
it is, nevertheless, determined to
check speculative activity and use
its powers and influence to cause the
American people to economise.
So many persons have thought that
because the federal reserve system
was constructed to bear the strains
that had previously been placed upon
our national banking system, any
burden whatsoever could be imposed
r.nd the structure would not sag. But I
the board has been compelled for the
nation's good to restrict the amount
of money which member banks could
borrow of the federal reserve banks
and otherwise use its powers to check
the extensive issue of credit paper
for speculative purposes.
Before the federal reserve act was
passed, a national bank could not
borrow more money than its capital
stock. This inflexibility was in one I
sense a good thing, but in many
other cases an obstacle to business
expansion. The federal reserve sys
tem has permitted banks to borrow
as long' as the funds were available
in the reserve banks. In some cases
member banks have borrowed ten and
fifteen times their capital stock. Now
the federal reserve board has called
a halt. It is determined to prove
that the federal reserve system not
only was designed to expand upon
emergency, but to act as a check
and restraining force, too.
Canae of I nr??inr??.
There is no denying the fact that the
approach of Christmas, with its tre
mendous buying In the retail trade,
caused some uneasiness. It will be
remembered that the armistice came
so suddenly that the public generally
was not prepared either as purchasers
or sellers to take care of the Christ
mas trade a year ago. All the pent-up
dsires of the American people, however,
resulting from the war period of invol
untary economy were let loose this year.
It was the first great after-the-war
test. And the federal reserve system 1
stood the strain. The western and
southern banks are In excellent condi
tion with piles of reserves. Dallas, for
instance, has 75 per cent reserve. As 1
rapidly, however, as the retailers of the
west and south, who sold tremendous
stocks of goods during the holidays, be
gin to pay their eastern balances, the
flow of money- eastward will begin.
Money is fully expected by the Federal
Reserve Board to be easier in the month
of January. But that doesn't mean
that the discount rate will be lowered
or that advances to member banks will
be resumed in unlimited quantities. It
does mean that the opportunity will be
at hand to take up the slack?the out-
standing federal reserve notes and oth- 1
er credits which have been a drag on
the situation.
Much good is expected by the Federal
Reserve Board to come from the opera- 1
tion of the Edge law. which would per
mit export organizations to be formed
to finance exports. The public gener
ally may not realize that the United
States has a trade balance in It*. i\?vor
of nearly three billion dollars, and yet
is shipping out gold in unprecedented
bulk.
Drawing on American Banks.
This means simply that American ex
porters have been selling to Europe on
credit and that America is paying for
her own imports in gold in the hope of
stabilizing the exchange market. But
while that big trade balance continues,
American concerns are drawing heavily
on our own banks for money to pay
overhead and other expenses. These
loans are a serious drain.
Cannot Contlnne Long.
How long can they continue? Not
much longer. And Europe must get
on her feet again In order to begin
paying her bills to America. It would
be a serious thing for America if the
bottom dropped out of European af
fairs and it became apparent that
Americans couldn't hope to get their
money for outstanding bills, and that
production would have to be curtailed
because of a stringency of credit. The
Edge law Is designed to save the
country to a certain extent from that
particular situation. Senator Edge
may not have intended to build up a
public sentiment for the peace treaty
by his proposal to distribute foreign
securities to the American people, but
a very practical argument for protec
tion of American investments in Eu
rope is bound to arise as soon as the
public begins buying in large quanti
ties the investments offered by the
new export finance organization.
If Instead of a few large business
and banking concerns the people gen
erally begin to acquire credit paper
and securities that depend for their
value on the maintenance of a politi
cal equilibrium their interest in Eu
rope is exi ected to increase, and pres
sure upon Congress for an early defini
tion of America's participation in
world afTairs will come to the surface.
America Standing Strain.
There seems to be no question of
the judgment of federal reserve offi
cials that America is weathering her
financial storm very well, but that she
would be immensely relieved if the
treaty of peace were in operation and
the moral protection of orderly gov
ernments in partnership with each
other could be thrown around the ex
port and import market.
The nation has gotten through the I
Christmas season with its extraordi
nary demands on the pocketbook in
excellent fashion, and the prophesy on i
every side here Is one of optimism
coupled with the expectation that the
treaty of peace when ratified will be
a stimulating factor to credits and
world business.

Great activity in retail trade, strong
demand for goods and consequently
large volume of buying orders at
manufacturing plants, high and rising
prices throughout the country, and
full employment of labor at unprece
dented wages, except in those cases
where employment has been rendered
unsteady and insecure as the result
of strikes or labor disturbances, are
the principal factors reported by fed-
eral reserve agents, In a bulletin of
the review of general business and
financial business throughout the sev-j
eral federal reserve districts during
the month of December issued today.
Anxiety Over Price Advance.
General anxiety concerning the con
tinued advance in prices is exhibited,
while unfavorable foreign exchange
rates are regarded as likely to bring
about a reduction in exports which may
necessitate some readjustment of domes
tic industry. Labor disturbances dur
ing the month of December, according
to the bulletin, have on the whole been
less severe than in November, both steel
and coal strikes reaching a practical
conclusion by resumption of work.
In practically all districts primary
emphasis is placed on the general dis
tribution of purchasing power and the
freedom with which recipients of wages
and salaries are expending their means
in the purchase of goods.
Detailed reports from the several dis
tricts are included in the report.
Prices Bit Baying; Power.
From sundry of the districts it is
noted that the growth of business is
far more noticeable in terms of dollars
than in units of production. There
are some in which very high prices
are already beginning to produce a
curtailment of buying power. This ap
pears to be more largely true in the
country and outlying districts than in
the cities.
The banking situation is spoken of
as reflecting the high prices of goods
in the form of larger demand for ac
commodation.
Increase in the cost of living is re
ferred to as a menace, and in some dis
tricts it is reported that the retailers
themselves recognize this fact.
The "work and save" program is re
ported by some to be hackneyed or
obsolete.
Skilled Labor Slowing* Up.
According to one report, "much of
our skilled labor works only sufficient
days during the month to keep going.
The effect of excessive wages has been
that of destroying regular standards
of living and the recognized basis of
prices."
The housing situation is reaching a
critical stage, which may result in
largely stimulating building opera
tions. In the middle west, particularly
in industrial centers, the shortage is
said to be acute. Rents have advanced
very greatly in view of the standstill
of building operations and the in
crease of city populations, which have
called for much more extensive ac
commodation.
