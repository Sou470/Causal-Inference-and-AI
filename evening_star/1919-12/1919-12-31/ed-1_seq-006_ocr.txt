Five Points of School Progress.
Enthusiastic co-operation by citi
zens' organizations is assured for tho
...constructive program of public school
improvements that has been recently
outlined, embracing five definite re
quiremonts. It is now evident that
?when Congress meets the voice of
Washington.will be heard at the Capi
tol unanimously in favor of these
measures, which involve a minimum
of disturbance and change in the
school organisation and .promise a
maximum of good results for the bet
terment of the system. These five
points of school betterment are more
teachers, more pay for teachers, a re
tirement system, more buildings and
S business management.
The success of this citizens' en
deavor will largely depend upon the
wisdom of the methods employed to
bring the needs of the schools before
Congress. The District in its political
impotence can not safely or success
tfully demand. It must ask. and the
hope now is that it will ask earnestly
and convincingly and unanimously
that Congress, recognizing the justice
and necessity of the changes pro
posed, will assent without hesitation
"or delay.
Furthermore this program of the
five essentials should be kept clear
of all other questions, involving mat
ters upon which there are differences
of opinion among the itcizens who are
now warmly advocating these particu
lar items of school aid. Extraneous
issues, directly or indirectly affecting
school welfare, are not to be pressed
without harmful reaction upon the
now united endeavor of Washington
for more and better schools.
It has been recognized generally
that if these five points are gained
through legislation and appropriation
the schools will be put in a better
position than they have occupied for
years, and without any fundamental
ehange affecting the personnel. Just
?At present the schools need relief
from proposals and projects for
amendment. It is impossible to con
duet surveys or to plan reorganiza
tion without distressing the teachers
'and distracting them from their work
of te&ching the children. The pupils
Inevitably feel the bad effects of such
projects of amendment and d'Sturb
ance. The classes become secondary
in the teachers' interest.
With a more adequate force, with
better pay and with retirement the
teachers, relieved of the excessive
bnrdens they are now carrying and
of the harrowing anxieties that con
tinually beset them, can do better
work than they have been able to do
for years. With more buildings the
sehools can be more adequately ac
commodated, with smaller classes,
with fuller hours and in consequence
with more efficient teaching. With a
business management provided to re
lieve the superintendent and his as
sistants of the physical and financial
cares that now burden them the
School administration will be more
efficient and satisfactory and econom
ies!
If these five purposes can be ac
complished greater strides will have
been made toward a model school sys
tem than through any other changes
that can be conceived or conceivably
executed at present. No voice is
laised against these five proposals.
Upon them there is unanimous agree
ment as to their necessity and as to
their effectiveness for immediate
school betterment. To get them all
will be a big task for the friends of
the schools, quite a sufficient win
ter's campaign without distractions
and difficult and mooted endeavors
along other lines.

No Influenza in 1919.
In looking back over the year just
closing there is one fact that stands
out most conspicuously as a reason
for great gratitude. The country has
been spared a return of the epidemic
that a year ago was just ending after
s terrible scourging of all sections.
When the influenza outbreak waned
last winter there were many who felt
that it was sure to return this season.
With the exception of a very few
cases, however, there have been no
signs of renewal. These few cases
have been so promptly treatel that
the disease has not spread from them,
an4 for many weeks no sign of the
disease has appeared.
There is no adequate explanation
of this peculiar wave of disease sweep
ing westward from the battle-torn
fields of Europe across the Atlantic
and throughout America. It was
doubtless a war disease, generated by
the conflict, but how and just why it
came in 1918 rather than one of the
earlier years of the war is one of the
mysteries yet unsolved. To us it
seemed to spread westward, though,
in fact, it went in all directions. It
was without exception the most ter
ribly fatal epidemic-disease that ever
visited America, and the country
prays that never again will it have
such an experience as that which
made 191S one of the most agonizing
times in its history.
The Seas and Sea Business.
This is from a London cable:
"Manufacturers in many lines have,
business booked far ahead. The cot
ton trade, one of the basic British
industries, is said to have all the or
ders it can fill for the next five years.
Shipbuilders snv they are in the same
happy condition. They have yet to
be convinced that America will be
able to take away the former British
supremacy in the carrying trade. The}
base their confidence on the belief
that while the British are a seafaring
race the Americans are not. Appar
ently they believe tha* American
ships built in the war are destined t(
be bought eventually by distinctly
maritime nations. Their confidence
however, is not ironclad. Lord Inch
cape toici the shareholders of one
Great Britain steamship company that
if the American government turned
over its ships to private owners. Great
Britain need not be afraid, but if the
American people were to be taxed
to build up their mercantile marine
tbe outlook for shipping industry in
the islands was anything but rosy."
Congress will soon take up the mer
chant marine question, and when it
dues we may expect a very interest
ing discussion. Without a merchant
marine, an overseas trade of any size
will be impossible; and some of our
calculations for the future are based
upon a very large overseas trade.
The accepted belief is that the trade
awaits us; but i' is a patent fact that
in order to '"go to it" we must have
a merchant marine of our own in suc
cessful operation.
Lord Inchcape thinks an unassisted
private ownership would fail. His
opinion is probably based upon the
fact that the highly successful British
merchant marine is assisted by the
British government. With that ad
vantage, Great Britain, he seems to
think, would continue to top us as a
sea carrying power.
If the Americans of the present
generation are not to be classed as
a seafaring race, it is because a pre
vious generation abandoned sea traf
fic for land improvement. That gen
eration built the great railroad lines,
and developed into states the rich
empire west of the Mississippi river.
But with our long Atlantic coast
line and our long Pacific coast line
to hearten us for the work, it is an
easy guess that, with other encour
agement, we should soon regain our
prowess on the water, and become one
of the leading water-carriers of the
world.
The problem is up to Congress. A
policy must be worked out, and should,
be put into operation as soon as pos
sible. The British shipbuilders ?nd
owners are, as this cable states, in
high spirits. Their merchant fleets
are in being, and profitably employed.
They have business booked for years
ahead. They are formidable competi
tors, with much experience and a
long period of prosperity to encour
age their furthef efforts.
It will be a goodly sight when the
Stars and Stripes again appears in tha
harbors of the world flying from the
staffs of merchant ships, and signal
izing the return to the sea of Amer
ica as a trading nation. At an earlier
day, and for a long time, we cut a
creditable figure there, and can do it
now under a policy devised to give
us an even chance for business.
