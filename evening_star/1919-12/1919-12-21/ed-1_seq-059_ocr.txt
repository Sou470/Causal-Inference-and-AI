The Electric Hood Heater.
If it is out of the question to provide a heated garage fur a /ar
during the \Vinter. starting difficulties, lubrication troubles and some other
r evils can be lessened considerably* by applying Jocal heat to the engine
only, either to keep it warm constantly or to somewhat raise its temperature
just prior to the starting operation. Jf only the engine itself be kept from
j becoming too cold, it is not of great#importance* if the rest of the car and
i the garage atmosphere is not warmed, and for this reason "under-hood"
j heating is of great utility. An electric heater is the most convenient way
of doing this and such heaters, of very neat design, are upon the market
j at reasonable prices. The under-hood heater consists of coils of resistance
wire inclosed in a perforated metal casing and adapted to take current from
an ordinary lamp socket through a flexible cord, the heater being placed
inside the hood space, the hood closed and a hood cover or blankets placed
in position to cover the radiator front and to retain the heat developed as'
completely as possible. The electrical consumption of these heaters is j
usually either 100 watts or 200 watts, the former size being recommended
for use in weather-tight garages and the latter in loosely constructed ones.
The smaller heater costs 1 cent and the larger 2 cents per hour, to operate
with current at 10 cents per kilowatt hour and proportionally more or less
if the rate is higher or lower. If the heater is put in operation while the
jacket water is still hot. and the hood is very carefully protected against J
heat losses, engine temperature is quite well maintained, even in severe j
weather, it is usual to place the heater close to the carburetor and intake,
where warmth is of most importance and. while in very cold weather it may j
be advisable to keep current on all night, under milder conditions, starting;
the heater a few hours before the car is wanted may take off the chill i
sufficiently. It is perfectly feasible to use in connection with it an auto
niatic time switch that can be set like an alarm clock and which will start I
the heater into action at any desired hour. Of course, a 100-watt incandescent j
lamp, tor instance, one of the thirty-two C. I*, carbon filament type, will
give- out just as much heat as a regular under-hood heater of 100 watts and
may be used successfully, but most motorists seem to prefer the special

K. D. writes: I own a sedan and
wish to know. the right way to re

Answer: The main thing is to
avoid scratching it. especially during
the washing' operation. Soap should
not be required in cleaning it. You
should remember that dust and dirt,
which collect upon it. will leave
scratches unless they are removed
very carefully, and floating them off
with a gentle current of water is
the safest way. The finish should
never be rubbed if there is any dirt
upon it. If oil or grease spots ap
pear on the finish, they can be re
moved by "dabbing" them with a
piece of waste moistened with kero
sene. A little good furniture polish
can occasionally be applied to the
body work with cheese cloth and a
polish produced by gently rubbing
the surface, but only when the var
nish is perfectly free from dirt. Var
nish cracks sometimes result from
keeping the garage too hot in winter
and driving the car out into very
cold outside air. Do not let the car
stand unnecessarily in the hot sun
or allow water to freeze upon it.
