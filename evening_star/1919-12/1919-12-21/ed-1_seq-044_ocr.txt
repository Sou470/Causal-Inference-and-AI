The Government Hotels.
Tn a report to the Seriate the public
buildings committee recommends that
the charge for rooms and board for
government hotels on the Union Sta
tion plaza should be increased by $3?0
.a mouth in order to cover the items
of depreciation and interest on the
government's investment. Apart from
.the question of whether the rent
should be raised, the suggestion that a
charge should be made for deprecia
tion is certainly to be resisted if, as
definitely announced at the outset,
these structures are only temporary,
designed to meet an emergency. De
preciation is a perfectly proper charge
in ease of a permanent investment, as
in a hotel, apartment house, an office
building or a dwelling. But to eharge
depreciation for the use of structures
that are built only to be torn down
within a short time is to put the gov
ernment in the profiteering class, and
also to menace the capital with the
perpetuation of these structures.
It cannot be contemplated that the
government continue in the boarding
house business indefinitely. It went
into that enterprise in a pinch to pro
vide quarters for the surplus official
population of Washington. It did it
so tardily that the buildings were not
available until after the congestion
had lessened. They have served a use
ful purpose and perhaps will be needed
for some months to come. But even
tually and soon the government must
quit the room-renting business and
permit the adjustment to take place
that is already in progress, and that
will be facilitated if the new rentals
law is put into effect and administered
-and works out as its proponents ex
pect.
To charge for depreciation could
only mean that the government would
seek either to maintain the buildings
at 100 per cent of repair?which is the
basis of permanency?or recoup itself
for the original construction. Such a
charge would mean to make the occu
pants of the buildings pay for them.
That would take a long time. Is it
proposed to keep them going long
enough to bring up the depreciation
charges to the initial cost? Consider
ing the conditions in which they were
constructed, at the peak of the prices
for labor and materials, it will be a
long time before these monthly items
-work out at full refund.
Far better will it be for the gov
ernment to regard these structures
strictly as temporary and charge for
their use and for the services render
ed there only enough to pay the costs
of operation and service. Instead of
adding depreciation and interest items
to the monthly rents, a period should
be determined upon at the end of
which the buildings will be vacated
and scrapped, and the Capitol-Station
plaza restored to its status as a park.

The Sole Issue Before the Court
Two cases have been presented to
the United States Supreme Court to
test the validity of the eighteenth
amendment to the Constitution, pre
scribing national prohibition. One of
these is brought by the state of Rhode
Island and the other on behalf of an
association of New Jersey liquor deal
era. A third case is on the way to
the 8upreme Court, in the form of an
appeal from a refusal by tho District
of Columbia Supreme Court to grant
a writ of mandamus against the Sec
retary of State to compel the setting
aside of the prohibition proclamation.
All of these cases involve declara
tion* to the effect that the eighteenth
amendment is invalid because it is
contrary to the Constitution. It is
hard to see how the Supreme Court
can hold that any amendment, regu
larly proposed by Congress and rati
fied by the necessary number of states,
is unconstitutional. It is within the
rights and powers of the people,
through Congress and the states?or
by means of a constitutional conven
tion?to amend the federal organic
law in any respect, to any extent,
prior guarantees and limitations and
prescriptions of power contained in
it to the contrary notwithstanding.
If a sufficient number of the mem
bers of Congress so propose, and a
sufficient number of the states approve,
the very form of government in this
country can be changed. It can be
transformed into a monarchy. The
states can be deprived of their indi
vidual organizations. The trial by
jury yn be abolished. The only limi
Ution which is baaie ui perpetual,
until changed itself by amendment
regularly proposed and ratified, is
that which prescribes the method of
amendment.
The real issue before the Supreme
Court in respect to the prohibition
amendment is whether the necessary
number of states hare actually and
sufficiently ratified it; whether, in fact,
the only constitutional provision
which applies or possibly can apply
in limitation has been fully met and
satisfied. That issue turns upon the
question of the referendum powers of
the voters in certain states. Docs the
word "legislatures" in the Constitu
tion mean solely the legislatures in
the matter of ratification, or does it
imply that if the people of a state
elect they may substitute another man
ner of ratification for the vote of the
legislature t That question, and that
alone, is the point upon which the
validity of the eighteenth amendment,
as at present proclaimed as formally
and finally adopted, depends.
Hearing the Half-Million Mark.
Washington's growth, as indicated
by the police census, has been 59,481
in two years. This gives a total of
455,428. Some disappointment will per
haps be felt at this announcement, as
a greater increase had been expected.
It is, however, to be borne in mind
that this represents a virtually perma
nent increase in the population,
whereas a census taken a year ago,
when the city was full of war workers,
dollar-a-year men and their families
and servants, and numerous visitors
here on war business, would un
doubtedly have shown close upon if
not beyond half a million population.
The shrinkage since then, due to the
civil as well as military demobiliza
tion, brings Washington practically to
a fixed basis, so close to half a mil
lion that that point is certain to be
reached in a very short period.
The gain of 59,481 in two years is
fairly evenly distributed throughout
all parts of the District, except in the
first precinct, where the increase is, as
usual, the smallest of all. From 951
in that precinct the gain runs to 14,646
in the tenth, which is the moat popu
lous section of the city, itself contain
ing 95,017. It is notable that the third
largest gain is in the ninth precinct,
the northeast section and the county,
showing a gratifying movement of
population eastward which serves to
maintain a wholesome balance.
It is hardly to be expected that the
population will diminish. The natural
gain of the city will more than offset
the slight reductions in the govern
ment forces, which are continuing as
the executive departments are reduced
from a war to a peace basis. Some of
the departments are adding to their
personnel, causing the net losses to be
comparatively small. In a few months
this movement out of town will prob
ably cease altogether, while the eity
will naturally grow steadily.
The decennial census taken by the
United States next July may show
discrepancies in comparison with the
police census just completed. Locally
conducted enumerations seldom agree
exactly with the census counts, owing
to the difference in methods. The po
lice census, however, is believed to be
fairly accurate, as it is conducted by
men who know the territories they
cover and are well equipped for
enumeration.
