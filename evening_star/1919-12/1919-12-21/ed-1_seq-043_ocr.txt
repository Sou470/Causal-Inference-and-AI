THE STORY THE WEEK HAS TOLD
By NEWBOLD NOYES.
WHKX Jim or Jane, average Ameri
can man and woman, devote a
half hour of a Sunday morning
to that highly profitable occupa
tion of "giving the week the
once over" and continue the
procesH over a moderately extended period of
time, they will make this discovery: That a
high percentage of the ma;or news of any
given week is merely a new installment of a
story which started several numbers back and
that a brand-new feature in the events of the
week is a rarity. I'pon the ability of Jim and
Jane to read the news developments of each
week in the light of what ha? happened during
the week or the month or the year before, de
pends their ability to extract from that news
its full significance. This fact is pointed out
lest, recognizing; such old friends in the subjects
treated below as "The Mexican Situation" or
"The Peace Treaty" or "The Coal Strike." Jim
shall assert "shucks, same as last week. Why
don't he spill sumthin' new?" and Jane, opining
her lord speaks the truth, shall with him pass
on to the comic section.
Take this as an explanation or an apology
cr what you will, my friends, the proof of
what has been said lies before you.

Take the peace treaty. We have seen, since
the inception of these articles, the Sixty-sixth
Congress called to order in its first regular
meeting with the treaty, undisposed of in the
hectic extra session which endured from May
to November, being battered and bruised upon
the Senate table, wherever that is. We have
noted the presidential failure to mention it in
his message incident to the opening of Con
gress. and the loathness of Senators to lay hands
upon the job before them. We have stressed
the unqualified desire of the public that the
treaty be disposed of. and that quickly. And
we have urged all concerned to limber up suf
ficiently to enable them to function properly
as servants of the hundred and ten million.
.Now what has the past seven days brought
forth?
Karly in the week it became apparent that
the gentlemen in the upper house of the
duplex establishment on Capitol Hill were
growing restless under the public insistence
that senatorial dexterity in passing responsi
bilities from one side of the chamber to the
other is not all they want to be shown. The
popular clamor for definite and constructive
action of some sort or another, seemed about
to jolt its way into the complacent minds of
those who seemed to feel that the treaty de
served a good long rest after the manhandling
it received in the extra session.
Into the midst of this chatty and restful
atmosphere came the latest Wilsonian bomb
shell. in the form of the White House an
nouncement that the President proposed to sit
pat and that he had no compromise or con
cessions of any kind in mind. The restful at
mosphere of the Senate chamber forthwith be
same surcharged with uncontrolled energy, and.
though its chattiness in no wise decreased, it
took on the form of talking to the point. For
the first time in several weeks the fact that
the Senate's business is the Senate's business
seemed to have percolated into the senatorial
consciousness.

Now what the President or his proxy said
to the Senate in the shortest message to that
body on record has been much misunderstood
by the public, if not by those to whom it was
immediately addressed. It has been asserted
broadcast that Wilson has announced he will
accept no compromise. He made no such an
nouncement. What he said to the republican
majority and the democratic minority of the
Senate?and his words were addressed primari
ly to the first-mentioned proportion of that
gathering?was, In effect, this:
"Do your own work. I've done mine. 1
made a treaty, presented it to you, and ex
plained it to you and to the country. It is up
to you to approve or disapprove of my work.
You are the ones to make any reservations that
are to be made, if any are to be made. You are
the ones to compromise among yourselves any
difficulties which are preventing you from com
pleting your share of the job that a world
which has suffered too much is waiting for.
This is your opportunity and your turn to act.
When you shall have acted it will be my turn
again?my turn to decide whether your action
constitutes approval of the treaty as drawn or
disapproval, my turn to act upon that decision.
Do your own work, gentlemen, and do it the
best you know how."
And the events of the balance of the week,
which hava shown the Senate to be co-ordinat
ing itself for one final effort, prove the wisdom,
if not the tactfulness, of the President's course
in bidding the senators play th?ir own part.
Assuredly they have sufficiently emphasized
their constitutional right to do so without
executive interference. _
