ACCUSED YOUTH AS LAWYER
MAKES SKILLFUL DEFENSE
Judge John T* McMahon. presiding in
the United States branch of Police
Court yesterday, had a surprise when
the ca^e of Pud Dent, a young colored
man, was called for hearing. The
youth was charged in five informations
with the larceny of a large number of
blankets, sheets, towels ami other sim
ilar things, the property of the Pull
man Car Company Judge McMahon
asked him if ho was represented by
an attorney. Dent told the court that
he would conduct his own case.
The boy was alive to every issue of
the cases against him. which were tried
as one case. 11" had been emnloyed at
one tim^ by the Pullman Car Com
pany. which accused him of theft, il^
raised objec tions to the introduction of
certain evidence, and in several in
stances was sustained by the court.
He cross-examined every witness with
an ability and shrewdness that would
have reflected credit on an attorney for
the defense.
When a representative of the Pull
man Company attempted to identify as
a general proposition all of the alleged
stolen property heaped high on the
desks of the court, giving the room the
appearance of the interior of a dry
goods store. Dent raised an objection,
claiming that the government could not
identify the property without specifical
ly identifying each article and satisfy
ing the court that there were marks on
the property that would indicate be
yond a reasonable doubt that the prop
erty in question belonged to the Pull
man Company.
The objection was sustained by the
court and the witness representing the
car company was forced to attemot to
identify the hundred or more articles,
signally failing in many instances.
Members of the bar expressed the
opinion that the boy was a born lawyer.
lie was convicted on four informa
tions, being sentenced to serve thirty,
ten. thirty and sixty days, respective
ly, or 130 days in jail. He won out in
the fifth case, which the court dis
missed.
Old practitioners at the Police Court
bar said that in their long experience
tln re they could not recall such a de
fense, pointing out that the adae*
which is credited to Patrick Henry that
"a man who acts as his own lawyer
has a fool for a client," did not hold
good in Dent's case.
