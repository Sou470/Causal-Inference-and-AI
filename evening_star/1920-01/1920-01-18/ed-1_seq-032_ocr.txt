Four Points of Street Safety.
In its consideration of the local
traffic problem a special committee of
the Board of Trade has adopted four
propositions as essentials in a pro
gram of street safety. These are, the
establishment of a traffic court, the
stoppage of "jay walking," the nam
ing of one-way streets in congested
sections and a thorough examination
all applicants for motor licenses.
All four of these are necessary if
Washington is to be redeemed from
its present high percentage of street
casualties.
A traffic court is required to give
prompt and thorough consideration of
oases growing out of the violation of
regulations. At present the enforce
ment of these rules is incomplete be
cause offenders cannot be adequately
penalized. The majority of them
leave collateral at the station houses
with the intention of forfeiting, re
garding the small sums deposited as
part of the cost of running their cars.
A traffic court would permit prompt
trial and the enforcement of court at
tendance. As soon as it is assured
that an arrest means confrontation
and the possible imposition of full
penalty there will be more carefulness
in the streets.
It is doubtful, however, whether
"jay walking" can be cured by regu
lation. If pedestrians are by rule con
fined between curbs to the crossing
?paces, outside of which thay are
wholly at their own risk, they should
be given some consideration at those
points and not compelled, as at pres
ent, to take their chances with traffic.
Much of the "jay walking" is due to
the fact that conditions at the cross
ings are normally qnite as dangerous
as those in midblock. With the ec
centric car stop system now in vogue
here, furthermore, midblock crossing
?is almost compulsory in many places.
A beginning has been made here
with one-way streets, but not to a
sufficient extent to relieve congestion.
This can be done by the Commission
ers without legislation. It is well
worth trying, and if the experiment
is not successful it can be abandoned.
More one-way streets would not only
promote traffic movement, but #bfii4
lessen the chances of accident.
A real test for all license applicants
is imperatively needed. The present
system is farcical. Practically any
body can get a license to drive a motor
ear. Cars are being driven by people
who are totally unfit to handle power
ful machines in crowded streets. Many
ears are being driven by nnlicensed
persons, including children. Not until
the license system is radically changed
will there be any assurance whatever
that the laws will be observed, what
ever the penalties, written or inflicted.

Influenza Reappears.
Influenza has reappeared in Wash
ington and numerous cases have been
reported by physicians to the health
office. The number of cases has been
lately increasing, indicating a spread
of the disease. The health officer
states that they are not of a virulent
type, and that the percentage of dan
ger is much less than during the epi
demic in the autumn of 1918.
Prompt attention to all pulmonary
ailments, whether of the ordinary
"eold"' variety or of a more serious
character, is the first essential step in
fighting this disease. The influenza
claimcJ many victims in 1918 because
symptoms were ignored. The disease
progressed swiftly in consequence.
Many victims were taken in this city J
for lack of care.
Washington is now far better pre-!
pared to fight the disease, if it be
comes epidemic, than it was fifteen
months ago. There is less congestion.
Better housing conditions prevail.
Then many persons fell ill and lay
without medi<al attendance for hours,
cntil they had passed beyond the
reach of help. Now it is well assured
that no such tragedies can be enacted
here, as in the light of that dreadful
experience immediate aid will surely
be rendered to all who suffer.
The influenza is an infectious dis
ease, easily spread through close
association. It passes in the form of
minute spray from the coughs or
ineezes of those who, in the first
itages, go abroad and mingle with
r
other persona. Avoidance of dense
crowds is essential to complete im
munity from contracting the disease
in this manner. Nothing, however,
can prevent infection through casual
contact.
As a basic precaution it is neces
sary to avoid exposure to "cold" mak
ing conditions. The general system
must be kept in order. Extreme phys
ical fatigue should be avoided, if pos
sible. Diet should be carefully regu
lated, to prevent disorders of the di
gestive system which promote pulmo
nary derangements. And always, as
already suggested, it is necessary to
give heed to early symptoms that are
warnings of illness that may be avert
ed by efficacious treatment.
There is no occasion for alarm at
present. The city is not in danger
of a recurrence of the epidemic if
everybody will observe the ordinary
rules of health and give quick heed
to indications that the system has ab
sorbed some noxious bacillus that may
speedily be defeated with intelligent
treatment.
The Bent Commission.
With the confirmation by the Senate
of the President's nominees the Dis
trict rent commission is in position to
begin work at once. It faces a diffi
cult task. It must sit as a court to de
cide disputes between landlords and
tenants, to supervise leases, to deter
mine the equities of rates, to adjust
troubles over repairs and service. It
has an extensive jurisdiction and save
for appeals to the Court of Appeals its
decisions are binding. The personnel
of the commission is such as to com
mand the confidence of and to give as
surance of scrupulously fair dealing to
both sides. Unfortunately, however,
the rents question has become so com
plex and difficult during the past two
or three years of housing congestion
in Washington that much bitterness
Jus developed. Property owners have
felt imposed upon by hasty legislation,
while tenants have felt injured by oc
casional increases permitted by vaca
tions of premises. Generally speak
ing, during the past two years the
tenants have had more protection than
the landlords. Thus the rents com
mission becomes a tribunal for both
sides in circumstances to warrant the
belief that justice will be done, that
profiteering will be prevented and that
tenancy will be made equitable. In
the discharge of this task the rent
commissioners will have the sympathy
of the community.
