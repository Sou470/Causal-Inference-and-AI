SURRENDERED WARSHIPS
DISABLED BY AUSTRIANS
Machinery of Vessels at Cattaro
Destroyed or Removed?Only
Hulls Left in Good Shape.
GENEVA, January 16 (by the Asso
ciated Press).?Austrian war vessels,
which under the terms of the peace
treaty must be handed over to the
allies, have undergone a second "Scapa
Flow," but on a smaller scale, accord
ling to a telegram received here from
Turin by way of Lugano.
The dispatch states that the allied
naval commission which arrived re
cently at Cattaro confirms first re
ports that the Austrian fleet had been
seriously disabled.
The Austrians were said to have de
stroyed or removed the principal parts
of the machinery of the ships, render
ing them incapable of navigation, only
the hulls remaining in good condition.
Repairs, it is said, will require several
months before the vessels can be mada
ready for the sea.
The fleet includes one battleship,
three large cruisers, four torpedo
cruisers, twelve torpedo boats and
some smaller craft.
The Austrians are declared to have
acknowledged the damage, but to
have blamed the Czechoslovaks and
the Jugoslavs. The ships eventually
will be towed to Toulon and Marseille
for repairs.
CRISIS IN MINISTRY
OF SPAIN IMPENDING
MADRID. January 17 (by the As
sociated Tress).?All the newspapers
this morning discussed the possibility
of a cabinet crisis because of the
attitude of the Maurist party and of
the party of Count Romanones toward
the cabinet. Both these parties have
representatives in the government.
The premier and the ministers of the
interior and public works are known
to be dissatisfied owing to lack of
support of their parties. These min
isters, when interviewed, did not deny
the possibility of a crisis in the min
istry.
The senate last night approved a
bill calling for the compulsory syn- I
dication of industries, trades and pro
fessions. affecting both employes and
employers. The measure has been sent
to the chamber of deputies, where it
is expected to arouse much opposi
tion on the part of the socialists, who
say that the measure establishes too
?many obligations upon the workers
and placed the syndicates in the
hands of the government, which could
dissolve them at any time.
