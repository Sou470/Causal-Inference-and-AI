This will be the last week of the
Corcoran Gallery's cotemporary ex
hibition. It will continue through next
Sunday afternoon, and In order to per
mit those whose occupation prevent
attendance during the regular hours
of opening, the gallery will again be
open to the public on Friday evening,
free of charge.
This exhibition is not only compara
ble with the best exhibitions that are
being held in other cities, but is far in
advance of the majority that are now
to be seen in New York. It is infinite
ly better than the winter exhibition
of the National Academy of Design,
which has just closed, and it is far
superior to the exhibition of cotem
porary French art, which has been
sent to this country under the au
spices of the French government and
is now on view in the Metropolitan
Museum of Art. This would, however,
not be saying a great deal, for rarely
has there been a more pathetic and
sorry showing than these paintings
by French artists of today.
There are in the catalogue of the col
lection some well known names and
the works exhibited are by no jneans
the works by the ultra-modernists.
They are feeble efforts, poor in many
instances in drawing and in color,
lacking the characteristics of the
splendid French school to which art
lovers of all nations have long looked
for inspiration and example. Such a
display can only be explained by the
conditions brought .through the devas
tating influences of the war. France
has given her best to the cause of
civilization and those who survive
have been so spiritually drained that
even their gifts have been diminished
and their vision for the moment im
poverished.
The pictures composing this French
exhibition were assembled by repre
sentatives of the three leading art so
cieties of Paris under the patronage
of the government, and it is not pos
sible to believe that they do not rep
resent the best of the French output
of today.
But the war is a very short dis
tance behind us, and it was fought on
French soil. That our artUts have
been able to rebound in this space of
time is not unreasonable, but that the
French should have could harjjly be
expected. The real pity is that the
time of holding this exhibition shouM
not have been postponed until condi
tions in France were more nearly nor
mal.

The French exhibition in New York
was sent to this country, it will be re
membered. as a return courtesy for
the exhibition of American art sent to
France last summer and shown this
autumn in the Luxembourg. It is
only fair to remind ourselves that if
the French exhibition is not all that
could be desired in merit, neither was
our American exhibition up to the
high standard that we should like to
have had it maintain. In fact, so far
did our exhibition fall below what
was hoped for it that the director of
the Luxembourg volunteered to sup
plement. it by loans from the French
national collection in order that
American art might be adequately
represented.
Furthermore, to the delight of
many and the confusion of a few, he
declined to have placed on exhibition
In the galleries of the Luxembourg
ten works by ultra-modernists that
had been sent with the collection se
lected by the members of the jury be
longing to this group of painters and
forwarded under the misapprehension
that It was the desire of the French
government that all phases of Ameri
can art. and especially this be repre
sented. He was eminently correct
because these modernists do not rep
resent American art and the modern
istic movement, which came over to
us from France, is well nigh spent
When Nevinson, the leading British
exponent of cubism, was asked on the
occasion of his visit to this country
some months ago how cubism fared,
he promptly replied, "CubiBm is dead,"
averring that its more ardent expo
nents knew it could not live, but be
lieved it was a progressive step to
ward something better.
And this may be so. There is no
doubt but what, irrational and un
beautlful as were the works of the
modernists, they did shook many out
of ruts, awaken thought and lead to
better things. Painting is not merely
an art of imitation; the painter must
interpret as well aa represent, and
though the principles of art are un-
changing, its outward manifestations j
are aa variable as the weather and as
numerous as the stars. To say that
one man's style is right and another
man's style is wrong is the height of
folly. But for individuality of expres
sion art woul'i soon oease to interest.

In the Corcoran Gallery's present
exhibition there are two portraits of
widely different style which, possess
each peculiar merit and interest. One,
by Ebin F. Coming, is entitled "The
Two Sisters" and is owned and loaned
by Mrs. Marshall Field; the other is
by Giuseppe Trotta and is entitled "The
Story," representing a little girl of ten
or twelve years of age, seated, with a
book in her lap.
Mr. Comins Is- a New Englander;
Mr. Trotta an Amerioan by adoption.
Both paintings show extreme simplifi
cation in method, but whereas Mr.
Trotta follows the style of the old
Italian masters (one might almost
say ?f the old Italian primitives), Mr.
Comina employes a broad method
peculiar to the present day. which
would seem to set aside as of little
value the amenities of technique and
craftsmanship.
Mr. Trotta is a. technician of the ut
most skill. Every detail of hispicture
is perfectly represented, but he has
succeeded in subordinating detail to
the higher human quality of his work
and has produced a portrait of extraor
dinary individuality. The little grl
that he has pictured Is a living per
sonality, the observer would recog
nize her in a crowd and must feel in
terested in her character.
Mr. Comins has not attempted te
interpret textures, though he has pro
duced a pleasing surface. It is spirit
ual rather than human quality that he
has suoceeded in getting into His
work, and, what Is more, he has em
ployed an interesting color scheme
for his purpose.

Mr. Carruth gave a comprehensive
survey of the works of the painter
friar, who impressed his personality
on the artistic progress of the first
half of the fifteenth century, and he
also showed in the most engaging
manner the development of Fra An
gelico's art, beginning under the in
fluence of the Byzantine and estab
lishing the style of the latter-day
school, discovering, as it were, the use
of light and shadow in modeling and
the laws of aerial prospective.
Mr. Carruth's lecture, like the oth
er's of the same course, was beauti
fully illustrated by colored slides. These
slides have been made, at great expense
and cost in the matter of effort, es
pecially for these lectures under Mr.
Carruth's direction. An artist in Flor
ence colored the photographs with
the utmost accuracy and a Boston ex
pert has made the slides under the
criticism, not only of Mr. Carruth, but
of well known experts and connois
seurs of Florentine art. On one of
the slides shown last Wednesday Mr.
Carruth and his collaborators worked
for one whole year before obtaining
the desired similarity to the famous
painting it reproduces.
