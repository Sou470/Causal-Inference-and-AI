THE STORY THE WEEK HAS TOLD
By NEWBOLD NOYES.
THE burst of speed with which Fatus?or
whoever else is responsible for causing
the events which make the news from
day to day?started out the new year
could not be maintained. It is Just as
well. For last Sunday Jim and Jane, perusine
the story of the seven days which had Just
passed, might well have been staggered in con
templating the task of assimilating it all in an
hour by the Are. All things even up. And while
there has been plenty to fill the columns of the
daily press during the week we are looking
back over today, only a limited amount of it
has been of that major caliber which demands
the thoughtful consideration of the thinking
Amerloan man and woman.

The campaign of the Department of Justice
against the reds continued sporadically during
the week, several small contingents being round
ed up, principally in New York. The House
sedition bill, referred to in this column two
weeks ago. which is a far more stringent docu
ment than the measure as passed by the Sen
ate, is due for early consideration in the lower
chamber of the Capitol, and its proponents
prophesy that it will be passed within a fort
night. It is the claim of those in charge of the
bill that while it "will go the limit" to prose
cute those who advocate force and violence to
change American institutions, no limitation will
be placed upon peaceable parliamentary efforts
to effect changes in the nation's system of gov
ernment.
Two important national movements to in
crease Americanism and combat radicalism
were launched during the week, both having
their inception in New York. The Constitu
tional League and the National Board of Fire
Underwriters are the motivating .organizations,
and it is their purpose to meet the "assaults
of the reds" mainly by vitally needed con
structive education.

In the Congress of the United States the
socialist, Berger, under prison sentence for hav
ing given aid and comfort to the enemy during
the world war, has once again been denied the
seat to which he was elected by a Wisconsin
district. In New York state four socialists
have been denied admittance to the state legis
lature to which they were elected by their con
stituents. While the action of Congress has
received, broadly speaking, the support of the
nation, there has been a very general revulsion
of sentiment against the course of the New
York state legislature.
The cases have brought to mind the fact
that a socialist, pure and simple, may not in
equity be classified as an enemy to the nation.
Provided he contains his advocacy of the form
of government in which he believes, to methods
warranted under the Constitution, it is main
tained that he is well within hia rights. Berger,
it is pointed out, is a socialist, but far from
being either pure or simple in his socialism.
And having been convicted of a crime against
the nation, it is believed to be highly proper
that he be denied the opportunity to sit among
our lawmakers. On the other hand, so far as the
facts in the case have been educed today, the
New York socialists are denied their seats solely
en the grounds that they advocate a form of
government differing from that Under which we
live at present. In other words, the objection
to Berger is hold to be personal, that to the
N?W Yorkers political.
The Interest in the two cases lies in the fact
that. Judging from the reactions of the public
to each, there is new and happy assurance that
Jim and Jane, with their every fiber protesting
hatred and contempts to the red criminals who
shame the nation, still are feble to differentiate
with Justice between the legitimate and the
illegitimate; the lawful and the lawless; the
socialist (however misguided they may believe
him to be) and the red.

While the nation is very properly incensed
at recent disclosures as to the extensive scope
and criminal methods of red propaganda in the
United States, it is well to maintain a sense of
perspective to the extent of appreciating the
fact that, comparatively speaking,) we are ex
tremely fortunte. Viewed in conjunction with
the situation obtaining in practically every Eu
ropean country, the reds on this side of the
Atlantic fade to what is, by comparison, an of
fensive, but easily eradicated shade of pink.
In Germany, during the past week, radical
communists in the capital of the nation sought
to rush the reichstag entrances as a protest
against the proposal of the government to place
the workmen's councils, an approach to the
soviet system, under government control. Sev
eral score of the rioters were killed and many
more wounded, when troops fired into and used
bayonets upon them. England is faced with
the threat of a general strike and Lloyd
George's government reported as "rapidly los
ing control." In France an alarming number
of radical socialists are numbered among those
who actually have the destinies of the nation
in their hands. Civil war is still waged in
China, though reported to be nearing its end.
Terrorists or extremists or reds, whatever
name they go by, are causing the governments
of Italy and Spain deep concern, not merely
from propagandist activities, but from the fact
that they constitute an existing political men
ace. The tragic Btate of Austria and Russia
need not be expanded upon.
This condition of affairs is reviewed to
persuade Jim and Jane, not that the vigorous
campaign against radicalism in America should
in any way be slackened, but that here we are
extremely fortunate to possess the means and
the determination to handle our little problems
as they arise. England and France, Italy and
Spain, China, Germany and Austria and Russia,
must In the end win their fights for continuing
or Installing decent democratic governments.
How lucky we, that have no more of a fight in
hand than would Jack Dempsey faced by a
vicious, cowardly, imbecile child. With us as
with him it becomes merely a matter of duty
to so dispose of the reds that they may not do
injury to the unsuspecting. And that we are
doing.

With peace with Germany less than a week
old the British public has been brought face to
face with the possibility of another war. Those
in charge of the military and naval destinies
of the nation spent the week end, following an
emergency call, in Paris, in consultation with
Lloyd George and other British officials. The
situation in the middle east is threatening as a
result of bolshevist military successes. Flushed
with victory, newly possessed of enormous sup
plies of food, raw materials, coal and rolling
stock, it is regarded as certain that they will
not rest content with their present conquests,
but will seek to extend bolshevism either east
ward or westward. The British government
undoubtedly anticipates trouble in the direction
of India. Announcement has been made that
the allies propose to finance the arming of the
Poles to meet the threat in that direction. And
all the world is watching for the nellt move in
the game in which all the world is vitally con
cerned.
