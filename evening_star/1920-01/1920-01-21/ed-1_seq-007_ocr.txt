Services Kquity Big Problem.
The great question which faced the
reclassification commissioners today
as they sat down at table to begin
on the final determination of these
and other problems was that of equity
as between the various "services" into
which government employes have been
divided.
Who i? there to say that a school
teacher, for instance, has a right to
a higher salary than a clerk?
Yet it is just this sort of question
that the commission has to face and
solve. Equity between the fifty-one
services into which the 107,000 em
ployes have been divided is the larg
est single problem, perhaps, which the
commission has to solve.
This question of equity as between
services is also the most "ticklish"
which the reclassification commission
faces. In solving it the commission
runs more risk of treading on tender
toes than in solving any other prob
lem, it is generally regarded. When
one attempts to say that certair
classes of positions oueht to draw
more money than others on account ol
tlie very inheritant qualities of the

trouble.
j The reclassification commission, it is
j believed, will attempt the solution ol
1 this truly great difficulty by the use ol
(common sense, above all. and by th?
j utilization of comparative pay data
secured in cities throughout the east
ern United States. These comparative
data have been obtained for all the
positions in the government service
here, and will play a large part in the
final determination of salaries to be
set for the 107,000 federal employes ir
the District.
'*Health and Deeeney Budgets.**
What will become of the "health and
decency budgets," from which the em
ployes are expecting so much?
j It was indicated at the commission
j today that these budgets,- set for the
single man and single woman and for
the family of five, will play a large
part In the determination of salaries.
As pointed out yesterday, govern
ment employes need not look for an
increase in salary simply on account
of possessing a family. In Canada, un
der civil reclassification, a bonus was
paid an employe for each child. Such a
scheme will not obtain here, but the
"health and decency budgets" will be
kept in mind by the commissioners ir
the ultimate pay solutions.
Clerks today were not sanguine
that the "health and decency budget'
of approximately $2,200 will prevail
in the final solution in every class
It was pointed out that each one ol
the 1.700 classes had opportunity in
it for an employe to rise by means
of ''periodic efficiency increases," as
detailed yesterday, but it is not be
lieved that the maximum of the four
proba.ble salary steps in each, class
will necessarily have to be $2,200.
Salary gradations in a particular
class might run something like this:
$1,200 in entrance, $1,300 the second
year, ?1.350 the third year and $1,400
the fourth year. Here the employe
would stop his salary advancement
until he took an examination to se
cure a real promotion to a higher
class of work.
Thus it may be seen that if a
clerk did not have brains and "push"
enough to get out of his class, no
matter how old he might be. or how
many children he might have, he
would never get more than $1,400 a
year salary.
