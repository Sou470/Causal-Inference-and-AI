The Housewives' Alliance.
At the first meeting of the house
wives of the city yesterday, attended
by 400 women, for the purpose of
organizing to tight the high living
costs, one of the speakers set forth
a model plan of housekeeping based
upon a family budget and a three
shift force of servants. It is to be
feared that this method of running
a household will not appeal strongly
to the average Washington woman
who has the care of the home. For
a good many months the servant prob
lem has been acute here, as perhaps
elsewhere. What with wartime
drains and other causes, the supply of
domestic "help" has been seriously
depleted. Many households hereto
fore blessed with kitchen service have
been thrown upon their own resources.
Many women who have never before
"done their own work" have baked
and washed dishes and even done the
laundry work. Servant hire has gone
up with everything else. The aver
age pay of the domestic worker has
doubled during the past four years.
A time limit has been adopted by
many. In seme- households it is nec
essary for the wife to prepare the
breakfasts in order to have the help
of the servant in the afternoon for
dinner. In these conditions to urge
a three-shift system is to ask the
impossible. Only the exceptional
household could possibly afford such
a plan, whatever the scale of "pay.
Household economy can be prac
ticed and can be made effective in
reducing the living costs. The house
wires of Washington are eager for
a chance to organize to secure mu
tual help in their struggle with the
burden of high prices. They will not
be encouraged, however, to go into
a movement that is based upon the
three shifts of servants plan, or into
any project that involves complex
budgetary systems. They want to
know how to buy cheaper, how to find
the best markets, how to contribute
in some way to organized efforts that
may bring down the seala of costs.
In other eities these housewives' al
liances have been formed, and have
done excellent work, with large mem
berships. Washington should have
such a movement. The average of
intelligence is so high here that any
enterprise in the line of co-operative
endeavor, if put on the plane of the
average family needs and resources,
will enlist an effective following.
Disputes are inevitable in Army and
Navy life. The important thing is
not to allow them to get in the way
when there is real fighting to be
done.

No Cause for Alarm.
Daily reports of large numbers of
"new cases of influenza" should not
cause alarm among the people of
Washington. It is altogether prob
able that many of these cases, though
formally reported by physicians as
influenza, are not of that character,
but are merely heavy colds or pos
sibly attacks of the old-style grip.
This is the season for pulmonary af
flictions. People "take cold" easily
in the snow and slush. They are ac
tive in going about to social engage
ments and in many cases do not dress
adequately. They wear thin shoes
without protection from the damp.
Every winter there is a heavy in
crease of sickness due to these ex
posures. Presumably physicians pre
fer to be safe in their reports and
class many of these cases as influenza
for fear that by not doing so they
may err on the dangerous side.
'Ihiis far there have been so few
deaths from pneumonia this month
that great encouragement should be
felt on the s<-ore of public security
from the dreadful visitation that
swept the country a year and a quar
ter ago. At the present rate of mor
tality from this cause the total for
January will be less than, a year ago,
which was less than that for January,
1918. It will be remembered that two
years ago intense cold prevailed and
this high total -of pneumonia deaths
was doubtless due to that fact. With
a falling rate from this enuse it would
seem well assured that there is no
immediate danger from a recurrence
of the influenza plague.
Raininess of mind is one of the
best safeguards against disease, and
there is nothing in the local situation
to warrant any disturbance of the
public, however high may run the
daily figures of so-called influenza
cases. The patients suffering from
these afflictions, so recorded, are re
covering so steadily that if the capi- j
tal has a return of the visitation at
all it is in the lightest form that need
cause no apprehension. Nevertheless
every precaution against disease
transmission is to be taken now as j
always. It is possible to be careful
without becoming alarmed.

The "Line of Duty Club" is grow

The hope is that several thousand
names will be enrolled, so that in
the future should such emergencies
arise as that of the slaying of Police
man McKimmie in pursuit of a bur
glar a sufficient fund will be auto
matically forthcoming for the benefit
of those he leaves.
But, however generous and prompt
the public may be in meeting such re
quirements, this is not the right way
to provide for the dependents of po
licemen who die in the discharge of
their duties. Public funds should
always be available in sufficient
amount for this purpose. The
police pension fund nt present is in

cient to meet the 'lemands upon it. It

Now that the whole question of police
