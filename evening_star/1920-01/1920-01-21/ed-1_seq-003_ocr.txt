CENSUS IN A WEEK
Supervisor Mattingly Sees
the Early Ending of the
D. C. Enumeration.
Completion of the taking of the
fourteenth decennial census in the Dis
trict of Columbia is expected by the
middle of next week. So well is the
work of "cleaning up" the few dis
tricts left uncovered being handled
that Supervisor Mattingly believes an
other week of hard work will finish
the job here. He cannot make any
positive prediction, he says, because
he does not know just how man]'
names his enumerators have left be
hind. but individual census slips are
coming into the office by mail in ever
increasing numbers, thus helping the
"pick-up" work of the enumerators.
Eipect* to Bf First.
Even with the increase in time need
ed for completing the taking of the
census in the District, census offi
cials believe the District will still
be the first locality of more than
25.000 inhabitants to furnish final cen
sus returns. New York city and Chi
cago have both turned in several
boxes of portfolios, but these do not
include the whole of the returns from
these cities ?nd both are expected to
lag far behind Washington in the final
returns. Assistant Director Steuart's
order of last Monday directing census
supervisors to send in portfolios as
fast as completed has resulted in more
being received at the census bureau,
but no city of 25,000 or more has
turned in completed census lists as
yet.
Enumeration of Kndlcala.
The alleged "radicals" now on Ellis
Island as a result of recent arrests
by Department of Justice agents
have all been approached by New
York enumerators and those who
claimed residence in the United States
on January 1, 1920. have been enu
merated. Mr. Steuart announced to
day. The same procedure has been
followed with others of this class un
der arrest throughout the country.
I'opnlation A boat 435,000.
Mr. Mattingly has been queried sev
eral times during the past few day3
for an estimate of the population of
the District, but in every case has
informed his questioners that no es
timate is possible at this time. The
census bureau estimates that the Dis
trict of Columbia, following the usual
rule of growth, would have had a
population of 379.886 on July 1. 1919.
This figure is considerably lower than
shown in the recent police census,
which was 455.528. Census officials
are inclined to the view, it was
learned today, that the police census
was too high. They claim that in
taking the police census the enu
merators took no cognizance of the
fact that many persons in the Dis
trict at the time of that census were
transients and could not be counted
as permanent residents. This num
ber is estimated at about 20.000,
therefore under this reasoning the
United States census would show a
population of about 435,000.
Civic bodies and city organization,
under the impression that the census
would positively end with the expira
tion of the fifteen-day time limit,
have wired the director for additional
time to obtain the count in many
cities. In each case the director has
informed them that he has granted
additional time, and that the census
count will be completed, no matter
how long it takes.
