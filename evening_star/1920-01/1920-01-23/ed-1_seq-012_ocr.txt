TEACHERS' SALARY
BEINGDISCUSSED
Subcommittee of Board
Meets School Betterment
Body.
The board of education bill provid
ing for an emergency salary increase
for public school teachers was the
subject of a conference between the
salary subcommittee of the board and
the salary subcommittee of the School
Betterment Council at the Franklin
School this afternoon. There was hope
today in official circles that the measure
will be in the hands of the congressional
legislation committees by February 2.
"The idea of the conference." said Dr.
John Van Schaick, president of the board,
"is to get the benefit of the knowledge
and experience of business men com
posing the betterment council subcom
mittee. We desire further to acquaint
them with what the board of educa
tion has done in the effort to afford
our teachers economic relief, to lay
before them its problems confronted
in connection with the effort, and in
dicate to them what the board wants
to do."
Chairman of Subcommittee.
Dr. Charles P. Neill is chairman of
the board subcommittee, and with
him are associated Dr. Abram Simon
and Mrs. Cora-lie Cook. William J.
Eynon is chairman of the betterment
council subcommittee. There were pres
cnt also at the conference Dr. Van
Schaick, Stephen K. Kramer, assistant;
Charles J. Corby, chairman of the School
Betterment Council, and other members
of that organization.
It was said today the board of edu
cation has delayed pushing the emer
gency bill rapidly through local of
ficial hands into the hands of the
congressional committees because the
board is between two fires and must
act with great care in order to make
its efforts successful.
Teacher* Demand Hl(h Schedule.
A large mimer of teachers have de
manded that the maximum figures
contained in salary schedule "A,"
which the board proposed to submit,
be raised several hundred dollars in
various items. On the other hand,
intimations have come from Con
gress that any salary schedules sent
to "the hill" from the board will be
scrutinized very closely and pared
down to the bone.
It is hoped the board and the
betterment council can work out a
salary-increase plan that will at
once relieve and satisfy the teach
ers and be at the same time accepta
ble to Congress.

Must England Fight Reds
To Retain Hold on India?
"To make a wide impression" on the elements of discontent in the Punjab, according to their
commander, Brig. Gen. R. E. H. Dyer, British and Indian troops fired without warning last April
on a meeting of Indians at Amritsar, killing five hundred persons and wounding about fifteen
hundred in ten minutes. This action is now being investigated by a committee headed by Lord
Hunter. The violent outbreaks of disorder in Calcutta, Bombay, and the Punjab, eventuated from
the "passive-resistance" movement against the Rowlatt Act, which is directed at revolutionary and
anarchical crime, and is made necessary by the attempts to overthrow British rule in India. Agita
tors seized upon this measure, the London Morning Post tells us, to organize an agitation which
"threatened the very existence of British rule in India." Events in Afghanistan, and even in
Bolshevik Russia, "may or may not have had a connection with A is movement."
