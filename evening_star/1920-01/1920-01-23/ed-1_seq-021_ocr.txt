RUTH SAYS HE WANTS NO MORE HOMERS.
Some one possessed of a penchant fOr figures and a sturdy adding
machine has calculated that the mathematical chances against drawing a
royal flush in America's most popular indoor sport are 649,730 to one.
The same odds might safely be quoted against Babe Ruth going through
with his intention to give up home-run hitting and confine himself to
^ingles in an effort to lead the American League in hitting, and in the
interest of team work. In a recent interview in Los Angeles, where lie is
keeping his swing in good working order by walloping golf balls, Ruth is
MUOtfd as saying:
"I am going to choke my bat a little more this year and try for more
hits and less distance. 1 figure that a man can be of more value to a ball
club if he hits oftrner than he will if he swings from the ground up all
the time and only connects once a game for a long one. If the long one
doesn't come at t^e right time it isn't worth a single or two in the 1
pinches. 1 went after the home-run record last year, and naturally I was
taking a full cut at the ball practically all the time. I'm satisfied now
that 1 did have enough luck to make a new record, and from now on I'm
going after the old base knocks and let the homers take care of them
selves."
Ruth's expressed attitude is commendable. There is no debate on
any of the points he mentions?except that of being satisfied with his
home-run record. With a ball player, amassing circuit clouts is a good
deal like making money with the average man. The more he gets the
more he wants. Ruth last year was second only to Washington's recently
acquired infielder?Maurice Shannon?in the frequency with which he
struck out. Theoretically he should get more hits ii he curtailed his
swing to the point where he would fan less often, but there is no assur- I
ance of it.
Ruth not long ago was sold for a price more than double that ever
before $iven up for a player, and his high valuation is due entirely to the
reputation he earned as the greatest long-distance walloper in the history
oi the game. He led the league in scoring runs and driving them in.
That's just about good enough. If he tries to tinker with his style it is
liable to throw him off Ins stride entirely. But granting that he boosts
his average by eliminating th? big swings he will depreciate as a spec
tacular attraction, and the Yankee owners wouldn't fancy that. They
invested heavily for a thrill producer, and that probably will be his as
signment again this year, as last.
Ruth no doubt, is sincere in announcing his intentions, but it is hard
for a player who loves to hit?and can hit?as Ruth does, to change from
the old toe-hold wallop to just meeting the ball. That short right field
wall at the Polo Grounds will be beckoning to Ruth in seventy-seven
games next seasot, and :t is exceedingly doubtful that he will be able to
resist the temptation to give the ball a ride.
