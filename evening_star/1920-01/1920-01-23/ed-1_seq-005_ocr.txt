false."

the full facts in relation to the in
vestigation of conditions in the city
of Newport last summer. It was ex

ENGLISH AUTHOR GIVES TALK
St. John Ervine at Central High
Under Fine Arts' Auspices.
St. John Ervine. English author and
play writer, lectured last night at
Central High School under the aus
pices of the Washington Fine Arts
Society, taking for his subject "Rec
ollections of My Elders."
Unusual interest attaches to Mr. Er
vine's visit from the fact that one of
his plays, "John Ferguson," is being
presented here this week at the Na
tional Theater. Mr. Ervine lost a leg
during the early stages of the world
war and his first-hand impressions
and comments on the conflict were
vitally interesting.
Entering upon the theme of the
evening, the lecturer gave personal
recollections and impressions of
George Bernard Shaw, H. G. Wells.
Arnold Bennett. Anatole France, Tol
stoi, John Galsworthy and other
literary notables, with many of whom
i Mr. Ervine has close personal ac
quaintance. Another is William But
ler Yeats, who lectures at Central
j High in February.
Mr. Ervine stated that the super
1 man doctrine of Nietsche. the German
philosopher. whose spirit largely
actuated and influenced Germany's
conduct of the war, had been dis
proven by life in the trenches and
dug-outs. There, the speaker de
clared. where men lived at close quar
ters in gToups of twenty and thirty,
the turn-the-other-cheek doctrine of
Christianity, as taught by Jesus and
Tolstoi, had proved a success and
yielded unexpected experiences and
results.
Another -war like the recent one. in
the opinion of Mr. Ervine, would
mean the end of our western civiliza
tion.
Mr. Ervine was heard by a large
audience, which was generous in its
applause and attention.

''Conditions on the river are not bet
ter," commented Lieut. Russell Dean
of the harbor police precinct today.
"They are worse, but I can't say there
is going to be a flood. I would ad
vise persons along the river front
in Georgetown, however, to protect
their property."
What follows the present condi
tion, the lieutenant stated, will de
pend entirely upon how the ice starts
down the river. If it goes out grad
ually there should be no damage,
done, but if a heavy rain should set
the ice in the upper river moving at
a rapid rate trouble may result.
Lieut. Dean said he thought the
solid ice in the river is about eipht
inches thick, nothing like as thick as
it was two years ago, but the snow
and hail increased it to probably ten
or twelve inches in sheltered places.
