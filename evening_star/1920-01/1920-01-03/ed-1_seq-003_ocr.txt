Statement Clarifies Bureau of
Education Member's Posi
j tion as to "Incompetents."
j in, W. Carson Ryan, jr., of the hu
re.in education, and former presi

_ has sent a statement to The

j Kyan believes that there is less in
competency among government em
ploye.'' than outside.
I?r. Hyan's Statement.
"1 notice that my name is mention
ed in Wednesday's Star in connection
with the discussion over elimination
of alleged inefficient employes from
the government service," Dr. Kyan
stated.
"Let me make clear at the outset
that what I said at the hearings be
fore the reclassification commission
on December 9 referred specifically
to retirement. I said that of Congress
really meant business in this talk
about reducing the number of em
ployes it could pass the retirement
bill :it once and thereby let out be
tween (1.000 and 7.000?this being the
estimate of the joint conference on re
tirement.
"It is true that in answer to a ques
tion l'rom Mr. Cooper of the com
mission. as to whether I believed there
were incompetents other than super
annuates in the government service, 1
replied that there undoubtedly were,
but that this problem would have to
l>e solved more slowly, by adoption of
higher standards, minimum wage and
other measures. I did not object to
The Star's account, because, though I
realized it might be misunderstood, it
did preserve the retirement connec
tion in part, and also because T did
not want to find fault. 1 considered
that The Star representative at the
hearings had acted in good faith, and
I appreciate, as T think we all do, the
valuable service The Star has render
ed in covering the reclassification
work so thoroughly. But the para
graph in Wednesday's issue, in which
my name was used does not make
clear that what I said had reference
specifically to retirement.
Standard Higher Than Outfiide.
"As to the general question of sup
posed incompetency in the govern
ment service, efficiency is of course
a relative matter, and individuals are
likely to judge entirely on the basis
of their own limited experiences. My
own judgment is necessarily qualified
in the same way, but I must say. after
having worked in both private and
public employ, and bad a rather un
usual opportunity to observe govern
ment employment. I am tlrmly con
vinced that there is less incompe
tency among government employes
than outside, chiefly because there
are fewer family deadweights and
other drawbacks of nepotism
that still characterize private
employ except in the most
progressive business concerns.
There are a few real incompetents in
government service, of course, out
side of those, who. having been faith
ful and efficient workers in the past,
are now incapable by reason of age,
but they are mainly the result of
other days when the civil service was
not as weil safeguarded as now. and
the percentage is very small in the
regular government establishments.
Certainly not over 2 or 3 per cent, I
should say.
Civil Service Relaxed in War,
"It should be said also that during
the war civil service standards were
relaxed somewhat for certain kinds of
service, and it is generally recog
nized that some incompetents got in
as a result of these lowered standards.
It would not be unfair to ask the em
ployes who came in by means of sub
standard examinations tq take a new
examination to prove their right to
permanent status?assuming, of
course, that there is genuine need for
them.
"As to the efficiency attitude of the
local employes' organization, it should
be noted that far from being a new
feature, the efficiency principle has
been a guiding principle of the union
since the start. It was written into
the constitution of the Washington
local at the very beginning and was
adopted by the National Federation.
Recently in the advisory committee
on wage policy it was the three em
ployes' representatives who took the
lead in asserting that better wages
were to be regarded as efficiency
wages intended to attract and retain
the efficient and eliminate the less
efficient. This' attitude has charac
terized all the hearings before the
reclassification commission. If any
thing. the employes have been more
insistent even than the administra
tion officials on the importance of
high standards as a prerequisite to*
adequate pay.
"It is not my function in this con
nection to speak in behalf of the local
unions, but I believe any competent
observer will find that their policy is
to secure fair consideration for any
employe who feels that he has been
discriminated against, but not to
shelter the incompetents. Its atti- j
tude may be seen in the fact that it Is
not opposing reductions of force when
these are in the interest of the gov-
ernment service, only insisting that
decent warning shall be given, that
the most competent shall be retained.
that the spirit of the civil service
regulations shall be complied with,
and that congressional influence, real
or claimed, shall not be allowed to
affect the situation. 1
Worker* Shonld Be Encouraged.
"There is one element that is usual
ly forgotten in a discussion of this
sort. I refer to the stimulating effect
of decent salaries and working con
ditions in increasing the actual out
put of work, whether in outside in
dustry or in the government. On the
whole, the government gets more for I
its money from its employes than pri-
vate business does?some public opin- j
ion to the contrary, notwithstanding.
"It would get higher service than
it does, and much higher than private
business gets, if it would once open
the door of hope to the employes. Un
derpayment is a very real evil in I
the government service today: it ap
proximates actual starvation in some
cases; it compels the employes to do
outside work at night and to put the
rest of their families to labor in or-
der to live, and it is destructive of
the whole ideal of government effi- j
clency."
