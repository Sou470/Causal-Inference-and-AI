The Wilson-Bryan Treaty Issue.
The issue between President Wil
son and Mr. Bryan on the score of the
peace treaty is clear-cut and signifies
.so distinct a democratic schism in
this matter that early ratification
along the lines the President desires
is practically out of the question. Mr.
Wilson wants the issue referred to the
people for decision at the next elec
tion. Mr. Bryan points out that this
is doubtful of results, and in any
case serves to prolong decision. To
carry the treaty through the Senate
as it is now written means the elec
tion of a two-thirds majority in that
body, involving the conversion of nine
teen republican seats into democratic,
an obviously impossible task save
through a tremendous development of
public sentiment. Mr. Bryan's po
litical acumen is such that he will be
credited now with clearer vision than
the President in this matter. If there
is one fact more plain than another
in this present situation it is that
compromise alone can effect ratifica
tion. Mr. Bryan realizes this perfect
ly. Perhaps the President does also,
but the latter is still hopeful that upon
a submission of the issue to the peo
ple they will demand a no-compro
mise ratification. The treaty can be
ratified with reservations in the near
future. If the question is submitted
to the people for decision in the next
election it cannot even in the best
circumstances be ratified until after
March 4, 1921, assuming the election
of a democratic President and a Sen
ate democratic by two-thirds. The
logic of the mathematics is altogether
favorable to Mr. Bryan's position.
Automatic Telephones.
A beginning in the installation of
the automatic telephone system in this
city is contemplated by the Chesa
peake and Potomac Company in the
near future. The experiment will be
noted with the keenest interest. It is
proposed, according to understanding,
to start with intrabuildin^ exchanges,
thus relieving switchboards of hand
ling traffic of strictly indoor nature.
This will not affect the general traffic,
which, until the automatics are ex
tended, must be carried through op
erator-manned switchboards.
The automatic telephone is Teally
not an experiment at present, having
been successfully installed and op
erated elsewhere, notably in Norfolk.
It has worked smoothly and has ex
pedited traffic so markedly that those
who have used it would not willingly
return to the old system. Washing
ton has been in such a state of tele
phone congestion?along with all other
forms of congestion?during the past
two or three years that it has not
offered a fair field for the new in
stallation. Now, however, conditions
are returning to normal, and it is
gratifying to And that a beginning
is to be made with the automatic
method.

Campaign Funds.
Tall yarns about campaign funds
are making their appearance. The
season has opened, and will last until
election day.
Both parties are, and will remain,
under charges. As the yarns run,
both are relying on money more than
on oratory or public service to win
the fight. It is money that will make
the mare go. Or, changing the figure,
the longest money pole will knock the
presidential persimmon.
This is not a now industry. On
the contrary, it is old. Such yarns
have marked political campaigns, and
particularly presidential campaigns,
for a long time.
It has probably never been diffi
cult for the managers of either party
to raise money for campaign purposes.
Party spirit is strong in campaign
years. Party cries when properly
J
raised make a strong appeal. Tight
wads become liberal contributors,
while spenders bceome more liberal
than ever. Touch a man on his party
pride and you get him.
This year large funds?larger may
be than ever before?will be neces
sary. Campaigning has taken on some
new and expensive features. And old
features make a heavy draft, and will
have to be supported.
Then millions have been added to
the electorate. The women will have
to be organized. They are very much
interested in the ballot, and prepar-:
ing to go to the polls in large nuni- i
bers in November. The cost of work
among them alone will reach a high
figure. They will be liberal con- j
tributors themselves to whatever funds
may be required to give them the
proper' standing in 'militant party
force#.
But there is no warrant for a charge
that either side meditates carrying
the day by corruption. The fact is
the electorate never has been bought,
and is not today for sale. Here and
there machine politicians have always
trafficked in suffrage, but never on a
large or nationally decisive scale. No
presidential result has ever been ob
taiiied by the corrupt use of money
at the polls.

Where Credence Ceases.
The men I've met are very few
Who would prevaricate.
I hail 'most anything as true
That any one may state,
Except those words that sound anew,
"I'm not a candidate."
The courses that men must pursue
Are much controlled by Fate;
It is a fact that very few
Can read the future straight.
So why put the announcement
through,
"I'm not a candidate?"
When oratory takes its cue
On stump or by the plate,
With faith implicit I review
Whatever they relate,
Except the words of one or two,
"I'm not a candidate."

able to get a number of people in
terested in the hope that some day
they'll catch him in the truth.
