Labor and the Campaign.
Tt is stated that labor, both union
and non-union, will take an active
jiart in this year's campaign. It
should. It has a large stake in the
game.
So has capital. So should it take
an active part in the campaign. It
should look out for its own interests.
The general public likewise should
bestir itself in campaign matters this
year. No campaign in many years
has signified so mucji to the public.
This is but to say that all classes
and conditions of citizens should ap
pear in great numbers at the polls
next November and exercise their
light of suffrage. 'lhe country will
benefit by a full expression, and on
all the issues presented.
Many more than one issue will be
presented. There will be the tariff
issue, with its appeal to all interests.
Capital wants a continuation of such
protection as may be necessary to the
earning of dividends, l^abor wants
such an arrangement of schedules and
rates as will insure good wages and
the maintenance of the American
standards of living. And the general
public will want to see that prosperity
which has long been promoted by a
protective tariff.
There will be the railroad issue,
likely to take the form of govern
ment ownership and operation. Should
the government enter upon that pol
icy? Those who object assert that
the step if taken will lead in the di
rection of state socialism, and that
state socialism will spell a radical
change in American institutions. Does
capital, does labor, does the general
public consider that our institutions
ha<e failed, or are outworn*
And the merchant marine issue is
of interest to all. Increased foreign
trade will mean increased domestic
production?more work for both capi
tal and labor, and more satisfaction
for the public. The national pride
will be heightened by the appear
ance of American merchant fleets on i
the seas.
These and other issues will be pass
ed upon by the two leading parties
in their platforms this year; and can
didates for the presidency and for
Congress will accordingly be pledged, j
No voter need be in doubt about his
ballot. He will know from his con
victions in which bo* it belongs, and
so can properly deposit it. And the
new Congress when it meets?very
likely in special session soon after
March 4 of next year?will address j
itself to the redemption of the prom
ises upon which it has obtained
power.
All dividend earners, all wage earn-,
ers, and everybody else should be very
active in politics this year.
Statements from the War Depart
ment that tho athletic spirit of the
Army caused the Tennessee moun
taineers to become tennis experts will
create lively expectations as to new
talent in the tournament news.

Baby Bonds for 1920.
Luring 1920 the government will
continue its "thrift" campaign. That
is, it will offer a new issue of thrift
and war savings stamps, both as a
means of raising revenue and as an
encouragement to popular thrift.
After two years of this form of small
contributions to the government's
funds the people have acquired a
habit that is invaluable and is cer
tain to make tor their prosperity.
The total of these securities bought
by the people during the past two
years has been $1,128,000,000. Prae
j ticall.v every dollar of this represents
money that would not otherwise have
been saved. A very large part con
sists of the pennies and dimes of chil
j dren. Youngsters who never had sav
ings accounts before have certificates
stowed away in safe places, for re
demption a few years hence.
There is no reason why this should
not become a permanent feature of
the Treasury's operations. Certainly
for a l3ng time the war debt of the
government will require unusual
measures for funding, and the short
term loans of the people will bo an
efficient aid in turning over the ac
count for gradual reduction. The
1920 issue of "baby bonds" will have
a slight advantage over the others in
that they will be redeemable at the
Treasury without notice, although the
ten-day notice requirement will con
tinue as to post office redemptions.
It is hard to see why this distinction
is made. Redemptions are not like
ly to be demanded at the post offices
in such sudden volume as to cause
embarrassment -without notice. Wash
ingtonians cannot complain of this
difference, as they have direct access
to the Treasury, but people living at
a distance from this city will find no
special advantage in the direct Treas
ury redemption over the tendav
period required for post office cash
ing.
The Armstrong Fund.
A most gratifying response has been
made to the appeal for funds for the
widow of Detective Armstrong, who
was recently slain in discharge of
his duty in this city. The fund, that
at the close of yesterday's reckoning
was $3,409.69, it is hoped to bring
to at least $.3,000. That it will reach
that point seems now certain. The
69 cents included in the total should
be carefully uoted. The pennies that
have gone into this fund are eloquent
of public interest and concern. They
are also indictments against the neg
lect through law to provide adequate
ly for those who are slain in the line
of duty in the public welfare. It
should not be necessary to ask the
people to contribute to such a fund
in order to keep from want the widow
of a brave man who sacrificed himself.
But inasmuch as it is necessary in
the absence of adequate pension pro
vision, the people are responding, and
in a measure that is eloquent of the
community's appreciation ot' the need.
