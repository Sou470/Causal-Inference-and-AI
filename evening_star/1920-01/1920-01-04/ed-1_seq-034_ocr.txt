The above matter, in an article dedicated

The nation has been marking time, possibly be

The outstanding feature of the week was
the announcement by the President's second
industrial conference of a tentative plan for the
adjustment of industrial controversies through
reasonable rather than forceful means. This
plan, of which the establishment of a tribunal
of employers and employes in each of twelve
industrial regions and of a central national ap
peal board are the basic provisions, was sub
mitted to the nation tor constructive criticism.
Briefly, it is proposed that regional boards
of inquiry and adjustment and "a natural ap
:PerT'
hint mafchlnery tor Mtmr the artm-ation or in
vestigation of such industrial conflicts as may
not be adjusted by direct negotiations between
employer and employe. Panels of employers
and employes are to be drawn in each region,
and from these, each side in the controversy
having the right of a specific number of chal
lenges, will be drawn the boards as occasion
arises. Where both sides consent to use a board
in, its adjustment capacity the process is to be
one of arbitration pure and simple, with the
unanimous findings to have the full force of
trade agreements. Where either side refuses to
participate in the adjustment the board func
tions in its inquiry capacity, and is to be au
thorized by law to make any investigations it
may see fit. These are, in the main, the broader
outlines of the proposal.
Such criticisms as have been volunteered to
date have taken one of two forms. The Ameri
can Federation of Labor hag expressed itself
as squarely opposed to the whole scheme on the
basis that the plan as outlined falls to recog
nize trade unions as the basis for representa
tion; that It Involves "compulsory labor," and
that tt does not permit workers to have a direct
voice in the establishment of adjustment boards.
There has been general criticism to the effect
that the solution of the problem does not go
far enough; that the conference should have
gone into the subject of how employers and em
ployes may be brought to a willingness to settle
their differences by reason.

Speaking frankly of the objections of the
American Federation of Labor, Jim and Jane
will be prompted to opine that the officers of
that organization covet the entire suoid mam
mal, and that, not so much for the class they
represent, as for themselves. The conference
could not definitely recognize trade unions as
the basis for representation, inasmuch as it
proposed a means for bettering the conditions
of labor, and all labor is not unidn labor. It
specifies that employes shall choose their own
representatives, union or non-union, as the ma
jority in any given controversy or region elects.
The implication that the plan involves "com
pulsory labor" is difficult to understand. Work
ers and employers may use the services of the
boards or not, as they see fit, and any finding
must be by unanimous vote. Such compulsion
as might be used could only be that arising
from justice and truth, supported by public sen
timent. As to the protest that workers are not
to have a direct voice in the establishment of
adjustment boards, it need only be pointed out
that in law there is an established precedent
that a litigant may not select his own jury.

In reference to the criticism that the con
ference did not go far enough in its proposal,
there is this to be said: The plan as outlined
merely offers the machinery for adjusting con
troversies, when those concerned are trilling to
make use of it. Upon the human element in the
problem, upon the methods whereby employers
and employes may be rendered willing to settle
their disputes by reason, it touches but lightly.
If any plan whereby labor and capital may be
awakened to a full appreciation of their respon
sibilities one to the other and each, to the nation
at large and of the economic fallacies of war
fare in industry can be found, it should, by all
means, be included in the finished effort of the
conference. The difficulty, being in the fact that
this phase of the problem is so largely one of
individual conscience, should not preclude the
effort of striving for the solution.

inating party might lose all sense of

their actions to feel no responsibility]
to the voters.
