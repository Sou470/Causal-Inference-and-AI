Definite stops looking to the adop

tions on the treaty- of peace with
Germany are expected to be taken at
a meeting- of some fifteen democratic
senators this afternoon. Senator Hoke
Smith of Georgia is a prime mover in
this matter. It is his opinion, and
that of a number of others on the
democratic side, that the Senate
should proceed to ratify the treaty
with such reservations as can be
agreed upon, and that it should then
be transmitted to the President.
Thr Senate will have fulfilled its
functions then as a part of the treaty
making power under the Constitution.
If the President should see fit not to
exchange the ratifications and pigeon
hole the treaty because it does not
suit him, the burden will be upon him
for failing to ratify the pact, and not
upon the Senate, it is argued.
These democratic senators feel that
the President, in adhering to his un
compromising attitude on the treaty,
is blocking the way to any ratifica
tion of the pact by the Senate. Some
j of them assert that they would never
vote for the treaty unless strong
reservations are adopted, particularly
with regard to article X of the
league of nations covenant, which
seeks to impose an obligation upon
all the members of the league to pre
serve the territorial integrity of the
members of the league.

They are anxious to have the treaty
ratified, however, and peace finally

reservation group of senators on the
republican side in an effort to frame

the hope of members of these two
