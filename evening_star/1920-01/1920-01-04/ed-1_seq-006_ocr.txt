ICE ON Ml BASIN
Sport May Continue Today
and Tomorrow, Weather
Forecaster Says.
"With the temperature hovering
not far above the zero mark. Wash
ingtonians again yesterday at d last
night had an opportunity to skate
on the frozen tidal basin in Potomac
Park.
From early in the morning until
10 o'clock last nierht the basin was
thronged with merry skaters. Every
circle of social life in the capital
was represented, from the humblest
newsboy. with his old-fashioned
"hand-me-down" skates, to mem
bers of the diplomatic and govern
mental services. Members of the
diplomatic corps who hale from
northern latitudes were in their ele
ment. Stately matrons vied with
the more agile of the "younger set,"
and the laughter of the skaters
mingled with the metallic ring of
the steel runners.
At one time yesterday afternoon
more than 3.000 skaters were o the
ice. Last night it was estimated that
upward of 2,000 persons enjoyed the
sport.
The skating concession at the tidal
basin is in the hands of L. G. Leech,
who is assisted by his brother. Sidney
W. Leech, as manager. They have
provided more than 2.000 pairs of
skates, which they rent for 50 cents,
requiring first a deposit of $2. which
is returned when the skates are hand
ed hack. A checking stand has been
provided for unnecessary coats and
wraps, and a lunch counter provides
"hot dogs" and other delicacies. Park
policemen were on duty yesterday to
regulate traffic.
The weather forecaster gave assur
ance last night that the temperature
would remain low today and tomor
row, with a possible cloudy condition
and flurries of snow. If that prog
nostication holds good, the tidal basin
will be in great shape for skating to
day. The hours of skating are from
8 a.m. to 10 p.m.

CHANCE TO ESTABLISH
MODEL SCHOOL SYSTEM
Charles I. Corby Tells Betterment
Committee Broad Vision
Is Needed.
"There is before the people of Wash
ington now the opportunity to estab
lish here the model public school sys
tem of the world," said Charles I.
Corby. chairman of the citizens'
school betterment committee, last
night. "All we need is plenty of the
get-together spirit, hard work and
big vision."
Washington may now be described
accurately as the most important seat
of government in the world, Mr. Corby
continued. "A great many people liv
ing here object to the National Capi
tal's becoming an industrial com
munity. Very well. J^et's make it the
most important public school city in
stead of a great industrial town. I
believe Con-gross will be willing to
give us ample aid if we make out a
strong case and show our willingness
to work together for the cause."
Mr. Corby recalled that a famous
South American educator, studying
the public schools in the United
States, recently told John Barrett,
president of the Pan-American Union,
that he was amazed to find that
Washington, capital of the world's
greatest nation, had not by any means
the best schools in the country.
"We ought to have too much pride
to be content with anything le?s than
the best," Mr. Corby declared. "I
hope the movement Just started will
not slacken until we have here a sys
tem which educators from all parts
of the world will come to admire and
study."
