SOLDIERS TO RECOVER VOICES
i

Mile. Barns Tells of Progress She Is
Making With War s Shell-Shocked
Men.
BY WILLIAM E. NASH.
Special Cablegram to The Star and
Chicago Daily \evr?, Copyright, 1920.
PARIS, December 30.?It is refresh
ing to find a person who has not for
gotten the duty we all owe to soldiers
mutilated at the front. Yesterday it
was my fortune to discover a devoted
little French woman who trembles
with indignation at the way these
heroes have been negiected.
Mile. Harris is her name and she is
teaching: twenty dumb soldiers how to
speak again. The unfortunates are
former poilus who on account of shell
shock or cerebral concussion have lost
use of their voices and were regarded
as incurable. It is to these pitiful
wrecks that Mile. Barris has devoted
her life.
Rare Example of Pare Charity.
Never was there an example of purer
charity* She has spent all her money,
amounting to 10.000 francs .normally
*2,000) in pursuit of the holy cause.
The mornings and afternoons she de
votes to the mutilated, the evenings to
rich people who can afford to pay'for
the lessons and supply money enough
*o feed and lodge the more unfortu
nate poilus. All her work for the mu
tilated is without compensation.
Mile. Barris comes from an Alsa
tian family Which fled from Strass
burg in 1871 in order to escape Ger
man rule. This accident of origin in
tensified her patriotism, so that in
1914 when the war came she longed
to do something for France and an
opportunity soon offered itself To the
deaf and dumb institution, where she
was a professor of phonetics, several
soldiers were sent who were suffering
from paralysis of the vocal chords.
Mile. Barris helped to cure them. She
became intensely interested in the
subject and soon she inserted adver
tisements in the daily papers urging
dumb soldiers to come to her for in
struction, and when they did come she
devoted three hours a day to them
free of charge. Gradually this work
overshadowed all other duties. She
gave up her position as school teacher
and the money she had laid aside for
her old age was quickly spent until
now she hasn't a penny of her own.
Rich People Contributed.
"During the war,*' said the unas
suming heroine, "certain rich people
contributed money to my support, for
the enthusiasm then was great for
any charity pertaining to the 'mutiles
de la guerra' (war mutilated). It was
largely due to their aid that I was
able to take so much time from my
private lessons. But since the armis
tice matters have changed. The en
thusiasm has waned and my income
from the outside has nearly ceased.
Once only last spring was I able to
obtain 1,600 francs ($320). If nothing
more comes in before February l 1
shall be obliged to curb my activities
or cease entirely. You know that it
is very difficult for me to find time
to make appeals to the public, for
besides my lessons I am cooking
meals for all my proteges and furnish
ing lodging for two of them. I do
this with the aid of one assistant pro
fessor.
"My pupils are of all sorts. Most of
them are well save for their special
infirmity. Some have lost the power
of speech, some stutter so hopelessly
that they cannot be understood and
some have forgotten how to write.
Others have lost their memory. This
complexity, you understand, makes it
imposHible to teach them ill large
classes. If I did certain of the bright
er ones would become discouraged by
the slow progress of the more stupid.
Therefore. I have been forced to give
a great deai of time to individual in
struction. My object is to give the
men back the power of expression
which will enable them to earn their
daily bread. To cure them completely
would require more time and appli
cation than I can afford, but the
utility of my efforts, I think, is shown
by the fact that I have already re
turned fifty pupils to the outside
world.
Report* Proprrpw Made.
"Those I now have are making sat
isfactory progress. Kor instance,
I-ieut. Robert Despas, who came td
me three weeks ago from Les Inva
lides. is already able to make him
self understood. In another month I
hope to discharge him altogether. To
show the results of the work I give
public recitals from time to time. My
desire is to obtain government back
ing and establish a public utilities
service. I could do so much more
pood if I were able to employ four
or five other assistants. For the mo
ment, however, that is only an idle
dream, for the government disinteresta
itself absolutely from the question."
"Do you realise, mademoiselle, the
magnificence of the task you have j
set for yourself?" I asked.
"It is nothing Aut my duty," came
the answer. "If 1 had been a man I
would have fought for my country
Vet I would appreciate a little more
interest from the outside world. It is
an ungrateful undertaking. As you
see, 1 myself have become deaf from
listening to so much incoherent con
versation."
After my talk with Mile. Barris I
was privileged to see some of her
pupils. Almost all were about thirty
years old and normally intelligent as
to outward appearances. But what
strange grimaces came in place of
words when they opened their mouths!
Sometimes they spoke fluently for a
moment and then broke into a chaos
of inarticulate sounds. One among
them particularly attracted my at
tention He had been a traveling
salesman for a fur dealer before the
war. Now he is an errand boy for the
same Arm. By repeating mechanically
such sounds as "Beda, bete, ceda.
ceti. ct'di, cete," he hopes to reiraln
his former eloquence and become a
useful member of society.

To the Editor of The Star:
The industrial conference for set

First, let attention be called to

is the vital thing involved. Right
in the start labor is snubbed, while
secrecy takes the place of open cov
enants.
This tentative plan is an elabora
tion of the Colorado industrial com
mission law, a vicious law that ties
men to their jobs for long periods
of time while employers exploit them.
It is a law, specious and fair on
its face, that established modified
peonage in that state.
Cites Cane In Colorado.
Under such laws, adroitly worded
by men who are not laborers, but
whose interests generally are the op
posite, labor can be delayed and bait
ed and unions wrecked. It worked
out that way in Colorado. I had five
months' experience with that com
mission there. Justice was a farce.
Cases were willfully delayed. Labor
dared not strike till its case was dis
posed of. The case of the journey
men tailors could have been heard in
five days. The commission purposely
delayed it five months. Other cases
were held up almost a year.
That condition could and probably
would obtain under the proposed re
gional plan of this conference of pro
fessors. lawyers, politicians and big
employers; not one of them can have,
or will have, the keen insight to what
labor needs for its protection.
Under the plan proposed, persons
hostile to labor could, and probably
would, soon dominate the whole ma
chinery of Investigation. To pay a
political debt in Colorado a preacher
was put on this commission as chair
man and to represent the state. He
was distinctly hostile to organised
labor. A lawyer was put 011 to rep

hampered, snubbed, while being held
to the onerous conditions it groaned
under
What assurance has labor that it
will Bet a square deal from this Col- 1
orado system on a larger scale? Only
lately two men. big employers and
implacable haters of labor, were put
on a commission to represent the pub
lic in a labor conference.
If this proposed plan is put into
law. as sure as night follows day it
will establish a modified form nf
peonage for American labor and
Americans will not teach their sons
trades. It will not abate unrest. It
will breed hatred and perhaps worse.
Its start was not auspicious. Suspi
cion shrouded its secret meetings. Its
enactments would be a national dis
grace. Its application would be a
tragedy for labor. Suppression will j
not cure utiresi. The only remedy for
unrest is a square deal for the plain
people. Such laws, badly adminis
tered, have within them the germs of
government dissolution. Yet there
are a few things that might keep the
fangs of delay from sinking too deep
into labor's legs, as follows: An ab
solute clause that any award would
bo retroactive to the day a case was
file4. And something less than a ma
jority decision should make an award
morally binding. Jn case unanimous
decisions are necessary a pro-employ
