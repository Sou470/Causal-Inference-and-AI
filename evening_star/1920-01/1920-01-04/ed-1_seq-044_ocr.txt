PARIS, January 1.?Whether Paris
is a safe place for American students
to live in has come up for discussion
lately in connection with a statement
attributed to the Rev. Stanley Fisher
of Paris by an American pastor in
Evanston, 111.
'*It must be remembered that there
are two Pariseg in existence." sayB a j
great American educator. "There is
one for respectable French families
who live as quietly as the bourgeois
of America, and there is another for
the huge floating population. The life
of the boulevard is wicked. That can
not be denied, but it mutt not be at
tributed wholly to the French. Paris
houses a parasitic foreign element
larger than that in any other city in
the world. It comes here simply for
pleasure, to spend mojiey and to pass
the time in idleness. To rater to the
pleasures of this element has become
one of the city industries. Knormous
amounts of capital, controlled by four
or five large trusts, have been invest
ed in hotels, cafes, theaters and
amusement places of the more ques
tionable sort. These trusts naturally
fight for a wide-open city and they
have defeated some promising legis
lation in the chamber of deputies.
Morality at Lorn Kbit.
"lastly, it can be stated that mo
rality is now at a lower ebb here than
it ever was before. This is one of the
phenomena of the reconstruction
period which is not by any means pe
culiar to Paris. It is aggravated here.
however, by the crisis in the housing
problem. The city at present holds
about 4,000,000 people, although it has
proper accommodations for only
2.500,000. It must not be thought that
there are no prominent Frenchmen
interested in moral reform. M. Raux,
the police prefect, is conducting an
active campaign to round up the un
desirables. His flying squadrons have
won an enviable reputation through
out the underworld. Likewise, last
summer, a regular campaign was
started to clean up Paris, but, unfor
tunately, as I have cause to know, it
ran up against the influence of a hotel
trust and was worsted."
For English-speaking girls at least
there are several place* of refuge from
temptation. The Ada" Leigh Home
alone has established two pensions
where governesses, stenographers, office
girls and students may obtain board
and lodging for six francs ($1.20) a
day. Girls absolutely without money
are taken in free. The object is to
provide a home rather than a refuge.
Ada Leigh homers, however, must sub
mit to a certain amount of restriction.
They cannot go out after 10 p.m. save
on two nights a week with the special
permission of the matron. The Ada
Leigh Home urges all friendless Anglo
Saxon girls to come there to live. It
provides an employment bureau for
those who wish work and investigates
all positions offered to its proteges. In
view of the organized white slave traf
fic, this last measure is necessary, says
Mrs. Butler, matron at the Paris head
quarters. "We do not keep girls who
have been led astray, though, of course,
we receive them temporarily and send
them on to England," she added.
