The Fourteenth Census.
For the fourteenth time Uncle Sam
is now counting his-people. He is do
ing it systemstically, on a big scale,
having prepared for months in ad
vance for this decennial enumeration.
The primary purpose of the census is
to ascertain the population in order
to determine the size of the House of
Representatives, under the constitu
tional provision that "representatives
and direct taxes shall be apportioned
among the several states which may
be included within this Union accord
ing to their respective numbers."
For a long period after the first
census these decennial enumerations
were conducted strietly for this pur
pose of determining the ratio of rep
resentatives in Congress, but eventual
ly advantage was taken of the oppor
tunity to utilize the survey for the
development of other statistics. In
recent years the census has been a
most valuable performance, eliciting
faets and figures regarding national
conditions. The population feature
of the census, though still the primary
object, is accompanied by an exten
sive survey of social, industrial, agri
cultural and economic conditions.
From the census bureau's returns and
compilations and bulletins the people
derive much valuable information.
Business is guided, research is aided
and in general the censns work per
mits the people of the country to
know precisely the conditions in
which they live, the trend of move
giants and the rate of development.
The first census disclosed a national
population of 3,928,214. That waa in
1790. The subsequent decennial fig
ure* show the growth of the country
graphically. In 1910 the enumeration
disclosed 91,962,266 people in conti
nental United States. Subsequent es
timates based on carefully devised
formulas have brought the total up
to 105.253,300 in 1918. It is believed
that the fourteenth eensus will show
continental population close upon
110,000,000.
Between national counts cities, with
an eye to their needs and ambitions,
conduet their own enumerations, and
there is, as a rule, dissatisfaction with
the federal census returns, which are
normally below the local counts. From
now nntil the announcement of the
population reports by the census bu
reau there will be keen interest. Cer
tain cities are hot rivals for su
premacy. For instance, Cleveland
and Detroit, at present relatively
sixth and seventh in rank in the
United States, according to the
census estimates of 1918, are now

1900 census, and Detroit has gone for
ward since the 1910 count. Detroit's

responsible administrative official. It

the workers who have grown old in
the government's employ.
No matter what the reclassification
commission may report in the way of

must be some form of retirement to
prevent congestion in the service.
The scale of pay may not be per
fectly worked out for action this
year, though that is highly desirable.
But the retirement bill ean be
enacted into law independently of
the readjustment of salaries. It
should not be held for consideration

legislation, the commission proposes

prove.
Retirement of the civilian em
ployes of the government will not

now proposed upon a cost-division
basis, with the clerks themselves ul
timately carrying the major part of
the expense. Actually, in respeet to
practical economy, whatever the gov
ernment contributes toward retire

It is stated that those in charge
of the bill in the two. houses expect
to see it made part of the stated leg
islative program. This is important.

its chances on the calendar or to
come into competition with other
measures that may for the moment
appeal more stTongly to members for
time. The session will be crowded
with matters of urgency, and unless

gram that this meritorious and now

I Perhaps the only traffic dog in
Washington is to be seen of sun
shiny afternoons at 18th street and
Columbia road, taking instructions
from a real "traffic cop" located there.
This traffic dog: is a big, lanky Aire
dale belonging to a householder up
the block a way. The big dog seems
to have taken quite a fancy to the
policeman, after the manner of dogs,
and comes out to assist him whenever
he can.
The policeman has a little wooden
stand, and on this he and his friend
the dog stand. The cop directs the
traffic, of course, but the dog takes
great interest in the proceedings,
standing faithfully by his friend's
side for hours at a time, scarcely
moving.
The Airedale has the making of a
great traffic policeman, his friend the
real policeman, says. The dog is
fearless, and often manifests this
.trait by standing out over the car
track until pulled in by his friend in
blue. Often the hand yanks him onto
the stand just in time to miss an on
coming street car.
