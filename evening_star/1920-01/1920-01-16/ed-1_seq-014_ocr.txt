Decisions to Come in Ohio
and Rhode Island
Situations.
Although the eighteenth amend
ment to the Constitution of the United
States, prohibiting manufacture, sale.
transportation, importation or expor
tation of intoxicating liquors within,
i into or from the United States or any
J of its possessions becomes effective
i at the stroke of 12 o'clock tonight, the
I question of constitutionality of fed
oral prohibition will not be officially
I decided until the Supreme Court of
the United States has passed on the
last case before it touching' on the
j question.
i Thus, though the prohibition amend
ment goes into effect at midnight to
night, and the prohibition enforce
ment law begins to function one min
ute thereafter, should the Supreme
Court uphold the contention of the
state of Khode Island, or, failing that,
should the tfibunal affirm the finding
of the Ohio supreme court as to le
gality of that state's ratification of
the amendment, the entire prohibition
program would be upset, and a more
or less prolonged "wet" season would
result.
Khode Island** Contention.
The contention of the state of Rhode
Island, in effect, is that the prohibition
amendment is outside the realm of con
stitutional changes that are permis
sible under our form of government,
and. therefore, that the amendment as
well as the prohibition enforcement
law is null and void.
While the decree of the Supreme
Court would relate merely to the state
of Rhode Island, since a constitutional
amendment is involved the effect
would be to lift the ban wherever state
prohibition laws are not in force.
The eighteenth amendment will de-
stroy the authority of states, Rhode
Island holds, and the Union being
found indissoluble in 1805. she now
wishes to know if the states are inde
structible.
Situation In Ohio.
If the decision of the Supreme Court
of the United States is against the con
tentions of Rhode Island, the tribunal
will be called on to determine whether
or not the people of Ohio have the
right to establish the method whereby
constitutional amendments shall be rati
fied in that state.
The constitution of Ohio provides for
ratification by a referendum of the
qualified voters of the state, and the
Ohio supreme court has upheld the
legality and constitutionality of that
provision. The prohibition amendment,
however, was ratified by the state leg
islature. and the ratification later was
repudiated by a referendum of the
voters.
Should the Supreme Court of the
United States uphold the Ohio supreme
court in its decision the legislative rati
fication will be nullified .and Ohio will
r.ecessarily be removed from the list of
thirty-six states ratifying, upon which
was based the proclamation of the Sec
retary of State fixing January 16. 1920,
as the effective date of the amendment.
Would Open Question of Date.
This would open the question as to
the date of ratification, and the neces
sity of the Secretary of State issuing
another proclamation that would de
lay the operation until January 16.
1921, or indefinitely. The prohibition
ists declare that a proclamation is un
necessary, and that the amendment
becomes effective one year from the
day on which the required three
fourths of the states ratified.
Rhode Island having refused to
ratify the amendment, eleven states
must override the action of their
legislatures by a referendum vote.
This can only be done if the Supreme
Court sustains the Ohio ease. Then the
fifteen states whose constitutions pro
vide for a referendum can submit the
question to the people for a final de
cision.
A majority of the people of New
Jersey registered their unmistakable
disapproval of prohibition by the elec
tion of Gov. Edwards, a super wet
candidate. Gov. Smith of New York
has asked the legislature of that state
to revoke the action of ratification.
Govs. Ritchie of Maryland and Davis of
Virginia have both recently shown
some concern over national prohibi
tion. and many other governors are
expected to follow.

WATERPOWER BILL
PASSED BY SENATE
Contains Amendment Author
izing $25,000,000 Great
Falls Plant for D. C. Use.
Ending: a ten-year fight, the Fen
ate yesterday passed the waterpower
bill, which now goes to conference
for the composing1 of differences be
tween the House and Senate. Final
enactment of the measure within a
few weeks is expected.
The vote was 52 to 18, and was
taken after the Senate- had recon
sidered its former action and re
stored, 44 to 22. an amendment which
would limit the government license
on development projects to 2o cents
per horsepower.
The bill as finally passed by the
Senate contains the Norris amend
ment authorizing the establishment of
a waterpower plant at Great Falls
to supply Washington with light and
power, at a cost of $25,000,000.
U. S. Waterpower Commission.
The bill provides for creation of a
federal waterpower commission, com
posed of the Secretaries of War,
Interior and Agriculture, which
would be authorized, after investi
gation, to issue licenses for de
velopment of waterpower projects*
"for a reasonable annual charge."
The licenses would run for fifty
yea rs.
The measure further provides that
the federal commission shall co-oper
ate with the states and other federal
agencies; that there shall be no
charge on state and municipal power
projects, and that industrial plants
developing less than 200 horsepower
shall not be required to pay a license.
On two years' notice in writing the
United States would have the right,
at the expiration of a license, to take
over and operate plants.
Present Plants Exempted.
Power plants now in operation
would not come under provisions of
the bill.
At the expiration of the forty-year
license period <he government would
have three alternatives in dealing
with all licensed projects, take over
the plants, issue a new license, or
have the original licenses continue in
operation.
