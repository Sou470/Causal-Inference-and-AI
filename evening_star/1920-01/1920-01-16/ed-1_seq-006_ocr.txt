The Senate Minority.
The democrats of the Senate are
evenly divided on the question of
their leadership. Two capable men
aspire to the place. Either is equal
to its duties. One is a southern, the
other a western, man, but sectional
ism is not involved in the contest. Mr.
Hitchcock has support in the south,
and Mr. Underwood in the tvest.
Neither is the peace treaty involved.
Whatever the issue of the contest, Mr.
Hitchcock will continue to lead the
democrats in the matter of the pact.
For two reasons the minority leader
ship of the Senate is a very impor
tant assignment at this time. (1) The
chamber is almost evenly divided po
litically; and (2) the congressional
card is crowded and difficult. To be
effective, therefore, the minority must
be well shepherded. The members
must be kept together on the various
propositions as they are presented.
Politics is now in full swing. The
war is over. Reconstruction is here.
A presidential campaign is in the
initial stages. Everything indicates a
struggle unsurpassed, if erer equaled,
for strenuositv. Preparations by both
sides promise the employment of every
legitimate agency of action.
The legislation of the present ses-1
sion of Congress may decide the com
ing election. Consider for a moment
what is expected of that body. Action
as to the railroads. Action as to a
merchant marine. An Army measure
and a Navy measure, and both of lib
eral scope. Rome adjustments as to
the tariff. Some changes in other
forms of taxation. And an economy
which, while reducing expenses, shall
not deny support to any object worthy
of support.
The minority has its responsibility
here. Mere objection, mere criticism,
will not suffice. It must indicate by
propositions of its own what it thinks
should be done, and thus go on record
itself and put the majority on record
outside of the majority line of action.
The majority controls by only two
votes, afld one of them is challenged.
It, too, must watch its step. It most
walk warily. Its leader in point of
service is the oldest member of the
chamber, and one of his party's ablest
legislators.
Stacked up thus against something
formidable, the Senate minority will
need to be handled with all skill. In
the presence of the enemy it cannot
afford divisions or bickerings. It
must have a policy and adhere to it.
It must have a leader and follow him.
The existing deadlock will soon be
broken; and when the battle between
the two parties begins, with the presi
dency and control of the next Con
gress the stake, the Senate is likely
to be the arena of many stirring and
stubborn scenes.
