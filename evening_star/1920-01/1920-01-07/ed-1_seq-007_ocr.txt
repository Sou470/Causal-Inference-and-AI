APPEAL IS MADE FOR
NAVY YARD WORKERS
Disturbance in Employment in Lo
cal Establishment Unlikely,
Conference Shows.
The 6,000 to 7.000 employes of the
Washington navy yard, one-half of
whom are expert machinists, are in
less danger of being disturbed in their
employment than are the employes
of other navy yards. This was shown
today when N. P. Alifas. representing
District No. 44. International Associa
tion of Machinists, was in conference
with members of the House appro
priations committee in behalf of navy
yard workers. These machinists are
fearful that unless the deficiency ap
propriation of $10,314,000. which has
been asked by the Navy Department
is carried in the deficiency appropria
tion bill, there will be a serious cut
ting down of the forces in the various
navy yards.
Last fall the Navy Department tried
to get an appropriation of $18,600,000
for the navy yards. This was reduced
in the House to $9,300,000, but the
Senate struck it out and the House
conferees agreed to the elimination.
The Navy Department contends
that this fund iB needed in order to
keep the fleet in good repair.
The position of the navy yard work
ers represented by Mr. Alifas ie that
they do not ask the government for
any appropriation for needless work,
but wince the Navy Department says
the work must be done, they want
Congress to make it possible for this
work to be done continuously rather
than to lay off a number of men now
and employ a larger number later on.
They argue for continuous employ
ment and not for serious turnovers in
the employment at the various yards.
Some of the appropriation asked
will go to the Washington navy yard
for routine work, J^ut the larger ap
propriation for construction work in
the local yard was carried In the
naval appropriation bill.

ANTWERP. Sunday. January 4.?The
sum of $760,000,000 was released from
the sale of American Army stocks
remaining in France after the de
parture of the American Army, said
Brig. Gen. W. P. Connor, hief of staff
of the American department of sup
ply, who sailed today for America.
Ho estimated the value of the stocks
at $1,700,000,000 and dec.ared it would
have cost $75,000,000 to take them
back to America.
Had they been retailed in various
countries great losses would have
been entailed, as the expense would
have been heavy, he said. France
paid $400,000,000 for stocks it took
over, while other allies and small
er nations purchased supplies for
$360,000,000.
As an offset against the stocks
bought by France, that country un
dertook to pay damage claims amount
ing to several million dollars as a
result of American operations In
training areas. When the American
Army went to France it was agreed
that farms and buildings used by it
in training would be left in the same
condition as found. Miles of trenches
were dug and buildings were de
molished and American forces were
rushed into Germany before the land
could be restored to its former con
dition.
Gen. Connor again denied the old
story that the French made the
American army pay for the trenches
it occupied in France.
"The whole truth," he said, "is
that the American Army has not
paid a cent for any ground used or
for anything destroyed at the front."
Gen. Connor sailed on the Lapland,
which was the first vessel to clear
from Antwerp since August, l'J14.
There were 460 passengers, including
Henry D. Morgan, American consul
general at Brussels, and Mrs. Mor
gan. Just before Mr. Morgan left the
qapital he was received in special
audience by King Albert.
ASKED TO JOIN PARLEY j
AS TO BETTER SCHOOLS
Heads of Citizens' Associations and
School Committees Invited to
Meeting Saturday Night.
The presidents and school commit
tees of all the citizens' associations
were invited today to attend a con
ference in the boardroom of the Dis
trict building Saturday night, at
which it is expected the organized
citizens, numbering about 20.000, will
actively get behind the movement
to improve conditions in the local
public schools.
The call was sent out by- Jesse C.
Suter. chairman of the committee on
[education of the Federation of Citi
zens' Associations, which is composed
of delegates from thirty-five of the
associations.
The last meeting of the federation
was devoted almost entirely to dis
cussion of the question of whether
the federation should send delegates
to represent the affiliated associations
on the citizens' school betterment
committee.
Those who believed each associa
tion should name its own representa
tives on the school betterment com
mittee prevented the adoption of a
resolution to have the federation rep
resent the associations. It was made
clear at the meeting that none of
the delegates opposed the school bet
terment movement, but that it was a
question merely of whether the fed
eration should name one group of
delegates for all the associations or
whether each association should
name representatives.
NEW METHOD IS ADOPTED
OF TABULATING PRICES
Dr. Meeker Seeks to Obviate
Month's Delay, Thereby Aiding
in Lowering Food Costs.
In order that the buying public may
be supplied in the quickest manner
possible with accurate and authentic
indications of the trend in food
prices throughout the central west,
the Atlantic seaboard, and particu
larly in Washington, Royal Meeker,
commissioner of labor statistics, has j
inaugurated a new method of obtain-
ing and issuing digests of food prices,
which will bring the information
strictly up to date.
Dr. Meeker will send out at the
end of the current week letters ask
ing for information on the trend of
food prices, to be sent to him not
later than January 15, showing prices
during the early part of January.
He says preliminary and probably of
ficial tabulation of these figures will
be made by the 19th or 20th of the
month, and on that date the public
will be informed of prices in the
early part of January. Thus the usual
delay of a month will be obviated
and prices will be available during
the month in which they are made.
Dr. Meeker believes that prompt pub
lication o f these lists of retail food
prices will be a powerful deterrent
to any boosts in price, and said that
the value of these current lists can
not be overestimated.
Washington dealers, he said, are
usually the ones who hold up pub
lished lists of food prices for all
cities, but with the sending out of
an urgent request for immediate in
formation he expects that these re
calcitrant dealers In Washington will
return the information promptly.
