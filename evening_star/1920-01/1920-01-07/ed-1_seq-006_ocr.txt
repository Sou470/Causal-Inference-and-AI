Thus the ease is stated: There should

contribution by the government; the
citizens of the District are now taxed

of other comparable cities; the
amount of revenue raised in District
taxation doubled by federal contribu
tions is now and will for some years
be needed to the full to meet the
District's requirements. Xo clearer or
more convincing reasons against the
enactment of the pending bill can be

Again the Watchword.
In the House yesterday Leader Mon
dell referred again to the question of
economy. The occasion itas the tak
ing up of one of the regular supply
bills. It may be, too, that he will
repeat the performance at the taking
up of each of the bills making pro

Such a course would follow a fa

Extravagance must be destroyed!
Sure it must; and attention cannot
be called too frequently to the ne
cessity.
The situation admits of no denial
or evasion. It likewise bears upon
both parties. It grows out of the
ivar, and both parties are responsible
for the war. They united in declar
ing the war, and in voting the money
for the prosecution of hostilities.
The sums required were very large.
War is expensive. There was aome
wtstc, for waste inheres in war. When
everything is moving at a gallop, per
fect order may not be expected.
Prudent outlays and up-to-date audit
ings are out of the question.
But the war is over, and the nrge
now is for a return to normal. Not
return to pre-war figures. They
g^nre gone forever. Our bills hence
forth are going to be very large. The
people know (tin, aad will pay.
Normal means a careful inspection
of all estimates, and careful allot

poses. We are not proceeding at a
gallop now. We have slowed down;
and there is time for eare and pru
dence.
Mr. Mondell speaks because he k
the majority leader, and the majority
is held responsible far legislation.
But his message is for both sides of
the chamber. As the two parties acted
together in increasing the supplies
for war purposes, they should co
operate now in reducing them for
peace purposes.
This question of economy is a live
one at present. Taxes are falling due
again, and the bills are large. You
cannot look the tax collector in the
face and say, "Tomorrow." The time
for the presentation of your bill is
appointed; and when the money is
due, it is due, and must be paid.
There is less discontent, however,
when a feeling prevails that the as
sessments represent a definite aim to
keep all expenses within good and rea
sonable bounds; that, as far as may
be. extravagance has been cut out;

money; that, in a word, revenue is
being raised and expended strictly

benefit.
