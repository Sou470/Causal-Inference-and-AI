Washington during 1919 went through a gradual process of
settling down to a peace basis alter two years of war-time excite
ment.
Although the signing of the armistice resulted in the departure
of hundreds of government employes and temporary officials irom
the city early in 1919, the exodus was not as rapid as had been j
expected.
The police in November took the biennial census, which dis
closed a population at the present time ol 455,428. The previous

crease of 59,481 for the two years. Of this increase 47,467 werc

NATIONAL REPRESENTATION SOUGHT.
With the war over, the civic organizations of the city were
more active during the past year than they had been since 1917.
Early in the year the Board of Trade, Chamber of Commerce and
Federation of Citizens' Associations took steps to revive interest
in the campaign to obtain representation for the people of the
District, both in Congress and in the electoral college.
Merger of the Washington Railway and Electric and Capital
Traction companies also was a live topic before the trade and
civic organizations throughout the year.
The Public Utilities Commission in September completed the
valuations of the two street railways after five years of investiga
tion. The commission placed a valuation of $16,106,368.14 oh the
property of the Washington Railway and Electric Company and
$14,270,495.51 on the Capital Traction Company.
TEN THOUSAND CITIZENS JOIN ASSOCIATIONS.
In the last two months of the year the citizens' associations
united in a membership drive which more than doubled their
membership. They started the drive in November with an esti
mated combined strength of 8,000. It is estimated that more than
10,000 new members were obtained.
Another event of city-wide interest was the nomination, in
December, of Dr. John Van Schaick, jr., president of the board of
education, to succeed W. Gwynn Gardiner on the board oif Com
ihissioners. Dr. Van Schaick is awaiting confirmation by the
Senate.
Although housing congestion was not nearly so acute in Wash
ington during 1919 as during the war, building projects were not
undertaken to an extent sufficient to meet the demand for houses.
