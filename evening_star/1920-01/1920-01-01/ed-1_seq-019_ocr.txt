A substantial salary Increase re
ceived by employes of the police de

year. The increase followed a stren
uous campaign for better salaries,
waged by Commissioner Brownlow,
Maj. Raymond W. Pullman and mem
bers of the department.
In a review of the work of the de
partment for the year Maj. Pullman
commended those who remained on
duty during the war period.
Maj. Pullman called attention to
the benefits of the prohibition law
and pointed out that there has been
a decided decrease in the number of
arrests for intoxication, the total
number the past year being 4,910,
while for the fiscal year 1917, the
last year the city had saloons, the
number was 9,648.
Increase in Crime.
Reports of crime last year were
more numerous than the preceding
year, officials of the department
stated, pointing to numerous hold
ups, house and store robberies, mur
ders and assaults committed. Several
serious assaults on women occurred.
Forty-nine homicides claimed the
attention of the police, one of the.
most difficult cases to be investi
gated being that of the slaying of the
three members of the Chinese educa
tional mission. Another involved the
killing of a woman war worker, and
Henry Jackson, colored, who took her
life, is in Jail, awaiting execution. Sev
eral of the forty-nine alleged murderers
are at large.
Police officials are deeply interested
in the search that is being made for
Herbert Copeland, colored, slayer of
Lieut. David T. Dunigan and Police
man John Conrad and Deputy Sheriff
McParland of Charles county. Cope
land escaped from the District jail.
A race riot the latter part of July
was the most serious incident of the
year with which the police had to
contend. It became necessary for
Maj. Pullman to summon members of
the military forces and Home De
fense League to assist in restoring
order. Seven lives were lost, De
tective Harry Wilson and six civil
ians being included in the list. Sev
eral other persons were wounded.
One of the most sensational events
in the year's police history was the
attempt on the life of Attorney Gen
eral Palmer. The would-be assassin
was blown to pieces when he fell and
exploded the bomb just before the
house of Mr. Palmer was reached.
Only damage to the house and death
to the anarchist resulted.

Wallace Mulcare, a 14th street merchant,

The murder in September of Emmett
E. Wood, whose body was found in the

tective Patrick O'Brien resulted in un

Because of the number of instances
in which weapons were used Maj. Pull
man renewed his recommendation for

arms.

THE COURTS.
litigation in Washington in 1919
showed a decided advance over previous
years. A total of 2.224 suits was insti
tuted in the office of John R. Young,
clerk of the District Supreme Court,
during tho past year. On the equity
side, which includes divorces, 1,010 cases
were started, while 1,214 suits at law
for damages and accounts were insti
tuted.
In the preceding year only 1.856 suits
were filed, of which 1,058 were law
cases and 798 in equity.
The already crowded condition of the
dockets has been enhanced by the in
creased litigation, despite the efforts of
the justices to dispose of cases, and
unless additional justices are provided
the outlook for catching up with tile
work of the courts is regarded as dis
couraging.
