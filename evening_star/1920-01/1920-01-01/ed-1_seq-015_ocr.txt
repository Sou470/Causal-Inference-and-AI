Faith, Hope and Charity,
Mr. Marshall's Prayer
BV THOMAS K. MARSHA 1.1..
There seem to be recurrent periods in the history of the
republic when the truths of Holy Writ are the only anchor
to windward that enables the ship of state to outride the
storm.
I see Washinton and his^ foot-sore soldiery, shivering
amid the wintry blasts at Valley Forge, and in the whistling
wind they hear the hiss of treasonable royalists. It is an
hour of agony. It must be that the ragged Continentals rose
triumphant from their doubt and despair because they felt
that there yet abided faith, hope and chSrity?faith that the
great body of the people believed in them and in the justice
of their cause, hope that the God of Nations would yet bring
triumph to their arms, and charity for their fellow men,
who as yet only dimly saw the great issues involved in the
conflict.
Again. I see a Kentunckian. tall and gaunt, in whose
veins I have oftimes wondered there was not the very ichor
of the gods, agonizing as the first gun boomed at Gettys
burg, and with it he heard the roar of all who stood for
precedent and privilege in the face of progress. And his soul
would have been beaten into the dust if there had not come
to him the thought that there yet abided faith, hope and
charity?faith in the ultimate destiny of reunited states,
hope that northern thought might all be national, and char
ity for the warring sons of the south, who looked back, not
forward.
Again in the republic men arc agonizing over the pas
sion and prejudice of both real and seeming injustice and
inequality, and the blackness of despair would settle over
our land were it not that faith, hope and charity still abide?
faith that a deeper knowledge of the wisdom of our institu
tions will be imparted to every son and daughter of the re
public, hope that more and more all men will turn from the
contemplation of their rights to a consideration of their duty,
and charity for all who are not vicious, but who, through
stress of circumstance, have become embittered.
God of our Fathers, take from us, if Thou wilt, material
prosperity and national glory, but give us, individually and
collectively, for all the years to come, faith, hope and charity.
