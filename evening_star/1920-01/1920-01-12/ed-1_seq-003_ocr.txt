home lands and $2,072,917.21 was con
tributed for foreign missionary en
deavor. For other purposes $981,
484.was contributed.
The Adventists are now operating
117 mission stations in ninety-four
different languages, according to the
report, with a force of 10,133 mission
and institutional workers.
Their total investment in buildings
is $17,500,000. Denominational liter
ature in the form of books, magazines,
pamphlets and tracts valued at $3,
416,000 was circulated, S53 persons
being employed in the publishing
houses alone, while nearly 3,')00 others
were kept busy circulating it.
In the report it is also stated that
the denomination is operating 848
primary schools, with an enrollment
of 18,105 pupils, and seventy-seven
colleges and academies, with an en
rollment of 9,908, the larger majority
of these training to becoftie active
and qualified missionaries or workers
in different institutions.
The church is also operating forty
six sanitariums and treatment rooms,
ministering to physical wants as well
as the spiritual needs, and employing
2,065 physicians, nurses and other
helpers. These institutions have as
sets totaling $3,225,110.25.
Mr. Rogers says the denomination is
progressing in a most encouraging
manner along all lines.

ON sue ACT BEGUN
Pittman Bill and Purpose Ex
plained by Representative Mc
Fadden?Eise in White Metal.
Hearings were started today by tha
House committee on banking and cur
rency on the bill introduced by Rep
resentative L. T. McFadden of Penn
sylvania to amend the Pittman silver
act of April 23, 1918, and to reduce the
standard of subsidiary silver coins
from 90 to SO per cent.
Mr. McFadden, -who is a member of
the committee, explained the Pittman
act and the purpose of his bill.
He said that act authorized the Sec
retary of the Treasury to melt or
break up 300,000,000 standard silver
dollars, sell the product of bullion
and repurchase a like amount of sil
ver bullion at $1 an ounce. "My bill,"
declared Mr. McFadden, "amends the
Pittman act by requiring that all our
standard silver dollars shall be melted
and sold as bullion, and repeals the re
purchase clause.
"The phenomenal rise in the price
of silver from 48% cents an ounce in
1915 to $1.37% in 1919. has caused our
standard silver dollar, worth only 37
cents in 1915, to be worth $1.06^4 in
gold In 1919, and the silver dollar, be
ing worth more as bullion than as
coin, has driven itself out of circula
tion by the operation of the inexor
able law announced by Sir Thomas
Gresham. the financial adviser of
Queen Elizabeth. Gresham announced
that "money of less value drives out
money of more value." This law ap
plies to the ?bimetallic' or so-called
double standard of value an well as to
worn coins. As our gold dollar, at the
coinage ratio of 1 to 16 with silver,
is now less valuable than the stand
ard silver dollar, the cheaper gold
dollar has driven the more valuable
silver dollar out of circulation. So
here Is a twentieth century proof of
the fallacy of "bimetalism."
"We had the very same experience
in 1852, following the discovery of
gold in California, when all our sil
ver change money was melted up and
the public had to use postage stamps
for change. At that time our subsid
iary sliver coins?dimes, quarters and
halves?were the aliquot part of
the standard silver dollar. Congress
relieved that situation by enacting on
February 21, 1S53, our present sub
sidiary silver coinage law. which re
duced their weight by 7 per cent and
sustained their parity with cold by
government credit. When silver is
worth $1,29 an ounce it is profitable
to silversmiths and silver exporters
to melt full-weight standard silver
dollars, and when silver is worth
$1.38 an ounce it is profitable to them
to melt our full-weight subsldiary
silver coins, of which we have more
than two hundred million dollars out.
To prevent the inconvenience experi
enced by us in 1852, when we lost all
our change money, I had the bill pro
vide that all future coinage of sub
sidiary silver pieces should be on an
<*0 instead of 90 per cent basis. The
Holland government has already pro
posed a bill to recoin their siK'er on
an SO per cent basis, and Canada con
templates similar action.
"In the debate in the Senate on the
Pittman bill, it was shown that sil
ver. under ordinary labor conditions,
could be produced at a profit of 50"
cents an ounce; yet the Pittman act
requires the government to repur
chase a like amount of silver at $1
an ounce. It is my purpose to repeal
this vicious clause of the Pittman
act."
