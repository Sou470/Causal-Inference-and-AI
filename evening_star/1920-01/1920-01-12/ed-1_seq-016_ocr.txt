"Would you mind telling me how to
distinguish between the different'
periods in furniture?" writes a woman
reader. "I don't want a 'period room,'
and I couldn't afford it if I did, prob- j
ably; but I do like to pick up odd
chairs, settees and so on, in the an-
tique shops now and then, and I want j
to know what I'm getting." j
She is not the first woman who has'
thought that all old mahogany chairs
were Chippendale. The writer even
heard once of one woman who asked
a dealer in antiques if "Chippendale" I
was a kind of finish for wood. He
probed a little and found she was j
thinking of veneer, and that she I
thought Chippendal. was made of
chips of wood pasted together.
j Well, let's learn what Chippendale is

dale," for he stands head and shoulders

country.

pendale furniture is its solidness. It is
