Two Active Citizens.
Mr. Taft and Mr. Bryan are fre
quent visitors to Washington, and
both are very welcome. They deliver
addresses here. They confer with
senators and representatives about
legislation. They meet many old ac
quaintance* and exchange views about
affairs in general.
This is to the good for them and
for the country. They have had to
do with much important public busi
ness. Their experience has taught
them much, and gives to their sug
gestions interest and value.
Both these men have been in the
limelight a long time. Both are well
considered even by those who differ
?with them. Both are accredited with
patriotic motives in all they do or
advise.
Mr. Tat't was educated for the bar,
and at the start had no aspirations
or purposes outside the line of his
profession. He was a local judge at
an early age. He was solicitor gen
eral of the United States at an early
age. He then returned to the bench,
and was serving there with much com
fort to himself when President Mc
Kinlev drafted him for politics; and
he has been in polities ever since,
reaching the topmost rung of the
ladder.
Mr. Bryan also was educated for the
bar, and opened a law office. But the
bar did not satisfy. Being what is
called a born politician, he soon for
sook the law for politics, and has
never returned to the law. The ora
tory in his system soon brought him
into notice, and, later, to distinction.
Although defeated three times in bis
efforts to reach the White House, he
is still suspected of a longing for
that domicile, and of an intention to
make a fourth effort this year to
reaeh it.
These men have not only the dis
position but the time to consider pub
lic questions. While both are active
in affairs, neither is chained to a
desk or to a routine. They move
about the country at their pleasure,
and as requested by those who value
their advice.
In this way both Mr. Taft and Mr.
Bryan keep well informed about pub
lie sentiment. They are seldom in
agreement. They belong to different
parties and different schools of
thought. Mr. Taft is a conservative,
while Mr. Bryan is a radical. But
they are students of affairs at all
times.
In these times, so fraught with
changes and the activities that go with
changes, these men find much to in
terest them, and they are thoroughly
alive to what is going on. And in
keeping close track of what is on the
card, and in manifesting lively con
cern about what is going on, they
set an excellent example to all who,
like themselves, have a citizen's stake
in the game.

Grip or Influenza
It is the belief of many physicians
in this city that a large percentage of
the cases reported to the health office
as influenza are in fact merely cases
of grip, a painful and sometimes dan
gerous affliction, but by no means so
greatly to be dreaded as the newest
form of epidemic malady that raged
so furiously and with such deadly ef
fect during the autumn of 191S. Th>>
symptoms of the two are somewhat
similar, but careful diagnosis hill de
termine the difference. Both of these
diseases affect the pulmonary organs.
Both of them have a tendency to "run
into'' pneumonia, which takes a heavy
toll of life. But the influenza is dis
tinct in its origin and in its develop
ment. It is much more speedy in
progression.
There are today many cases of both
grip and influenza here, but it may be
doubted if the latter disease predom
inates in this class of ailments. The
grip is debilitating and painful and
hard to conquer. It leaves trouble
some after effects. It has, however, a
record of a very low rate of mortality
as compared with the influenza, perhaps
for the reason that the latter is so
new in its development that medical
science has not yet been able to f:ud
the effective remedies.
Whether suffering from grip or in
fluenza, however, Washington is to
day a "sick town," as are many others.
The physicians are overworked. There
is a scarcity of nurses. The hospitals
are filled and facilities for caring for
the sick are scant. In these circum
stances it is necessary for all who are
well to bear up courageously, to keep
out of the range of possible infectioh,
to be attentive to the essentials of
ventilation and sanitation, to take
notice of early symptoms of disorder
and seek prompt treatment. Above
all, it is well to avoid nostrums, that
may bring about the very ailments
they are supposed to cure, by causing
unsettlement of the digestive organs
and so exposing the system to attack.
Conditions are so much better than
they were fifteen months ago that
there is no occasion for alarm. If
disease develops it can be treated now
as it could toot have been treated
then, even with a shortage of nurses
and hospital accommodations. The
government is about to spend half a
million dollars in a nation-wide fight
against influenza, and this effort is
certain to have its good effect to les
sen the ravages of this mysterious
disease.

been lost in it warrants its classifi

limb. There may be places where, so
