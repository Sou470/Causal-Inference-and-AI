STECHER AND CADDOCK
MEET ON MA T FRIDA Y
IN CHAMPIONSHIP BOUT
Winner of Match Between Ex-Service Men in
New York Will Attain Undisputed
Possession of World Title.
By DENMAN THOMPSON.
That there is a genuine and country-wide interest in wrestling,
despite the disrepute into which the game has fallen because of the
unscrupulous tactics of taking performers and grasping promoters, is
proved by the general attention attracted by the coming contest be
tween Earl Caddock and Joe Steelier. Nearly every match held, 110
matter where or who the contestants are, is billed as having some
title or other at stake, and the public has long since ceased to take
seriously the extravagant claims of irresponsible managers in that
regard, but there can be 110 dispute on the point that when Stecher
and Caddock step into the ring at Madison Square Garden in New
York next Friday night America's leading grappler will be determined,
and that the winner will be acknowled a worthy successor to the late
Frank Gotch.
This Caddock-Stecher meeting will be a world championship
Ifnatch in the full meaning of the term, and with no possibility of a
charge of slackerism to tarnish the crown. Caddock was undisputed
champion until he entered the Army and his retirement was announced.
Stecher, also in the service, although the naval unit to which he was
attached did not get into action in foreign waters, in Caddock's ab
sence from the mat earned the right to dispute his claim to highest
honors by an unbroken string of victories over the most formidable
opponents available, including W'ladek Zbyszko and "Strangler" Lewis.
$40,000 PURSE FOR CONTESTANTS.
In other aspects the match is of championship caliber, too, the
size of the purse offered being one. Incidentally, $30,000 is named as
the price given for the motion picture rights. Promoter Jack Curley,
who had to outbid western promoters to land it, has estimated that it
will cost him upward of $50,000 to put the show on, of which $40,000
is announced as the westlers' end.
Curley in the last ten years has managed half a dozen of the most
important heavyweight matches and established the present high-water
mark in wrestling finance in the bout between Frank Gotch and George
Hackenschmidt at Chicago. The "gate" for the meeting between the
American and European champions, which was held at the White Sox
ball park, was $87,000. t
it is doubtful that the gross receipts for next Friday's event in
New York will equal that huge sum, even with ringside seats at $20
each, but it may closely approach that mark, and it is practically as
sured they will exceed the second highest figure of $40,000 paid by
spectators to see the match between Gotch and Mahmout, the Turk,
at the Dexter pavilion in Chicago, which also was managed by Curley.
Under the "one-fall'' rule that now prevails in wrestling, fans who
pay their good money to see the championship decided cannot be dis
appointed in that respect, for the match must continue until the shoul
ders of one or the other are pinned to the mat. The bout ends then.
Criticism has been made that the new rule does not put a premium
on ruggedness and endurance: that if a grappler. by the exercise of
skill, manages to obtain a fall in the first few minutes it counts for
just as much as if he succeeds after a couple of hours of strenuous
effort. This is true, but wrestling is as much a game of skill as strength
or endurance, and cleverness merits reward.
DECISION RULE PROVED UNSATISFACTORY.
The old plan of arranging matches for the best two falls in three
sometimes resulted in two evenly matched men wearing out the on
lookers by struggling until daylight. Dissatisfaction with the "best
two-out-of-three-falls" plan resulted in the innovation of a decision
by the referee on points at the end of a time limit of two and one
half hours. This naturally resulted in a lot of tiresome stalling, with
points rather than fall sought. The one-fall rule is the outcome, and it
seems to have been accepted by mat enthusiasts as the best solution.
Whether .Stecher or Caddock will emerge victor Friday night, time
alone can tell, although the former is due to cop if the rule against
"comebacks" holds good. On the occasion of their previous meeting
in the spring of 1917. the men were very evenly matched. Both ob
tained falls. Stecher first and then Caddock, after more than an hour
of grappling in each instance, Caddock being declared the victor when
Stecher refused to appear for the third and deciding fall. Stecher, in
the meantime, has remained active in the game, and has obtained a
wealth of experience, while Caddock. who was gassed overseas and re
turned to this country some twenty pounds under his normal top
weight of 188 pounds, had to work strenuously in the hills of Montana
to regain his health.
Caddock's chances of victory depend largely upon whether he has
been able to recover his speed and stamina, for although he is the
smallest of the restricted group who have dominated the heavyweights
in recent years, none ever was better versed in the tricks of the game.
He is referred to as the "wrestler with a thousand holds," but, like all
the others, has a favorite grip?the head-scissors.
BODY-SCISSORS STECHER'S SPECIALTY.
Stecher sprang into prominence almost solely through the use of
the deadly body-scissors. 'rhree years ago this was practically the only
good method of offense Stecher possessed. Caddock then demon
strated his ability to repeatedly break ;his hold when half applied, and
others since by concentrating their efforts on evading the grip of
Stecher's steellike limbs have convinced him of the necessity of en
larging his repertoire. As a result, he has perfected himself in the
application of several other grips, including the arm lock, with which
he recently vanquished Ed Lewis. Caddock will face a much improved
Stealer when they clamber between the ropes this week, and will
have to be at the top of his form to retain the crown he now right
fully claims.
Caddock will be dwarfed by Stecher in size. The latter reaches
almost six-feet-two in height and wrestles at 205 pounds. Caddock
stands five-feet-eleven, and will train down to enter the ring at 180
pounds, which will give the challenger an advantage of twenty-five
pounds in weight. The ex-Army sergeant will not be daunted by this
fact, however. Three years back, when he defeated Stecher and
Wladek Zbyszko, he weighed only 176 pounds. Zbyszko tips the
beam at 235.
