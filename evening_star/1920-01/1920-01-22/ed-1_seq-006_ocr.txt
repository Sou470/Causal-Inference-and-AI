Schoolroom Costumes.
A plea for a regulation designed
to simplify the dresses of the girl
students in the local public schools
has been presented to the board of
education by representatives of an
organization of women of the Dis
trict and careful consideration has
been assured. It is proposed'to adopt
what may l?e termed a uniform as
the standard garb for the girls. Some
opposition may be expected to this!
proposal, but it is undeniable that
there is room for improvement in
this matter.
The average schoolgirl of today is
not simply dressed. There is much j
emulation among the feminine stu- j
dents, and those who are more for
tunately circumstanced set a pace in
the matter of dressing that cannot be
followed without serious sacrifices on
the part of parents. The desire to
be prettily gowned is perhaps in
stinctive, and parents are glad to do
what they can to satisfy this desire.
Often this parental kindness runs
away with judgment.
I niforming the public school pupils
is not altogether desirable. The boys
who belong to the cadet corps of the
high schools wear uniforms, but there
are many who do not join the ranks,
and many others wear their uniforms
only on "drill days.-' There is no
suggestion of uniforming among the
pupils of the lower grades.
Teachers can accomplish, much to
ward simplifying and standardizing
the dress of pupils by constantly ad
monishing those who are overdressed
or unsuitably attired that they are. in
innocence, offending against the prin
ciples of good taste. It may be made!
the "fashion" to dress simply, through
the voluntary adoption of plain co?
tumes by girls who are known to be
in circumstances to permit more cost
ly costumes. The cooperation of
parents i3 essential in this matter.
On the score of cosmetics, which
have been brought into this discus
sion, there is less difficulty. Girls
who present themselves at school with
their faces painted can be required
to wash them before appearing in
class. This can be put on the ground
of health, if the moral basis for such
a rule is not deemed desirable. In
any case there will be very general
public sympathy with the movement
which seeks to restore the school
rooms to the simple, wholesome con
ditions of the past, and those parents
who feel such sympathy can them
selves contribute directly and help-'
fully toward gaining this end.

Suffrage Now Stands 26 to 4.
After a long series of successes in
the state legislature.'., the federal
woman suffrage amendment has met
an obstacle. The lower house of Mis
sissippi has refused to ratify it, thus
putting that state for the present, at
least, definitely in the negative col
umn, along with Georgia, Alabama
and Virginia. South Carolina, it is
expected, will soon follow the same
course. With four states voting in
the negative, nine more must so vote
in order to defeat the amendment.
But there is always a chance for re
consideration. A move to that end
has been made in Virginia, the legis
lature of which is now in regular
session. Suffrage sentiment, it is said,
has developed there markedly dur
ing the past four months, and a re
versal of the rejecting vote is pos
sible. Rejection in Mississippi was
discounted. In fact, success for the
amendment is not looked for in any
of the southern states unless Ten
nessee may turn that way. Granting
Florida, Louisiana and the Carolinas,
there will be. with the four already
rejecting, eight in the negative, with
thirteen needed in all. The amend
ment must gain ten votes and the op
position?on the basis of eight as
sured rejections?must gain five, from
this list of states: Arizona, Connecti
cut, Delaware, Idaho, Maryland. Ne
vada, New Jersey, New Mexico, Okla
homa, Tennessee. Vermont. Washing
ton, West Virginia and Wyoming.
Ratification is confidently expected in
