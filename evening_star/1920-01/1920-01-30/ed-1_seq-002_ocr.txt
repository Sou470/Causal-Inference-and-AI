WEST VIRGINIA'S FORCE
OF CONSTABULARY AFIELD
Declared by Officers Heady for Any
Emergency?Are Entirely For
mer Service Men.
CHARLESTON. W. Va? January
20.?Composed entirely of former
service men, the West Virginia con
stabulary. after six months of prepa
ration and training, has taken the
field to enforce the law. Although
recruited to only one-half of its max
imum strength, the men on duty are
fully equipped and. according to
their officers, are ready for any emer
gency.
Under the command of Col. Jack
son Arnold, the men have been or
ganized into provisional companies
and assigned to fifteen subpatrtil sta
tions- These cover all trunk line
railroads in the state, for the pur
pose of facilitating mobilization
should it become necessary to move
the entire force to some point in a
hurry. At each substation from
three to five men will be on duty.
All will be mounted on horses ex
cept the patrols at Wellsburg and
Martlnsburg, who will be equipped
with motor cycles.
The service has already attracted
natives of twenty-three of the fifty
five counties in West Virginia. They
are picked men, whose Intelligence,
courage and endurance have been
proved to the satisfaction of Col.
Arnold.
The bill creating the state con
stabulary became a law six months
ago.

SHIPS TO OTHER PORTS
Twelve Hundred Freight Handlers
Out and Dock Space lie
ported Scarce.
BOSTON. January 30.?Diversion to
other ports of steamships bound hero
was begun today because of the strike
of 1,200 freight handlers. With dock
space scant and wharves piled high
with accumulating freight the ship
ping board steamer National Bridge
was ordered by wireless to take her
cargo from Chilean ports to New York
Instead of this port. Fifteen other ves
sels are due within a few days, and if
settlement of the strike is not in sight
most of them will be similarly di
verted.
The freight handlers' union coun
tered with a message to affiliated
freight handlers and longshoremen's
unions in New York, Baltimore and
other ports announcing "strike on in
Boston; look out for diverted ships."
Meantime many of the men who
struck while receiving 43 cents an

shovelers at rates of 60 to 90 cents an
hour, and leaders said that as long as
there was snow on the ground the
strike would be without cost to the
men.

H. U. MUDGE DIES.
Wai Former General Manager of
Atchison, Topeka Santa Fe.
DENVER. Col., January 30.?H. U.
Mudge, former general manager of
the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe
railway and one of the best known
railroad men in this country, died here
today.
Mr. Mudge was stricken with a
cerebral hemorrhage Monday night
last. He was sixty-four years old.
From water boy to general manager
of the Santa Fe is the story of Mr.
Mudge's rise In railroad work. He
went to work for the railroad in
Kansas in 1872, and before he left its
service in 1905 he had served five
years as its general manager. In 1905
Mr. Mudge became vice president of
the Chicago, Rock Island and Pacific
Railway Company and was elected
president of that road four years
later.
Coming to Denver in 1915 as presi
dent of the Denver and Rio Grande
Railway Company, Mr. Mudge served
In that capacity until two years ago,
when he retired.
