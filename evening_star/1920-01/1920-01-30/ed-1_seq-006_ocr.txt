"Never Start Wrong!"
A young man was electrocuted last
night at Sing Sing, N. Y., for the
murder of two bank clerks in Brook
lvn. He went to the death chair with
a remarkable nerve, maintaining to
the last the nonchalance which he had
displayed from the moment of his
arrest for another crime in the west.
I He refused spiritual solace at tlie end,
but said to a priest who asked him
for a message to the youth of the
|country:
"I don't wish to appear in the light
of a moralist, but you can tell them
for me never to start wrong. Once
you get started in crime you can never
stop."
This man's career was an exempli
fication of the truth of his admoni
tion. Well educated, of good family?
though he never disclosed his con
nections?and with superior advan
tages when on the threshold of man
hood, he "got started" wrong, ar.d in
a few years he ended his days in the
electric chair. He was the coldest
blooded criminal who has been taken
by the law in many years. Appar
ently he was not a typical case, but
his fate nevertheless serves as distinct
warning to young men.
In recent years a tendency has pre
vailed to lessen the penalties for
crime. The law gives a much better
chance for the wrong doer than ever.
Probation is granted to first offend
ers. Paroles are given to prisoners
who manifest a disposition to reform.
Sentences are lighter. Prison condi
tions are infinitely better than in the
past. Education is offered to men
serving terms. Everything possible
is done to eJfe t reformation.
Still the criminal bent continues to
manifest itself. Whether from a
spirit of adventure, or from neces
sity, or in acceptance of the challenge
of the law, young men embark on
careers of crime. Their beginnings
may be accidental, or deliberate.
They take chances, and eventually,
with few exceptions, they are caught
and punished. The chances are over
whelmingly against them.
This man Hamby in an earlier
period would probably have been a
freebooter, a bandit. In these times
he was an anachronism, a reversal to
a type that is slowly passing. The
confirmed criminal is usually the
product of environment. So far as
known Hamby had no excuse in his
circumstances for his deliberate adop
tion of this career.
The moral lesson taught by this
man's course and fate should be im
pressed upon all young men. In the
schools and in the churches he might
profitably be made the subject of
teachings. His final words, given to
the priest whose services he spurned
in his last hour, constitute a most im
pressive text for the youth of Amer
ica.
