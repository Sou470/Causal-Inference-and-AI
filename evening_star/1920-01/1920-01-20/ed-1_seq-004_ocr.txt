WOOD DENIES SAYING NAVY
"FLOATING DEATH TRAP"
General Declares He Referred in
Boston to Ships Filled With
Untrained Men.
BOSTON'. January 20.?Maj. Gen.
Leonard oud last night denied that
he had referred to the American Navy
as "a floating death trap" in his ad
dress at the American Legion meet
ing in this city Sunday. He said that
he could not recall the exact words
he had used, but dictated the follow
ing as the substance of what he had
said:
"We want a first-class Navy always
ready. A navy cannot be made in a
hurry. First-class ships filled with
untrained men do not mean a navy,
but are sometimes a death trap to
those who are in them."
Gen. Wood arrived here from Port
land early last evening, and a few
hours later took a train for New
York, whence he will go to Chicago.
The press report of his speech in
the Old South meeting house Sunday
was based on what purported to be a
stenographic copy of his remarks. An
examination of the stenographer's
notes shows that he reported the gen
eral as saying:
"I do not think we want a large
Regular Army. I am vtry much op
posed to it. We only want u large
enough Army to police the n*tion in
times of peace. We need a Navy. All
that we have now is not a navy; it is
a floating death trap. We want a good
Navy, with highly trained men and
?ood ships. They are a very active
force for peace. We want to build
up a good merchant marine. We
want this merchant marine for our
commerce and for our Navy. Back
of all that comes the big military
force of the nation, which means the
people of the nation."
Gen. Wood spoke extemporaneously
and at times rapidly touched briefly
on a number of national questions.
When he saw the version of his re
marks published he took the first op
portunity he had to explain that he
had been misquoted.

PERU TO SUBMIT CASE
WITH CHILE TO LEAGUE
Controversy Over Territories of
Tacna and Arioa Will Go Be
fore International Body.
SANTIAGO. Chile, January 19 (by the
Associated Press).?Peru will submit
to the league of nations her contro
versy with Chile over the territories
of Tacna and Arica. which are situ
ated between the two countries, and
possession of which has caused con
siderable bitterness in the past, ac
cording to an interview with Jose
Carlos Bernales, who was president of
the Peruvian senate during the ad
ministration of President Pardos.
"The foreign policv of Peru regard
ing questions with Chile," he is quoted
;is saying, "has undergone a radical
hange because the whole problem
will be submitted to the league of na
tions by my country. Then it will be
decided whether the Ancon treaty re
mains valid, and, if not. damages suf
fered by Peru by the act of Chile in
retaining possession of Tacna and
Arica may be determined. The league
of nations must formulate principles
for a permanent peace on the Ameri
can continent, and any plan involving
a direct agreement between Peru and
Chile will never have the support of
Peruvian opinion."
The treaty of Ancon was concluded
in October, 1883. Under its conditions
Peru ceded to Chile the province of
Tarpaca, and granted Chile the right
to occupy the territories of Tacna and
Artca for a period of ten years, when
the ownership of the districts would
be decided by a plebiscite of the
regions involved. This period expired
in 1694, but at that time internal trou
bles prevented active steps to bring
about a solution of the matter, which
is still pending-.
