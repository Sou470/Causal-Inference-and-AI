The Two Old Parties.
An organization calling itself tlic
Social Democratic league of Am erica
has issued :in appeal to wage earners
and the workers of the country to
unite for political action this year.
It does not propose a new party, but
takes a savage "crack" at the two old
parties. This is from a press report:
"The statement denounces impar
tially both the republican and deno
rratic parties. It asserts that the
democratic party, 'completely revers
ing its admirable war record,' has tried
to 'destroy organized labor by the
mandatory injunction and has taken j
the blame for the high cost of living
off the shoulders of the profiteers and
placed it. on the backs of labor.' 1 he
republican party is declared to be
'openly outbidding even the Bourbon
democrats for the support of reac
tionary employers, trust magnates and
Wall street.''
This being presidential year, some
thing?even a great deal?may be al
lowed for campaign extravagance. And
yet, after that allowance has been
made, there is room for sincere regret
that men of the grade of Charles Ed-
ward Russell, William English Wall
ing and ,T. G. Phelps Stokes should
have sigued spch a deliverance.
True, it is much in line with the de
liverance of the bull moosers in 1912.
They denounced the republican party
and the democratic party, holding that
those organizations had become the
subservient tools of the "interests''
and the enemies of the people. And
Theodore Roosevelt led a revolt that
year, and had in his long train both
republicans and democrats.
But let us remember that eight years
ago neither America nor the world at
large had been shaken by a world
?war; that in our own country here
our wrangles were only of the con
ventional sort, and testified only to the
"pep"' inhering in a capable and en
ergetic people forging ahead under a
system of party government. There
was no particular danger in hectic
Thetorie. The sophisticated could
smile it away.
The situation is very different to
day. This country is in a turmoil.
Discontent was never before so wide
spread or deep seated. Hectic rhetoric
was never before so inadvisable. All
men with the proper regard for the
public welfare should watch their
tongues and pens, and refrain from
even the conventional campaign ex
travagances.
If the two old parties were as de
scribed by these critics there would
be no hope for the country. We should
have failed signally in managing our
own affairs. The most rampant "red,"
foreign or native born, would be in
part justified in his contentions and
demands for an overturn. This is a
government by parties, and if both
the leading parties are rotten we
should try some other form of govern
ment.
But we have not failed. Neither of
the two old parties is rotten, and one
or the other is certain to triumph at
the polls next November.
Henry Ford is credited with the
statement that success lies in the
ability to produce in quantity.
Hence the success of the able con
tributors to the Congressional Record.
Socialists who disapprove of laws
aTe perhaps consistent in their desire
t.o join a legislative body and. if pos
sible, spoil them in the making.
The Navy.
Gen. Leonard Wood submits this:
"We want a first-class Navy always
ready. A Navy cannot be made in a
hurry. First-class ships filled with
untrained men do not mean a Navy."
Could there be a sounder proposi
tion? May not, should not, every
American subscribe to it? Did the i
great war disclose anything more con
spicuously or of higher importance
than the value of lighting power on
the water! Without it, where would!
the allies have been? With it, they i
were able to put their program j
through.
A first-class fighting ship requires:
first-class handling. Without it she is I
of small consequence. Men must be j
trained and kept fit for the work.
Climbing ropes and shaking out sail, j
and banging away with muzzle load
ers, are in, and of, the past. Jack
today is assigned to duty on a very
eompUe.-ifed contraption. He cannot j
learn his trade in a jiffy and perform j
his duties without practice. Rome was
not built in a da>. Nor can a fighting]
sailor be turned out while you wait.
Time is required.
The slogan, therefore, is. first class j
ships manned by first-class men. An
adequate American Navy for all Amer
lean purposes. i
Surely there are but few little Navy
men now.' Why should there be any?
What has taken place these past five j
and a half years to persuade any man
that wars are no longer to be pro
vided against? What nation is dis
arming, afloat or ashore? W hat mari

But there would be no economy in
skimping the Navy. On the contrary,

us at no distant date losses that money
alone could not repair. We might
suffer in prestige as a nation, and be
set back in all our plans for the
future.

Thrift Week.
This is thrift week. The lesson of
personal thrift and prosperity is be
ing taught throughout the country in
schools and is being offered to adults
in terms of practical persuasion, that
through saving and economy and in
dustry individual prosperity is gained.
Every week of the year, of course,
should be a thrift week. The present
week, however, is an intensive object
lessen to stimulate public interest, to
bring squarely home to everybody the
principle that no matter what the in
come, some part of it should be saved
as a reserve or the foundation of cap
ital.
During the war the people of this
i country learned thrift on a big scale.
In the face of the enormous expendi
tures of the government they were
given opportunities for saving through
the buying of bonds and stamps on
the installment plan. It was put with
I in the ability of every person in this
j country to save something by these
means, with the incentive of aiding
the government in its war work.
The result was several billion dol
lars thus saved by the people and
forming today a Capital that would
Otherwise have been lacking.
Now the war is over, but the gov
ernment is still offering opportunities
to the people for small scale saving.
The stamp system is continued. Any
person who wishes to do so can start
a systematic thrift campaign fty the
year 19C0 by buying theno stamps.
Children who bought the 1918 and
1919 stamps should be encouraged by
their parenU to continue. They ahould
be glad of tho chance. During the
present week the lesson of thrift will
be brought home to them in tho
schools and it should bo also taught
in the homes.
Through thrift, which is saving and
investment, comes economy, and
through economy on the part of all
the people will come relief from high
prices. As a m^ans to the end of re
storing the price level to normal,
thrift is to be encouraged. Every one
who saves should urge all others to
save, for the effect is cumulative and
cverbody benefits from tho thrift of
his neighbors.

A Recollection.
I like to think about the place
Where as a child I played.
In fancy often I rctrace
The paths where once I strayed;
As cherished memories I recall
Amid the haunts of yore,
I seem to prize above them all
The good old grocery store?
Where you could sit and take your
ease
And eat the crackers and the cheese
And browse around the bounteous
shelf,
And when inclined, just help yourself!
I see the shadows in the pool,
I hear the distant bell,
Which, with its summons back to'
school,
I heeded none too well.
Yet whatsoe'er may be the spot
I linger to explore,
My dreaming cuts across the lot
To that old grocery store?
Where bacon from the rafters hung
And fruits in gay festoons were strung;
Where you could get without a thrill
Some change back from a dollar bill!
