DECLARES LABOR WILL
AFFECT NEXT ELECTION
Secretary Morrison of A. F. I
Criticises Pending Legislation
and Discusses H. C. L.
Frank Morrison, secretary of the
American Federation of Labor,
speaking before the members of Co
Jumbia Lodge. No. 174. International
Association of Machinists, last night
at Naval Hall, reviewed national labor
conditions, paying particular atten
tion to legislation before Congress as
it relates to the labor movement and
The interests of the wage-earners, and
devoting some time to the high cost
of living.
He declared that organized labor
would be a factor in the4 coming
presidential election in the selection
of men standing for election to the
Senate and House, and that the po
litical activity of labor in the ap
proaching election would be upon a
more extensive scale than ever before
in the history of the movement. He
asserted that the labor vote would be
cast for candidates, regardless of po
litical affiliation, whose records were
friendly to the interests of the masses,
or men who would pledge themselves
to give labor a square deal and hear
ing.
Responding to a question why the
American Federation of Labor had not
officially taken up and organized a
national labor political party, Mr.
Morrison said the officials of the
American Federation of Labor act
only upon instructions from the con
vention of the organization: that at
the Atlantic City convention last
June the instructed delegates, repre
senting the 4,000,000 wage-earners of
the American Federation of Labor,
had decided, in line with the long
established policy of the national
labor movement, that the American
Federation of l^abor was an organi
zation with one purpose?the better
ment of the laboring classes?and not
a political body.
Criticises Proposed Legislation.
Mr. Morrison critisized pending leg
islation in Congress that would make
i it a crime for workingmen to strike
land also the anti-sedition bill that h?*
asserted would take from the masses
constitutional rights. the basis of
free speech, a free press and the right
I to assemble. He censured Attorney
(Jeneral Palmer for his action in ap
plying: the Lever food act against the
striding coal minors as in violation of
assurances given labor that that law
was not intended to be applied and
would not be applied to any labor
movement.
Touching upon the action of the
courts against the interests of labor.
Mr. Morrison said that the masses can
not get justice in the courts for the
reason that the cost of litigation is
such that the man of ordinary means
cannot stand the expense, while the
classes have that advantage over the
masses. In this connection he quoted
as authority statistics from the Car
negie Foundation that has made an
elaborate report on the subject.
Taking up the high cost of living
he said that there had been an increase
of SO per cent in necessities since I>e
j cember, 191S, and more than SO per
cent increase since June. 1919. He
pointed out that by reason of Con
gress' failure to make the necessary I
appropriation the Department of La
bor has made no survey of increased
costs for 1919 of foodstuffs, housing,
heat cost, furniture, clothing and mis
cellaneous articles. He said the cost
of living had advanced generally 90
per cent and in most cities food sup
plies more than 100 per cent. The only
substantial wage increases to meet
tYiese abnormal conditions, he said,
was among the common labor in the
steel mills and that all other organ
ized labor was working for a wage
away below the advances in the cost
of living.
