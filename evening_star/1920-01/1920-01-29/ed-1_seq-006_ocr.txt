The Eligibility Issue.
Opposition to the confirmation of
Dr. John Van Schaick, jr.. for the of
fice of District Commissioner ha? been j
expressed before the Senate District j
committee in varying terms and on;
diverse grounds. Advocates of his
confirmation have presented his case
rigorously with full appreciation of
his qualifications as a man and as a
prospective executive official. The
committee is now considering the mat
ter preliminary to a report to the Sen
ate concerning it.
The latest objection raising a seri
ous issue in the controversy over the j
continuation of Dr. an Schaick bears
upon his status a> an actual resident
of the District, and questions his eli
gibility under the law to be appoint
ed Commissioner.
The organic act of 1?7S, whose ben
eficent provisions Washington jeal
ousy defends, provides, as our sole re
maining fragment of suggestion of,
municipal home rule, that the civil- j
ian Commissioners '"shall have been

It develops that Dr. A an Schaick has j

he has canceled his New ork citi

There is no record of a formal act of

thus expressed, severs him from New
York citizenship s-.nd transfers him
to actual residence in Washington,
and whether this actual transfer took
place more "than three years ago are
questions which the District commit
tee and the Senate will decide.
The eligibility issue differs from all

it is a legal question to be decided j
by the Senate as a court, entirely
irrespective of the individual opin
ions of senators concerning Dr. an
Schaick personally and of their de

And the judicial responsibility of the
Senate is heavier in this instance be
cause, in effect, its decision is final,

dered, be reviewed and corrected in
any court.
The practical effect of the Supreme
Court's decision in the Newman case
is that appointments to office by the
President in the District, when they
have been confirmed by the Senate,
may not be questioned or reviewed by
the courts on the ground of ineligi
bility under the law.
If a citizen having, as the court says,
only a public interest in the office
challenges an incumbent's title to of
fice he must secure action by the At
torney General or the district attor
ney. It is an obvious proposition that,
ihe President having appointed and
secured the confirmation of a munici
pal official for the District, the Presi
dent's chief law officer will not act at
the behest of citizens in challenge of
his chief's right to appoint.
Without the intervention by the
Attorney Genera!, a citizen to chal
lenge the eligibility under the law of
:i Commissioner must be, tl<f Supreme
fourt says, "interested'' in the office;
that is, he must have a direct, per
sonal, pecuniary interest in it. Micro
scopic examination develops that no j
such person can exist.
The residential qualification may
thus, in effect, be repealed by execu
tive action and judicial prohibition!
of intervention by citizens. This de
rision throws upon the executive in
making these ap|?ointmects to local
office and the Senate in giving consent
to such appointments an additional
obligation to respect scrupulously the
requirements of the law respecting
residential eligibility.
Since the Senate is in this case the
jury, the judge and the final court of
.?ipp-.ils. all in one, its judicial de
termination of the question whether
the requirement or* the law as to local
residence has been fully complied
with both in letter and in spirit
should receive the same careful,
thoughtful, painstaking consideration
that the Supreme t'ourt gives to its
decisions. For in this case the Sen
ate, under the decision in the Newman
case, is substituting with full juris
diction and power for the Supreme
Court of the I'nited States.
On American principles of repre
sentative government all municipal
officials are, of course, exclusively
l'rom the local community. The or
ganic act of 1878 gives legal assur
ance that in the local government of
the capital community two municipal
officials, the civilian Commissioners,
shall be Washingtonians and nothing
else. The President, the Senate and
the Supreme Court, actmg for the

Public Men and Public Service.
The west ami the near west are very
much in the presidential picture. Sen
ator Johnson and Mr. Hoover are list
ed as Californians. Senator Poindex
ter is from Washington. Mr. Bryan
and Senator Hitchcock represent
Nebraska. Gen. Pershing was born in
Missouri. Gov. Allen and Senator
Capper are Kansans. Champ Clark is
a Missourian. Senator Owen writes
Oklahoma after his name. A goodly
list.
The length of the list suggests the
Saying, "the more, the merrier." Well,
why should not both the Chicago con
vention and the San Francisco con
vention be merry? Why should not j
the west, with her favorite sons, the
cast, with hers, and the middle states,
with theirs, meet and have it out in
vigorous American fashion?
It is assuring to see so many men
mentioned in connection with the
presidency. In the east, in the west
and in the middle country are po
litical leaders worthy in the eyes of
their friends of the highest office un
der the government. They have
shown quality in their public serv
ices, and thus have increased con
fidence in their character and ca
pacity.
And yet we are told by the bilious
and the discontented that our institu
tions are a failure; that they do not
produce the right sort of public ser
vants; that as a rule our leaders lack

vision; that as a nation we are j
stumbling along either in awkward j
or in reckless fashion, and seem head
ed for the ditch.
A presidential year may be de
scribed as a stock-taking year. We
go over our material 111 search of the
best in the shop. We need the best
in our business. The work in the
White House makes heavy demands
011 the man chosen to perform it,
while the work on Capitol Hill is of
such, sweep and variety the large
number required to perform it are se
lected with care.
We do not in every case get the j
best material. Here and there, now j
and then, a bit of shoddy slips in.!
But, as a rule, our selections are good, I
and our public servants acquit them- j
selves so as to earn the respect in
which they are held.
The men in public office today and
those mentioned for office are in their
abilities and training and achieve
ments a refutation of the charge that
as a nation we are slipping?going
back at an alarming speed. We are
doing nothing of the kind. We are
today the strongest nation in the
world, with resources beyond those of
any other forward nation, and fully
capable of playing the part fortune
has assigned us.

The Chicago Platform.
In his announcement of the ap
pointment of an advisory committee
011 policies and platform, to report at
Chicago in June, Chairman Hays says
of the republican party: "It is the
party of the future, or there is no
use for the party." This is a truth
well expressed.
What of the future? The party is
now in power on Capitol Hill, and is
seeking this year full control of the
government. The country has never
before faced such a condition of af
fairs as it now faces. We are con
siderably upset, as indeed the whole
world is. We must fashion some Dew
things, and refashion some old things.
But we should do both in conformity
with the great charter upon which the
1 government rests.
At Chicago in June the republicans
must pledge themselves as to the im
mediate future. A platform covering
the leading issues of the day must be
prepared and adopted, and lived up
10 if the voters in November accept
it. It would be worse than folly to
construct a platform merely "to get
in on." The (situation is such that if
the people felt they were being
trifled, with after giving their suf
frage for stated things they might
take extreme measures ;ig:iinst their
misleaders.
Mr. Hays is justified, therefore, in
I making the fullest provision for the
[construction of the best platform pos
sible. The advisory committee he has
appointed is large, but. not too large.
It has a wide field to cover. It is
composed of al)le men and women,
representing many lines of thought
and with opportunities and the time
for making investigations. They have
four months for gathering the ma
ferial which they will turn over at
Chicago to those charged with the

structing a firm foundation for the
presidential campaign.

The Army-Navy Pay Bill.
Failure of a (juorum on Tuesday
caused postponement by the Senate of
consideration of the pending bill for
the establishment of a more adequate
scale of pay for the personnel of the
Army and the Navy. Again yester
day the bill was temporarily laid aside
because of the absence of several
senators who wished to discuss it. The
hope is that there will be a sufficient
attendance to permit, the conclusion
of the debate on this measure at an
early date, so that it can be put on its
passage, and, if adopted, sent to the
House. The matter of better pay for
the Army and Xavy is of the most
pressing urgency. In this respect, as
in the civilian administration, the
government lags far behind. It is at
this time impossible to maintain the
[military and naval forces of the eoun
i try at their authorized strength, for
the reason that the pay offered will
not suffice to keep men in the service.
Officers cannot maintain themselves
on the comparatively meager allow
ances granted. Resignations are be
ing filed at a rate that threatens the
serious depletion of the service. There
is no stimulus of war now to keep
these men. They are brought direct
ly to face the uncompromising fact
that their pay does not suffice for the
sustenance of themselves and their
families. Many of them are falling
into debt, from which they can ex
tricate themselves with the greatest
difficulty, even if the new pay scale
is adopted. In the ranks there are
wide gaps, due to the fact that the
military and naval services cannot
compete in the matter of compensa
tion with the industries. Recruiting
is attended with the most serious diffi
culty. It is hard to persuade able
bodied young men that they can find
a career in the Army or Xavy when
the pay offered is so meager that their
enlistment may appear merely as a
refuge from non-employment. The
early enactment of this bill into law
will greatly stimulate enlistment and
will, moreover, serve to keep within
the service many capable and valu
able officers who are now strongly
tempted to resign and who are in
alarming numbers yielding to the
temptation to leave for better-paid
occupations.
