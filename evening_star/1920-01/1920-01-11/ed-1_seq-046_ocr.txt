SOMETHING ABOUT ENGINE OIL.
When Sn*pld?iu as to Oil Qulltr. Try Them.
Hardly second in importance to providing' a sufficiency of oil to all parts
of an engine, at all times, is the question of the quality of the oil furnished
it. The following remarks relate to the properties -which a good oil should
possess and as to what tests are employed to demonstrate them: (1) A gas
engine cyclinder oil should be of purely mineral origin, as otherwise corro
sive fatty acids will be present. f2) As all oils become thinner with in
crease of temperature, a reliable oil should resist this thinning effect to
such an extent that, at the temperature of boiling water, it shall still have
thickness or "body" enough so as not to be squeezed out. under pressure,
from between the surfaces to be lubricated, thus allowing them to come into
contact and cut each other. (8) Since, on the other hand, all oils tend to
become thick and "buttery" when cooled, practical use requires one which
shall remain fluid and capable of free circulation at the lowest temperature
existing within an engine, after it is in operation. (4) In order that it may
be durable as a lubricant and not foul the internal engine parts with car
bonaceous substances, it must possess the power of resisting blasts of ex
tremely hot gases and contact with hot metals, not only as against rapid
evaporation but also as against decomposition. If an engine oil fails to
answer, requirement (1) there will be some roughening and pitting of en
gine parts, through corrosive action. If it fails in (2) there will be an undue
loss of power through excessive friction, abnormal wear 6t parts, unusual
heating and possibly cylinder scoring and burned-out bearings, especially
if an engine is worked hard. If requirement (3) is not successfully met
there will be danger of scratched cylinders and melted bearings in starting
an engine from a cold condition, on account of the thickened oil failing to
reach and distribute over the bearing surfaces promptly enough. Any fail
ure to fulfill condition (4) will result in an abnormal consumption of oil, in
a smoky exhaust in fouled spark plugs and faulty ignition, in rapid carboni
zation of valves, pistons and cylinder h^ads and?in case water finds its way
into the crankcase?in thfe emulsification or "beating together" of the oil
and water, in which condition lubricating qualities are lessened and th?re
is danger of oil passages becoming obstructed.
