to Crushing Defeat in World Series,
Sounds Knell for Chicago.
By DENMAN THOMPSON.
With the prospective loss of half of the infield with which lie won
the American League pennant last year, Kid Glcason now finds himself
the recipient of condolences from all sides over what observers regard
as the breaking up of his championship combination. ith the an
nouncement from Chic Gandil that he has sickened of big league ball
and hankers to run a team out on the coast and Swe.de Ri.sberg mak
ing a noise like a Greek in opening a restaurant in Chicago, critics pro
fess to see the end of the White Sox as flag contenders. But it is not
the possibility that Gandil and Risberg will leave holes at first and
short that makes Comiskey's chances for playing host at another world
series next fall appear slim. The Sox are through, anyhow.
It is doubtful that Gandil will be given permission to try his hand
at managing, and it is unlikely he will quit Chicago unless he is.
Furthermore, Risberg's retirement is not taken seriously in the Windy
City, where it is regarded as merely the preliminary to salary demands.
What is more, if Kisberg does quit, his departure will not be greatly
mourned by South Side fans, many of whom believe Buck Weaver is
the greatest shortstop in the game, and regard Fred McMullin, who
was part of the infield combination that was so effective against the
Giants in the 1917 world series, as an unexcelled third sacker. Then
there i? Harvey McClellan, who is such a promising juvenile candidate
for the far side of the diamond that Gleason has refused several tempt
ing offers from rival managers.
As for first base, in the event Gandil does quit, Shano Collins is
available fn a pinch. He broke into the league as a .first baseman,
although he has played in the outfield almost exclusively for the past
eight years. Then there are two other possibilities for the keystone
sack. One is Ted Jourdan, who subbed for Gandil in 1917. was in the
Army the following year, and last season distinguished himself by
landing in the .300 class with Minneapolis in the American Association.
Fred Henry, last season with Milwaukee, is the other. Chicago has
had a string on hint for two years.
WEAK IN PITCHERS AND IN SPIRIT.
It will not be a dearth of infielders that will prevent the Sox from
maintaining this year the pace fh'ey have set for the last few seasons.
Their weakness is in the box?and in loss of the morale that is essgn
tial for any club to land at or near the top. The flaw in their hurling
corps was apparent long before they entered the title series, and the
wonder of it is that they managed to stave off the inevitable long
i enough to land the American League title. They literally staggered
through to victory, and doubtless would have failed had the seasoai
been of normal length, but it was in the post-season series with the
Reds that they got their knockout blow. Disintegration already had
set in, but the crushing defeat administered by the Mpraninen finished
the job, and the subsequent charges of faking have not helped them
in recovering their spirit. The dismal showing made by Oscar Felsch
in the blue-ribbon event was another sign of the times, and many be
lieve this once brilliant flychaser is due to continue skidding.
But it is on the firing line that the Sox are fatally weak. Cicotte,
it is true, pitched brilliantly over the greater part of the season, but
wabbled badly at the finish, and was a great disappointment in the
title games. He will be thirty-six years old next season, and has been
playing professional ball for seventeen years. He cannot go on for
ever. Claude Williams, although some seven years younger, showed
unmistakable signs of strain last fall, and the three defeats he suf
fered in the series will prove a great tonic for the American League
batters who must face him, and will not tend to increase the south
paw's confidence in himself. Dick Kerr undoubtedly will show great
improvement next season, and much is expected of young Wilkinson,
but Chicago must acquire several new moundsmen of proved worth
before it can hope to do better than put up a fight to linish in the
first division.
Gleason will deserve to be hailed as another miracle man if he can
round up all his veterans, keep them traveling at their best gait, tone
up the weakened morale of the team and land another flag. It is as
