In The .Star yesterday appeared a
description of the new building, now

house the Frear art collection in this
city. This will be one of the most at
tractive features of the capital when

ton was chosen by the donor, the late
Charles 1,. Frear of Detroit, for its
permanent location, and especially in
such liberal terms that the collection
will grow with the years.
That Washington is destined to be
one of the world's art centers is evi
denced by late developments. It now
has two galleries?the Corcoran and
the National?and a third?the Frear
is about to be dedicated to the pub
lic. several efficient art schools are
conducted here. The city is the home
of many artists of note who find here
a congenial atmosphere for their work.
A biennial exhibition of the works of
cotemporary American artists is held
at the Corcoran Gallery, being now
regarded as one of the most impor
tant displays of art held in the coun
try.
Whether by public endowment or
by private bcncfaetion, other galleries
and collections arc certain to develop
in Washington. Projects are already
under way to that end. The local
population is steadily and rapidly in
creasing. The city is being chosen

to encourage artists bv their purchases
and patronage, so that it is expected
that other artists will com here to

Taken altogether the prospect of

Freight rates have played a very small part in the
rising cost of living.
Other causes ?the waste of war, under-production,
credit inflation have added dollars to the cost of the
necessities of life, while freight charges have added
only cents.
The average charge for hauling a ton of freight a mile
is less than a cent.
A suit of clothing that sold for $30 before the war was
carried 2,265 miles by rail from Chicago to Los Angeles
for I6V2 cents.
Now the freight charge is 22 cents and the suit sells
for $50.
The cost of the suit has increased 20 dollars.
The freight on it has increased only 5^4 cents.
Other transportation charges enter into the cost of the finished article
?carrying the wool to the mills and the cloth to the tailors but
these other charges amount to but a few cents more.
The $10 pair of shoes that used to sell for $5 goes
from the New England factory to the Florida dealer for
a freight charge of 5% cents?only one cent more than
the pre-war rate.
Beef pays only two-thirds of a cent a pound freight
from Chicago to New York.
American freight rates are the lowest in the world.
