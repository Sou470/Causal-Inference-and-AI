Women to Work for Better Schools.
The women of Washington have
taken a hand directly in the matter
of getting the public schools better
equipped with teachers and buildings.
The District Congress of Mothers and
Parent Teachers' Associations has
taken the field in this crusade and,
without definitely joining the citizens'
school betterment movement, has
pledged itself to the work of putting
the schools of the capital in shape for
effective teaching of the children.
This is a material addition to the
movement and will doubtless have its
immediate effect. The women propose
to conduct their own inquiries into
school needs. They will quickly find
that the primary necessity is for more
teachers and more buildings. Better
pay for the teachers is, of course, re
garded as an essential. Retirement
has been already secured. A business
management for the school system,
the fourth item of the citizens' pro
gram left remaining, is so definitely
agreed upon by all that it cannot fail
to appeal to Congress as an urgent
need.
School reorganization is not likely
to be proposed by the women who have
thus formally joined the campaign for
school betterment. It is generally
recognized that in some respects there
is need' of readjustments and changes.
But complete agreement cannot be had
at present upqn the aature of those
changes in the basic system. Their
lack does not lessen the efficiency of
the schools in a degree comparable to
the handicap of inadequate teachers
and buildings. The essential, impera
tively needed reforms today are phys
ical, upon which everybody is in ac
cord.
With the citizens' committee co
operating with and substantially sup
ported by the board of education, and
with the women of Washington
through their largest representative
organization aiming at the same pur
pose of immediate school develop
ment, the District's wishes in the mat
ter of public educational betterment
will be made known to Congress with
a unanimity seldom, if ever, before
expressed. Results should flow quickly.

Potomac Ice.
With ice obstructing navigation in
the Potomac river there is necessarily
some apprehension of another visita
tion such as that of 1918, when the
channel was blocked and much dam
age was done. But conditions thus far
are not conducive to a blockade, as
they were two years ago. There has
been little snow and the hills of the
watershed are lightly covered, if at all.
Unless the season is exceptional from
this time onward, there will be no very
great volume of water to be brought
downstream suddenly with the break
up of the ice. Tw| years ago the ice
was almost unprecedented and the
snow was deep in the hills. By this
time Washington had had as much
"winter" as it usually gets in all three
months of the cold season. It has be
come an axiom of this climate that
the capital's worst period occurs be
tween February 1 and March 15. It
will take a phenomenally severe visi
tation during those six weeks to put
the capital in a position to be menaced
by such a gorge and freshet as 1918
brought.
