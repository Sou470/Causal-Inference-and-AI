SINGLE BODY TO RULE
GAME IS IN PROSPECT
Centralized Control Would Make Commission
Chairmanship Most Important Office.
Landis and Edwards Lead Candidates.
BY DENMAN THOMPSON.
With the possibility that a reorganization of the government of or
ganizfd base ball will develop from the joint meeting of the major leagues
in Chicago February 11. the question of who will be chosen as chairman
becomes increasingly important. From intimations made by President
Hevdler it is gathered that a great majority of the club owners of the
National League are in favor of'a centralized government for all of or
t ganized base ball in the interests of harmony between the major and
minor leagues. At present they are estranged because the latter a year
ago voluntarily abrogated the old working agreement, but the latter now
are as anxious to re-establish relations as they formerly were to reject
them.
A single ruling body for both the big and little circuits would end the
existence of the board of arbitration of the national association of minor
leagues, the guiding body of base ball as it is conducted in the sticks. It
also would enhance the importance of the national commission, which
then would shape the policies of both the majors and minors and act in all
affairs between the two. In this event it will be necessary to accord to
the minors a boon they have been lutilely clamoring for for years, rep
re>entation on the commission, and the powers that be in the sport would
make no mistake if they went even farther and accorded like recognition
to the playing fraternity. It is the athletes who actually toil on the dia
mond that make the sport possible. They are the real backbone of the
game, and it seems no more than equitable that they be represented at the
councils, where matters of vital importance to them are decided.
MOST IMPORTANT OFFICE IN GAME.
In the event that a reorganization of the national commission were
effected, the chairmanship of the new body would become the most im
portant executive office in base ball, greater, even, than the presidency
of the major leagues. Thus, added incentive would be given for the exer
cise of the greatest care in the selection of a chairman. Garry Herr
mann's succes>or should be a man of known integrity and unquestioned
executive ability, combined with that rare attribute referred to as a judi
cial mind. He should b^ one who would warrant the offer of a long-term
contract at a salary of at least $12,000 a year.
There is no lack of applicants for the position. Nearly every promi
nent figure in the game has a pet candidate, the latest to enter a nomina
tion being the retiring chairman. Herrmann is credited with having in
dorsed the candidacy of Adrian C. Anson, the famous slugger and one
time captain of the old Chicago Colts, whom he believes better qualified
for the place than most of those thus far mentioned. One thing in Anson's
favor is that he knows base ball thoroughly, something which cannot be
said of many of the others. He has seen the game rise from an inconse
quential position to its present high state, and has a national reputation
for sterling honesty and commcn sense.
Robert H. Young of this city, who is said to be favored by President
Heydler and has the unqualified indorsement of Clark Griffith, is one of
several mentioned whose aspirations will be seriously considered, but the
ronnineess who command the greatest backing to date undoubtedly are
Judge Landis of Chicago,, a member of the federal bench, and "Big Bill"
Edwards of New York, collector of internal revenue in that district. It
1 is known that both would accept and it is believed that either would
prove eminently acceptable, btt both have powerful opponents, and for
this reason it is not beyond the realm of possibility that some one as yet
not mentioned in connection with the position ultimately will be selected.
Persons familiar "with the status of Judge Landis assert that he
would not say "no" if properly approached and the conditions were suit
able, but believe he would have to be offered at least a ten-year term
at a high salary to induce him to relinquish the position he now holds.
EDWARDS NATIONALLY FAMOUS SPORTSMAN.
It is different in the case of Edwards. The latter, a great foot ball
player in his Princeton days, an ardent fan and close student of base
ball, is nationally famous as a sportsman. Although on Uncle Sam's
pay roll, he is not dependent upon the salary he receives as internal
revenue collector, being one of the most successful insurance brokers in
Xew York, with an income three or four times the size of any salary
base ball could offer him. Edwards believes in sports for sport's sake
and is withal a two-fisted guy, with great force of character and unques
tioned executive ability. He proved this when as deputy street-cleaning
commissioner of New York he earned a national reputation by weeding
out the grafters and welding the organieation into a model of efficiency.
It is assured that Edwards would not demand an exorbitant salary if
Offered the position, and that he would not insist upon a long-term con
tract. He is of the type that would not want the job if he could not make
good on it to the satisfaction of all concerned. If Edwards ever becomes
rhairman of the national commission the problem of the eliminatin of
gambling from ball parks that now is giving leaders of the sport so much
roncern soon would cease to exist. Persons who know hini well insist
1 that "'Big Bill" is one of the best little housedeaners extant.
