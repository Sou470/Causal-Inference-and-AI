SKELETON OF FLYING
REPTILE IS EXHIBITED
Prehistoric Creature's Bones May
Aid Present Aeronautic
Experiments.
NEW TORK. January 24.?The
skeleton of a huge flying reptile,
probably the largest flying creature
that ever existed, which in its pre
historic day was as wide ranging an
ocean traveler as a modern seaplane,
has been placed on exhibition at the
American Museum of Natural History,
it was announced today. Study of the
mechanics of the creature's flight,
which is planned by scientists, may
throw light on the practical problems
of aeronautics, it is declared.
The clumsy, toothless animal?a
Pteranodon?which flourished in the
age of reptiles, many millions of
years ago. had a wing spread of six
teen feet from tip to tip, but because
of its curious dimensions may not
have been as heavy as a modern con
dor. which sometimes attains a thir
teen-foot spread. It was nearly all
wines and head, with a small body,
slender hind legs and a mere stump
of a tail.
The specimen was found by Handel
T. Martin, curator of the Geological
Museum of Kansas University, in the
cretaceous chalk beds of the Smoky
Hill river of western Kansas. It is
believed to be the only mounted speci
men of a pteranodon in any Ameri
can museum.

Purpose of Council of Agriculture.
To the Editor of Tb* 8t?r:
There seems to be an Idea on the
part of some people that the purpose
of the recently-organized District
Council of Agriculture is to secure
2.W0 members who will plant back
ywd gar-dens. As a matter of fact.
th?re have been from 10,000 to 12.000
baokyard and vacant-lot gardens cul
tivated in the District during the past
two years and a portion of the veg
etables for the table of that number
of families have been produced.
Stimulating home gardening is but
one purpose of this organization, and
the problem of getting the producers
within a radius of fifty or sixty miles
of Washington in closer contact with
the consumers in the District is an
important part of the plan of the
work. Before the consumer can get
his foodstuffs at lower prices, and at
the same time the farmer get a larger
slice of the consumer's dollar, there
must be a better understanding be
tween the consumer and the farmer.
The fact that farmers have sold tur
nips at 30 cents a bushel (as re
ferred to by Mr. J. H. Shepherd in an
article in the The Sunday Star) and
the consumer paying $2.40 a bushel
is due to the system of handling. In
the first place, the farmer probably
brought in a wagonload of ungraded
turnips in sacks or barrels and sold
them to a jobber; the consumer
bought them In quarter-peck lota and
asked that they be delivered to his
door. When the distribution of farm
products becomes so organized that
the grower can deliver in quantity to
oentral distributing points, from
whiah the consumers will be supplied
without extra handling costs, the
problem will be at least partly solved.
We have communities around Wash
ington where foods are being bought
in carloads from the producers and
distributed in suitable quantities to
the homes without loss and with a
minimum of handling expense.
Back of it all lies the proper or
ganization of the producers and the
standardization of grade and pack.
By standardization is meant, first, the
planting of the same crops and vari
eties by all the farmers in a given
community, and, second, uniformity
of grading and marketing, placing
only products of uniform quality on
the market. Owing to the many haz
ards under which the farmer produces
a crop it is impossible for him to
enter a contract guaranteeing deliv
ery at a price fixed in advance.
Prices are governed, and will con
tinue to be governed, by the old law
of supply and demand, and the only
way to lower priaea Is to increase
production. The only way to accom
plish this Is to see that the grower
gets a larger percentage of the con
sumer's dollar.
FRANK W. BOI/3IANO.
