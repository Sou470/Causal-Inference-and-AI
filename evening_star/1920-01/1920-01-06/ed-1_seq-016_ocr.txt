BELASCO?"The Guest of Honor."
A large and highly pleased audience
saw "The Guest of Honor" at the
Shubert-Belasco last night. This time
Mr. William Hodge has chosen a poet
as the type of personality to reflect
his distinctive talents as a delineator
of character. Mr. Hodge assumes
trank responsibility for the entire en
tertainment. including that of author
and stage director as well as star. It
was to be taken for granted that as
I author Mr. Hodge took excellent care
of Mr. Hodge the actor, and that as
stage director Mr. Hodge did not per
mit Mr. Hodge, the actor, to eliminate
or garble the favorite lines of Mr.
Hodge the author. The elements of
l-the triple function harmonized for the
production of a good, workable, ap
plause-winning entertainment. The
leading role was that of John Weath
erby, who, despite his profession of
poet, succeeds in presenting the pic
ture of droll humanity in which Mr.
Hodge has specialized tor so many sea
sons. Crisp, telling lines are delivered
with a simplicity and directness that
give them the force of spontaneous
utterance. Mr. Hodge as stage direc
tor has not been unduly solicitous to
prevent possible rivalry with Mr.
Hodge the star. The play is admir
ably cast and a little boy comes for
ward with a role of captivating inter
est. The juvenile part is interpreted,
the program states, by either Graham
Lucas or Gene Howard.
The youngster who played it last
night is a handsome child with a
winsome smile and a wistful appeal
in voice and action that made him
a center of attention. A prominent
role is that of an elderly man nearly
blind. It is enacted consistently and
impressively by Scott Cooper. The
feminine interest supplied by the role
of Rosamond Kent gives Jane Hous
ton opportunity for good playing in
two particularly effective scenes.
Humorous touches were conveyed by
Jennie Lamont, as the Irish laun
dress, and Charles W. Butler, as a
lodging house proprietor. The story,
which depends more upon treatment
than upon plot, is a pretty romance
built around a struggling poet living
in the proverbial garret and a child
whom he adopted when the boy's I
mother died in poverty. The affec- I
tion inspired by the child in the heart
of the poet when circumstances call
for separation provides a motive for
scenes of deep, sympathetic appeal.
The audience last night was most
liberal in demonstrations of ap
proval.
