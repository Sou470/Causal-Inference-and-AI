The Expanded Plumb Plan.
It was inevitable that the so-called
I'lumb plan should be enlarged be
yond the original bounds of the rail
road field. That pLm, proclaimed last
summer us a means of adjusting the
relations between 'he transportation
?workers and their employers, by
transforming the rail system of the
country to a profit sharing basis, was
obviously only the 'eginning of a
proposal ultimately affecting all
branches of industry.
Briefly stated, the I'lumb plan,
whether applied to the railroads or
to other industries, is to put labor on
an equality with capital and with the
public, giving it a proprietary share
in the products of work and limiting
the earnings of the "owners'' to a
fixed ratio. It is a step toward eom
manal ownership. It is a definite de
parture from the wage system in
favor of one under which the work
men shall receive "that rate of wages
?which in the free markets of the
?world procures the services of that
class of labor and, in addition to
wages, shall receive its share of all
the savings which labor can effect in
the processes of production."
It is announced that this plan will
he presented to the people of the
country as a platform of principles.
The assumption is that labor organi
zations will seek to have the political
parties declare for its adoption i:>
their platforms at the next conven
tion. Failing that, there may be an
independent appeal to the country.
Obviously the presentation of this
scheme at this time is a move toward
its submission as a political issue.
Under the I'lumb plan, whether ap
plied to railroads alone or to other
industries, capital will, in effect, be
loaned at a practically fixed rate of
interest to the "publie'' and the work
ers, the former paying the interest
in the form of prices that insure small
profits and the latter paying the in
terest in the form of deductions from
compensations which are measured by
the volume of business and the profits
earned. This, in the plan itself, is
called a tripartite partnership. The
only change from the present system
in effect is that labor is advanced to
partnership without any increase of
investment, its sole investment in fact
being its services. It is compulsory
profit-sharing. The theory is that
there will never be losses and there
will never be strikes, and there will be
no profiteering. In practice such a
plan may not yield all these highly
desirable results. It is assuredly,
whatever its merits in theory, a move
toward communism which in the light
of present experiments in Russia is
not calculated to appeal strongly to
the people of this country.
