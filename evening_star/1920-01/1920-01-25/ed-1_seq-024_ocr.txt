There is going to be something

vision of the foot-fault rule, adoption
of a new plan of organization and
voting power, approval of a new sys
tem of competition in the national
doubles and a change in the method
of scoring will be recommended by
various committees. All seem des
tined to be adopted except the last
mentioned, which would throw into
discard "love," "deuce" and other long
used terms and put forward the
purely mathematical phraseology of
1?0, etc.
All of these recommendations were
detailed when reports were made a
month or so back and only the one
catling for the change in terms of
scoring has drawn seriously unfavor
able comment. This item is the one
that is- going, to stir up all the' action
at the big confab, and while the pro
posed changes seem assured to be de
feated, there is going to be some in
teresting debate on the subject.
"Lort" Hard to Explain.
Those who seek the change in scor
ing simply are reviving an old cam
paign against the use of the word
'love." which means "nothing" in
tennis. They argue that It is an
absurd bit of phraseology and that it
has been the reason for the charge
of efficiency that has been made against
the game.
But if the flght results in nothing
more it is bound to create curiosity
as to how the various traditional
terms In tennis scoring originated.
Not strange to relate, the toughest
of the lot to explain is the much de
rided "love." It always has been used
for the innocent cipher and no one
has ever been able to satisfactorily
trace its origin. One possible solu
tion, and perhaps the most plausible
offered, is that it came from the old
Scotch word "luff." meaning nothing.
Another word of similar sound is said
to be of far eastern origin and, as an
old form of tennis unquestionably
flourished in the orient, this word
also may have been the parent.
As for "deuce," it is merely the
equivalent of the French word "deux,"
meaning two points have to be won in
succession to make the game. From
time immemorial in tennis it has been
decreed that a tie that resulted after
one player was within one point of a
game meant the necessity of winning
two points in succession.
"Set" is easily explained as a group
or collection of games. But the orig
inal term probably was "sept," as
there originally were seven games in
a set. "Vantage" is. of course, short
for advantage, with its old Italian and
?Yench parallels, "vantagio" and
"avauntage "Volley" comes from "a
la vo\ee," meaning "on the fly."
"Racquet" has been traced to sev
eral sources, the best etymology de
riving it from the Dutch and low
German, "racken," which means ''to
stretch," referring, of course, to the
stretching of strings. But there are
two old words, "rachette" and "ras
quetta," meaning the" palm of the
hand, and these easily may have been
the ancestors of "racquet." For ten
nis originally was played like hand
ball, the earliest strings being stretch
ed between the fingers, before pro
gressing to a frame and handle of!
their own.

"Tennis" itself has many possible
origins. The Greeks had a game
known as "phennis, of which the
I,atin equivalent was "teniludium." or
"the sport of tennis." Both were play
ed by knocking the ball back and
forth. The province of France, where
paume, or hand ball, was extremely
popular, may have given the modern
tennis its name. Then there are those
who argue a derivation from "tenez,"
in the sense of "get ready," but this
appears far-fetched.
Whatever the details of Its his
tory tennis surely is one of the most
ancient of all games, and it now
seems destined to hold the affections
of the world for all time. Chaucer and
Shakespeare both referred to it, and
France recorded the deeds of its
famous players even earlier. Queen
Elisabeth is Baid enjoyed
watching her courtiers at play.
