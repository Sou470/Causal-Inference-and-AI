The Silence of Mr. Hoover.
Herbert Hoover's presidential boom
appears to have attained such pro
portions that the former food ad
ministrator cannot afford much long
er to ignore it. No one has yet ap
peared with authority to say that Mr.
Hoover harbors presidential aspira
tions, but there is nothing in his
career to warrant the assumption that
he is so abnormally constituted as to
find distasteful the prospect of a term
in the White House. If he is not seek
?ing the presidency, at least no one
is called upon to believe that he is
running away from it.
Mr. Hoover's friends have said that
he would not refuse to accept a presi
dential nomination if convinced that
among the people there was a real de
mand that he stand for election. If
that is a correct representation of his
position, the conviction is forced that
Mr. Hoover owes it to the country to
help it reach an intelligent decision
as to whether he is the man best
equipped to take ap the burden when
Wood row Wilson lays it down.
The very large volume of Hoover
sentiment which exists today is based
upon approval of and admiration for
things accomplished which have only
a more or less remote bearing on the
problems with which the next Presi
dent will bo confronted. His execu
tive ability and his very unusual
capacity for getting things, done are
admitted. It is to be assumed that,
were *he elected President, he would
carry into the White House that same
capacity for getting done the things
he thought ought to be done. What
the country does not know, and -what
it is beginning to feel it has a right
to know, is what the things are Mr.
Hoover would seek to have done in
the event of his election to the presi
dency. His capacity for getting things
done might be as hurtful to the coun
try, if he set out to do unwise or
wrong things, as it would be beneficial
if he aimed -at only Tight and wise
things.
It worild be natural and appropriate
if Mr. Hoover should declare what he
thinks on .the great, questions which
will be pressing for solution during
the next few years.

Periodic Promotions.
A plan for periodic efficiency pro
motions for government employes is
proposed by the reclassification com
mission. It is well conceived-and the
hope is that it will be adopted. One
of the most serious faults of the gov
ernment's civil organization is the
failnre systematically to advance em
ployes who demonstrate merit and
who, by their increasing efficiency,
become more valuable.
It has been often urged that in this
respect the British civil service is
superior to the American. In that
system the promotions are antomatie.
The employe enters a certain class at
the outset of his career and advances
by stated degrees from the minimum
to the maximum pay, regardless of
his record?save, of course, that he
must prove capable of doing his work
?and at the top he remains to the
close of his career, which is marked
by retirement. It is thus assured that,
given good behavior, he will rioe to
the top of his class before he is per
mitted to retire on pension.
In one respect the plan for this
service is similar to the British, in
that a maximum is proposed. But in
the American, nnlike the British serv
ice, there are no "water tight com
partments." An employe once enter
ed here can be promoted out of his
class if he proves special aptitude and
uapacity and thus go beyond the
maximum of his grade into the higher
administrative positions. This is un
mistakably better.
This propoaei plan wonld relieve
congestion in the lower ranks of the
service. It woul^ furnish incentive
to eentinuathm in the government's
employ. It would stimulate endeavor,
by holding out the assurance of pro
motion based upon efficiency.
Under the present method there is
no system in promotions. Employes
who serve faithfully and capably are
overJoehed in the occasional distribu
tion of rewards of merit. Favoritism
is alleged in practically all the de
partments and bureaus. Instances
are numerous of men and women with
excellent records as clerks waiting
for years with fading hopes for pro
motions while newer comers are ad
vanced. This breeds discontent and
bad feeling and weakens the morale
of the service.
Better pay, more assured promo
tions and a definite plan of retire
ment are all needed by the govern
ment workers. The service itself
calls for these reforms, and the gov
ernment will benefit from them in i
better work, greater contentment and
enthusiasm on the part of the per
sonnel and a constantly maintained
standard of efficiency. Business or
ganizations have long since learned
this lesson in practical economy and
have applied it with plainly advan
tageous results. The government
Bhould not longer lag behind.

A Bit of Hiatory.
Col. John M. Parker has just won
the democratic nomination for Gov
ernor of Louisiana. The nomination
is equivalent to election. His com
petitor was an old-time democrat.
The triumph, therefore, is of the
straight party variety.
And yet eight years ago Col. Parker
was prominent among the men who
left the democratic party and became
a bull mooser. He was a close per
sonal friend of Theodore Roosevelt,
and subscribed to the Roosevelt
proposition that both of the old par
ties had ontlived their usefulness and
should give way to a new, a clean
and capable organization.
The defeat of Mr. Roosevelt that
year produced no confessed change of
opinion politically on the part of Col*
Parker. He remained committed to
the proposition that a new organiza
tion was necessary to insure the
proper transaction of the country's
business.
So at Chicago in 1916 Col. Parker,
still insisting that the two old par
ties had degenerated into tools of
the "interests," urged the renomina
tion of Mr. Roosevelt for President
on a bull moose platform, and ac
cepted for himself the nomination
for Vice President.
But Mr. Roosevelt had undergone
a change of mind. He could see
nothing in a second try at the presi
dency as a bull mooser. So he re
turned to the republican party, and
supported the republican national
ticket, even taking the stamp for Mr.
Hughes.
Left thus alone and forlorn, Col.
Parker revised his opinion of the
democratic party and returned to it,
taking the stump for Mr. Wilson.
Later, he received recognition.
Mr. Roosevelt, as all know, did not
again leave the republican party, nor
repeat his denunciation of the old or
ganization he had led for seven years,
and which had signally honored him.
He regained its favor rapidly; and
no opinion is more generally accept
ed today than that if now living he
would be far in the lead for its nomi
nation at Chicago in June.

strengthened himself within it. He
has now become its leader in Louis

United States Senate.

citation today, when some prominent
republicans and some prominent
democrats, in a fit of impatience, are
denouncing the old organizations and
demanding the formation of a new.
