In connection with the recently discussed subject of "splash lubrication,"
It Is well to realize that this method Is altogether the most important one In
automobile' practice and Is employed In the lubrication of all the parts of the
change speed gear (transmission) and in that of the transmission parts,
locate*! at the rear axle. Often it is applied to clutch parts as well and. in
general, wherever a collection of moving parts is inclosed in a tight housing.
In the gear case, lubrication is required at the following points: The faces
of the gears, so that they may run together quietly and with little wear,
and also mesh together easily; the slides upon which the gears are moved
In shifting, as well as their locking devices, in order to facilitate speed
changing speeds; the surfaces of the high speed or direct-drive clutch: the
pilot bearing between the engine driven gear shaft and the flnal-drlve shaft;
the stud on which the reverse idle gear runs and the ball or roller bearings
that support the gear shafts. In the rear-axle housing, there are the
drive and differential-gear faces, the inboard axle-shaft bearings (including
thrust bearings); the radial and thrust bearings of the pinion shaft and the
studs that carry the differential pinioTis. The principle relied upon for
the lubrication of both these important element.* is as follows: Sufficient
lubricant is placed in the housing so that one or more of the large gears
constantly dip in it and carty it up as they turn, dropping it on the shafts,
along which it works into the bearings, slides and other parts or. at high
speed, filling the housing fully with flying lubricant, which settles upon and
distributes over all working parts. This method works out well, so long
as the lubricant Is fluid or semi-fluid, but fails disastrously if the lubricant
used is so stiff that it cannot follow the gears, spatter about or be distributed
by capillary action. Oils and greases stiffen with cold and a grade that
may be fluid enough to be constantly distributed over the working parts
during warm weather may become so thick as to fail to do so in wirfter.

Ford Magneto Adjmttment.
i .1. E. S. asks: Will adjusting the
magnets of a Ford magneto closer
to the coils make the engine start
easier? How close should they be set?
Answer: The closer the magnets
and coils run together, the greater
will be the current produced at any
given speed and the lower will be
the cranking speed at which suffi
cient current for ignition purpose-!
is produced. However, it is not safe
to make the clearance too small and
this is usually correctly adjusted,
when cars leave the factory. The
correct clearance between magnets
and coil faces is given as 1-32 inch.
and it is hardly safe to make it less
than thili. The magnets should be
fully up to strength in order to in
sure easy starting. A battery of dry
ceils, connected to the other side of
the ignition switch, makes rapid
cranking unnecessary at starting, so
far as ignition is concerned.
