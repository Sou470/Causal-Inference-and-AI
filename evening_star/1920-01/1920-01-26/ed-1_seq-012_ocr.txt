"The Sewing Girl." by William M.
Paxton of Boston, as the result of
popular ballot, was awarded the so
cal.ed popular prize of the Corcoran
Gallery of Art. Ten thousand ballots
were cast by visitors to the exhibition
during the week of January 12, and,
though there was by no means una
nimity of opinion, the Paxton paint*
ing far outstripped all others in popu
lar favor.
The fact that comparatively few
ballots were cast for the prize-win
ning pictures has been explained on
the basis of an inclination on the part
of the public not to duplicate honors
and also from a lack of appreciation
of specifically technical merits.
Mr. Paxton is an able painter and
has heretofore received awards at the
hands of his fellow artists?prizes
awarded by the most astute of pro
fessional juries. Furthermore, his
works have been purchased by the
leading art museums in this country
and are included in their permanent
collections. The Metropolitan Museum,
New York, has his painting entitled
"Tea Leaves." the Boston Museum has
one of his nudes and the Pennsyl
vania Academy, Philadelphia, his
"Girl Sweeping"; while the Army and
Navy Club, this city, has a mural
deco ration.
It is true, howe*r, that the public
will, as a rule, give first place to a
painting with subjective interest;
that the majority of persons like a
picture which tells a story, and that
the semblance of reality invariably
attracts attention.
Some of the greatest pictures in
the world have told stories, but it
should be remembered that if a story
could be better told in words there is
no reason for seeking pictorial ex
pression. Herein lies the difference
between the Japanese print and the
painting by the great Japanese artist.
The print? invariably told a story;
Wike the poster, it conveyed a message
to the public; it was illustrative; it
sold originally for a few cents. The
painting, on the other hand, was pro
duced from a desire to interpret
beauty, to convey to some beholder
beauty in nature or in color and de
sign which otherwise might not be
discovered. The picture which opens
one's eyes to previously unseen
beauty is a better picture than the
one which represents things "just as
they look." A craftsman can imitate
Nature, but it takes an artist to in
terpret her manifold beauty. Tech
nical achievement does not signify
except when it attains this result;
but it takes technical ability of a
higher order to interpret subtle things
than to imitate reality.
The Chinese porcelains are ex
tremely beautiful, but their beauty is
of a different kind from the Japanese
potteries, with their variety of color
and tones in glaze.
Mr. Paxton's paintings recall more
truly Chinese porcelains than Japa
nese pottery. There is a distinct
kinship between the early paintings
of Edmund C. Tarbell and Thomas W.
Dewing and the latter. No one will
quarrel with the public for having
selected Mr. Paxton's picture as most
meritorious, but it should not be
thought that for this reason the pub
lic and the artist are out of accord
or that it would be better if all
painters painted like Mr. Paxton.
