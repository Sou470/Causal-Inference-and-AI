Letters highly commending the con
tinuation of the savings movement
during 1920 have been received by the
saving division of the Treasury De
partment from Cardinal Gibbons of Bal
timore and Cardinal William O'Connell
of Boston.
In his letter Cardinal Gibbons says:
"Every American citizen should
realize that the problems arising from
the war are individual problems, and
can best be solved by careful, frugal
living and a curtailing of unnecessary
expenses. The prosperity that has
come to us is not ours to be spent
lavishly and without regard to con
sequences, but is to be increased by
consistent saving, thoughtful invest
ment and wise use. In no other way
can we seize upon the opportunity
that is presented us, and thus while
jjjcreasing our own resources, con
tinue to play the good Samaritan to
the suffering people in Europe."
The letter from Cardinal O'Connell
said:
"I shall be very glad to co-operate
in so far as lies within my power with
the savings division of the Treasury
Department in checking the tendency
toward extravagance and financial
carelessness and in encouraging con
sistent saving."
fiood Point. Sny? Mr. I.ewin.
"The point made by Cardinal Gib
bons that the responsibility for the
solution of today's problems lies with
the individual is a most important
one," said William Mather Dewis. di

there is continued public outcry
against the high cost of living, there

mass of the public not only refuses to

but consistently demands the highest
priced articles without regard to the
comparative quality of lower priced
ones.
"It has become an old story In the
retail stores throughout the country
that the public will not buy low
priced articles. The country seems
to be suffering from a peculiar form
of self-hypnosis which makes it turn
from all articles priced in figures
no higher or^only a little higher than
they were before the war. There are
authenticated instances of manufac
turers being forced to take back
goods from retailers, eradicate the

time of Pharaoh that the flood tide
of prosperity will not last forever.
After the seven fat years will oome
seven lean years, and it should not
require another Joseph fo point out
to us that the savings for the lean
years should be piled up during the
yearn of plenty we are now experi
encing.
"The government In continuing the

ury savings certificates, has made a
safe and easy way of increasing sav
ings available to every Bchool child,
to every man and woman who is a
