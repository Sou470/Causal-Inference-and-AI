The American Forestry Association
is to appeal tomorrow to the Senate
committee on forestry against the
action of the agricultural committee
of the House in reducing the appro
priation for forest investigation from
$78,728 to $35,000.
The reduction, the association points
out, would compel the forest service
to abandon the experiment stations
for forest investigation in Colorado,
Idaho, Washington and Arizona; will
cripple, if not stop, forest investiga
tion work in Oregon. California, Utah,
Montana and in all the eastern states
in which there are hardwood forests.
"When the cost of news print paper
is increasing rapidly, owing to the
advance in pulpwood: when lumber
prices are higher than ever before,
when many industries are suffering
from a shortage of wood supplies, it is
not national economy to reduce ap
propriations for work tending to re
duce such costs," says Charles Lathrop
Pack, president of the American For
estry Association. "This, unfortun
ately, is just what the committee on
agriculture in the House has done. Il
is to be hoped the Senate committee
will restore such appropriations and
help to reduce one form of the high
cost of living."
