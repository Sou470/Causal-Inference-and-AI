SEDATE DAYS FOR LENT
FOLLOW CLOSE OF MOST
SPECTACULAR SEASON
The Winter Was Filled With Brilliant Balls and
Pageants and Unusually Interesting Events in
Official Life, While Lent and the Brief Sea
son Following Hold Important DateA.
SALI.IE V. H. PICKETT.
WHAT was perhaps the most spectacular season Washington
socicty ever witnessed closed?or at least subsided?with Ash
Wednesday, when for the first time in many months the pages
of social calendars lay blank. Not bccause of its official social obliga
tions, all of v.hich were promptly met, was the season bri'liant. but
because women, so long denied the wearing of evening clothes and the
display of their jewels, turned to balls of all sizes, ran mad for fancy
dress occasions and catered to carnivals and pageants and just mere
balls with special features as they never did before. Instead of one or
two costume bal's. a play or two. a pageant or two, and so on, they re
peated themselves over and over, until the gamut of historic, mythical
and -tierely fancy characters were repeated until they became as familiar
as government workers in war times.
ABOUT the most vitally interesting of all the season's spectaculai
events was in the rehabilitating of historic men and women as
seen in the historic pageant at the American ball, and those who strug
gled to see the tableau in all its beauty and deplored the fact that hun
dreds of others were just as anxious as they will by special invita
tion or otherwise fill either the Pan-American building or Continental
Hall in two or three weeks, when the seven-real moving picture show
will reveal society folk to themselves as others see them?when they
are on parade. The placing of the groups is a matter of elaborate care,
and the treat will indeed be a rare one.
THE pageant of the season, on Ash Wednesday, which prefaced the
ball, when jesters danced with queens, and lords, ladies, vassals
and serfs?and big American Indian chiefs?mingled in one gay whirl,
added another never-to-be-forgotten touch of beauty to the season, and
even during Lent there will be a few small dancing parties for various
organizations. Not the least interesting feature of the Mardi Gras
ball was the booth in the corner of the ballroom where was arranged
a hoard of beautiful things created in Neighborhood House, which was
the beneficiary of the ball. Scarcely a handcraft but had its example
there.
THE death of Count Goran Rosen, second secretary of the Swedish
legation, early in the week cast a shadow over society, for he was
a particularly brilliant and popular diplomat. Not only the diplomatic
corps -but society folk in large numbers will pay their last tribute of
respect to Count Rosen, when at high noon tomorrow memorial cere
monies in his honor wilUbe held in St. John's Episcopal Church?the
Church of the Presidents?on Lafayette Square. The State Department
arranged the event, and later on the remains of the count will be taken
to Sweden, where his mother, Countess Rosen, and his brothers and
sisters live on their splendid estates.
AN event of contrasting interest in the diplomatic corps was the wed
ding on Thursday of Senorita Maria Alfaro and Senor Carrizosa,
when, filling St. Patrick's Church, was a notable company to witness
the ceremony and later to attend the wedding breakfast at the New
Willard. In the company were the acting secretary of state and Mrs.
Polk, the ambassador of Spain and Senora de Riano, the ambassador
of Argentina and Mme. Le Breton, the ambassador of Peru and Senora
de Pezet, the latter acting as a witness at the wedding: many of the
foreign ministers and their wives, and others from official and resident
society, nearly a hundred guests being seated at the small flower
decked tables.
A RESUME of the closing events of the season would indeed be in
complete without mention of the splendidly arranged annual break
fast given by the Congressional Club, and the first Lenten entertain
ment in the club, when on Friday afternoon, preceding the regular tea,
Mrs. Carrie Jacobs Bond was the gaest of honor, and charmed tne guests
with a recital of her own composTffons, giving a history of each, "even
"The Old Sweet Way," and prefacing the entire program with a heart-to
heart talk, a beautiful, philosophic sermon, which did every one a world
Of good. The Rev. Robert J. Burdette calls Mrs. Jacobs Bond the Riley
of the world of song and melody, and as she adjusted the poetical
lines to the grace of her musir every one agreed with him. But Mrs.
Jacobs Boad is not only a song writer and publisher, with a veritable
fortune in a single song, but she is as well a humanitarian, and it was
?he who planted the first American flag in France. She gave her time,
money and talents for the happiness and welfare of the soldier, and
when she held her little reception in the Congressional Club it was
that which came uppermost in the thoughts of the women who sent
their heroes to war.
MANY dinner parties of note are scheduled for this week, and there
will be one or two large receptions and several tea parties on
the program, but the real brilliancy of the season has passed, and will
not be revived until the little season, or post-Lenten season, comes on.
