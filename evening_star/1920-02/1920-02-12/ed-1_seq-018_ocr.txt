62 Q. My income for 1919. exclusive of the time while in service, was
approximately $1,500. My wife, as a public school teacher, had an Income of
$900. Am I obliged to pay a federal tax? Is not my wife's income exempt?
62 A. Your wife's income is exempt from federal taxation. Your income
while in the military or naval service of the government up to $3,500 is also
exempt. Accordingly, your total taxable income appears to be $1,500. If you
live with your wife, as I assume you do. you must neither file a return nor
pay a tax.
63 Q. I would be very much obliged for information regarding the fol
lowing: I am a hotel clerk, and besides my salary the manager makes me a
present of $200 per year; also many of the guests make me cash presents.
In addition to the foregoing. I also receive commissions on automobiles and
on sub-rentals. Am anxious to know how I am to make my return?
63 A. Your return should include your salary as a hotel clerk and com
missions on automobiles and sub-rentals. Presents received by you are not
subject to tax. Waiters are taxable on the tips received by them, but I am
of the opinion that the gratuities received by hotel clerks are not in the same
category as waiters' tips.
64 Q. I am a school teacher, resident in New York. My salary for 1919
was $1,600. My interest from savings bank account was $300. My sister,
who keeps house, had an income of $85 from her savings bank account for
the year. This sister is not incapacitated in any way. but never did any
thing else than take care of the house and never had any other income.
Our family consists of another younger sister, whose income Was $1,672. She
expects to fill? a return and pay the tax.
Could I. as the oldest of the family, and unmarried, be considered the
head of the family and claim an exemption of $2,000?
I 64 A. You are not the head of the family and can claim exemption of
only $1,000. This should cause you no worry, however, because your salary
is exempt. You must neither file a return nor pay a tax.
65 Q. 1 am single and earned during 1919 $1,313.60. How much is my
tax and where do I pay it?
65 A. You are subject to a tax of $12.54. Your tax return should be filed
at the office of the collector of internal revenue.
66 Q. I would be pleased to learn from you. what percentage of wear
and tear and loss is allowed in a hotel of about 100 rooms on linens, carpits
and bed clothes. Also, what allowance is made for a piece of business prop
erty valued at $200,000? Is a person allowed anything for paying off mort
gages on real estate?
66 A. I should suggest that the difference between the value of your
linens and bed clothes at the end of the year and the corresponding value at
the beginning of the year, plus purchases during the year, would be a fair
means of determining the allowable deduction due to use and loss. Your ex
perience regarding the life of carpets should be the best judge. My guess is
that about one-third to one-half of the total cost would be a fair measure of
depreciation. Depreciation on real estate is based on the actual cost of the
building (exclusive of yie land). Such depreciation varies from 2 to 5 per
cent, depending on the material employed in the construction of the building,
exposure to the elements, etc. If the building was acquired prior to March 1,
1913, the values as of that date, rather than the cost, must be employed.
The payments on account ot' mortgage do not constitute tax factors.
