ACQUISITION OF DAVENPORT SHOULD STRENGTHEN TEAM.
Acquisition of Dave Davenport from St. Louis for cash will
strengthen the Washington pitching staff beyond a doubt. Davenport,
who is about the tallest pitcher in base ball today, standing six feet
five inches, in his socks, and weighing close to 300 pounds, has the
proverbial "world of stuff" in the way of speed and curves, but never
has been able to get along with either Business Manager Quinn or
Pilot Burke of the Browns. Dave has an odd disposition, and his
habits have been far from exemplary, but friends of the attenuated
hurler maintain tbat the eighteenth amendment should make a world
beater of him. Quitin today said he would not be surprised to see
Davenport come through in great shape for Griff, attributing his failure
to shine in St. Louis to the fact that he needed a change of scenery.
Quinn has a high regard for Davenport's native ability, and cited his
performance of three seasons back, when Davenport worked in nearly
fifty games, and with a seventh place club won seventeen games while
losing an equal number.
Davenport has been playing base ball since 1910. and is twenty
eight years old. In 1912 and 1913 he worked in the Texas League, and
the following season had signed up with Cincinnati during August.
Then came the I-ederal League in 1914, and Davenport lost little time
jumping to the St. Louis Cards, where lie won ten games and lost fif-?
teen. He did better in 1915. winning twenty-two games for the Cards
and losing eighteen.
Peace was then patched up. and Davenport was assigned to the
Browns in 1915, and has baen with St. Louis ever since. He did pretty
well his first season with the Browns, winning thirteen and losing
eleven games. Davenport showed improved form in 1917. doing a lot
of work and hanging up a record of seventeen victories and the same
number of defeats. Ten wins and eleven defeats came his way in
1918, when he was in thirty-one battles altogether. Last year Daven
port became dissatisfied with his surroundings, and his work fell off,
but it is expected that a change of clubs will do him a world of good.
In announcing the raise of prices the moguls asserted they were
compelled to take action because of the increased cost of everything
necessary to conduct the sport, and pointed out that the increases are
far less than the boosts in prices made for every other line of amuse
ment. Owing to the different arrangement of the stands in various
cities, all will not be affected exactly alike, however. A majority of
the parks are laid out similar to that in Washington, and there a
bleacher patrons hereafter will have to pay 50 cents, includnig war tax.
instead of 30 cents as formerly, the price for the seat really being 45
cents, the added nickel going to Uncle Sam. Pavilion seats will cost
75 cents, 7 cents of it representing the federal levy. For the past two
seasons these seat* cost 55 cents, the increase in this instance also
amounting to 20 cents. All grandstand accommodations at Georgia
avenue henceforth will be SI. 91 cents for the seat and 9 cents tax.
This is a net increase of 15 cents, the price formerly being 85 cents.
The box seat prices were left to the option of the various clubs. In
Washington they will cost $1.25. as against $1.10 charged heretofore.
GRIFFITH MAKES ONLY IMPORTANT DEAL.
Washington's purchase of Davenport, so far as is known, is the
only deal of any importance engineered by the American Leaguers dur
ing their sojourn here. Kid Gleason of the White Sox tried to talk
trade with Connie Mack and other magnates, but nothing developed.
It was announced that Charley O'Leary, the old Detroit shortstop, has
been signed by Miller Huggins as coach for the Yankees, and Harry
Stevenson has been taken on as trainer for the White Sox. Outfielder
Chick Shorten of the Tigers is said to have enrolled in the holdout
brigade, along with Ben Dyer, utility infielder.
For a newcomer in base ball, Billy Richardson has made a most
auspicious debut, for it is he who is credited with paving the way for
the compromise that finally resulted in the signing of a peace pact in
the American League. That it is a genuine peace is evident from the
fact that following the reconciliation Ban Johnson shook hands with
his former arch enemy, Charley Comiskey, and later was seen in a
middle of a conversation with Col. Huston and Harry Frazee. Rich
ardson's role was an uninspired one. When the magnates entered their
first session it was apparent to all the veteran campaigners that the
factions were as far apart as ever. The Washington vice president is
a business man. and could not understand why, with so much valuable
property at stake, the club owners should permit what appeared to be
petty considerations to interfere with the proper safeguarding of their
interests. He said as much, fight out in meeting, and Col. Ruppert,
gratified at finding a man in the camp of the opposition willing to
listen to his grievances, all of which were an old story to the others,
cornered the Washington magnate and recited for his receptive ears'
the "impositions" hp had been subjected to. It mattered little that Clark
Griffith and not Richardson did the voting for the Washington club,
for the attitude assumed by the part owner of the Nationals provided
the opening wedge for the reconciliation that eventually was brought
about. Not a bad piece of work for a fledgling in base ball politics.
