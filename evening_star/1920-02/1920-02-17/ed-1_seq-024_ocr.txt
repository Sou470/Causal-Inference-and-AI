Unnecessary Motions by Pitcher Expected
Materially to Shorten Time of Contests.
BY DENMAN THOMPSON.
One of the expected effects of the overhauling given the rules at
the base ball pow-woiv in Chicago last week will he the speeding up of
games by the elimination of all unnecessary motions 011 the part of the
pitcher. It has been customary in recent years, with the spitter, shiner.
mud ball and other varieties of trick box work finding increasing
favor, for a hurler to spend about half pf his time on the rubber in the j
preparation of the ball for delivery. It has been the rule, rather than
the exception, for a flinger upon receipt of the ball, especially a new
one, to seek a particular clump of dirt or grass, sojnetimes several feet j
distant, to alter its surface. Much revolving of the sphere in his glove
usually followed, and then there was a dip into the rear pocket for a
touch of resin, a more or less lengthy process of rubbing the pill on j
his trouser leg or a careful apointing with saliva. Oftentimes these
movements meant nothing so tar as actual tinkering with the ball was
concerned. In the case of the spitter many were bluffed and compara- 1
tively few thrown, and so it was with the other brands, but the psycho
logical effect was what was sought, and it takes iu>t as much time to
simulate practice of the tricks as it does to actually attempt them.
Both real and phoney tinkering arc to be barred under the re
vised rules. For instance, it is stipulated in one explanatory para
graph inserted into the code
"After the pitcher has taken position, with both hand.-, holding
the ball in front of him. he cannot take either hand off the ball except
in the act of delivering the ball to the batsman or in throwing to
bases." It is further provided that a pitcher cannot step off the rubber
after being in position to dry his hands, rub his eyes or for other rea
sons. unless in the judgment of the umpire it is a legitimate action, not
understood or interpreted by the arbiter as intended to decei\e a base
runner.
ALL MANIPULATION OF BALL BANNED.
Another rule is amended by adding the following new section
"At no time during the progress of the game shall the pitcher be
allowed to il) apply a foreign substance of any kind to the ball: (2)
expectorate either 011 the ball or his glove: to rub the ball on his
glove, person or clothing, or (4) to deface the ball in any manner or
to deliver what is called the shine ball, spit bail, mud ball. For viola
tion of any provision of this rule the pitcher shall be ordered from the
gtme. and be barred from participation in any championship contest j
for a period of ten days." In the case of spit ball pitchers it is stipu
lated they shall be exempt from the operation of this rule, but only tor
this year. y
Another rule amendment provides: j
"In the event of the ball being intentionally discolored by any
player, either by rubbing it with the soil, or by applying resin, para
ffin. licorice or any other foreign substance to it. or otherwise inten
tionally damaging or roughening the sahie with sand or emery paper or
other substance, the umpire shall forthwith demand the return of that
ball, and substitute for it another legal ball, and the offending player
shall be disbarred from further participation in the game. If, how
ever. the umpire cannot detcct the violator of this rule, and the ball
is delivered to the bat by the pitcher, then the latter shall be at once I
removed from the game, and as an additional penalty shall be auto
matically suspended for a period of ten days."
Pretty stringent rules, these, but essential to attain the objects
sought, one of which is the elimination of unnecessary delay in playing I
a game. Not only will the average contest be reduced in time con-
sumed from close to two hburs to a little more than an our and a half. I
the games will be improved by the assurance of cieaner fielding. Wild
heaves due to slippery balls will be known no more; the spectacle of
an infielder getting hold of a saliva-decorated sphere and firing it over
a baseman's head no longer will be witnessed.
