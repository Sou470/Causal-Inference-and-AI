Two radical revisions in the rules were accomplished by the joint
committee yesterday in' addition to several of minor importance, but
not without a struggle that lasted well into the night. The regulations
changed include the elimination of all freak pitching and measures
to curb the intentional passing of a batsman. Griffith led the fight
for both and had to overcome some sturdy opposition to do it, espe
cially from Hank O'Day and Bill Klem, the veteran umpires of the
National League. The ban on freak deliveries includes all varieties,
the pitcher and other players being prohibited from rubbing the ball
on the ground, their uniforms, gloves or their persons and bars the ap
plication of any foreign substance, the penalty for violation being ten
days' suspension for the player guilty of breaking the rule and the
pitcher to suffer in case the identity of the guilty player cannot be
determined. An exception is made in the case of the spit ball. Pitch

execRtives /the nanjes of pitchers claiming the one-year exemption'
ten days prior to the opening of the season.
Curbing the issuance of intentional passes will be accomplished
by stipulating that a catcher cannot step out to either side of his posi
tion directly in back of the plate, until after the ball has left the
pitcer's hand, violation of this rule entitling all base runners to ad
vance one base. This rule is not expected to entirely eliminate pur
poseful walks, but it will tend greatly to discourage them and will
prevent the catcher from deliberately stepping far out to the side and
advertising the fact, that the batsman is being intentionally franked
to first. Incidentally, it will not be such an easy matter as formerly
for a hurler to toss four wide ones without putting a couple within

The dinger needs a target to shoot at in tossing 'em wide just as in
