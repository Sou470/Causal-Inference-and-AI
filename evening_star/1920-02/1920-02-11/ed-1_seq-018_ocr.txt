CHICAGO. February 11.?The war is over. After a session that
lasted practicallv all of yesterday, last night and until an early hour
this morning tlie club owners of the American League reached an
agreement that is expected to assure only peace and harmony in the
organization instead of the bickering and brawling that has rent the
league since the middle of last season, when the suspension of Carl
Mays precipitated the biggest fight the American League ever wit
nessed, and one that threatened the entire fabric of organized base
b?ll-
The settlement of this row was hailed by all parties thereto with
the greatest satisfaction, and will be heartily welcomed by followers
of the national pastime who have become weary of the game as
played in the courts. How peace was attained is concisely told in the
statement issued by the magnates at the end of their strenuous day.
Here it is:
"The difficulties heretofore existing between the members and
the association or any of its officers have been thoroughly settled once
and for all.
"The meeting unanimously approved and adopted resolutions to
carry out the following:
"No. 1?The dismissal of all litigation instituted by the New York
club.
"No. 2?The reinstatement of Carl Mays as a member of the New
York club.
"No. 3?The recommendation to the national commission to award
third place in the American League race to New York and third prize
money to the New York players.
_ No. A?The appointment of a committee, to consist of Col. Jacob
Ryppert of New York and Clark Griffith of Washington, to act as a
reviewing board with final and binding powers to review any penal
ties or fines in excess of $100 or ten days' suspension or any a<:t of
any member in excess of his constitutional rights as a member of the
association. In case the members of the reviewing board arc unable
to agree, a federal judge in Chicago will be called upon to decide the
question submitted."
The peace protocol cannot be accounted a complete victory for
either faction. Both made concessions. The insurgents yielded to the
extent of not insisting upon the elimination of Ban Johnson from
base ball and in consenting to the dismissal of all the numerous law
suits they have filed against Johnson and his supporters. The loyal
faction consented to the reinstatement of Mays, the awarding of third
place to the Yankees and agreed to the curbing of Ban Johnson's
power.
The appointment of Clark Griffith as one of the two members of
the arbitration committee may be regarded as a tribute to the in
tegrity and fairmindedness of the Washington president. It assures
a square-deal policy prevailing in all cases coming before that body.
The Chicago judge named to decide questions Griffith and Kuppert
fail to agree on will, it is assumed, be Kenesaw Mountain Landis.
whose name has been prominently mentioned for the post of national
commission chairman.

NO PLAYERS TO BE SOLD AFTER JULY 1.
In addition to ratifying its schedule, the American League adopted
the proposal made by Clark Griffith to further restrict the sale or trade
of players during the playing season, setting July 1 as the time limit
for exchanges of talent of any nature. Griff would like to bar the prac
tice of swapping entirely, except by the waiver route, and had hoped
that June 1 or June 15 would be agreed upon, but is satisfied at the
action taken, as it is a step in the right direction. The penalty for
violation of this rule is that all games in which players illegally ob
tained participate shall be declared forfeited to the opposing club.
Tampering with players hereafter will cost the guilty persons a fine of
$1,000 for the first offense, with expulsion the penalty for a repetition
of it. A rule was adopted making waiver requests non-recallable after
two days from the time a club has put in its claim, and waivers on
players who ha, e been in the league five or more years cannot be re
called at all. It was stipulated that hereafter all postponed games
must be played oil, at the park of the visiting club, if necessary, and
that in the ease of all postponements the home club must specify
whether the postponed game is to be played off in the first or second
series and give forty-eight hours' notice.
The National Leaguers had a very orderly session and finished up
their business in short order. They adopted their schedule as a matter
of form, and raised their waiver price from $1,500 to $2,000. President
Heydler gave out his list of umpires, which contains the name of one
newcomer. Fyfe. He is expected to feel at ease working with the
drum corps in session daily in the bleachers. Fyfe had some expe
rience in the Federal League, and guessed all last season in the Michi
gan circuit. The parent organization refused to follow the lead of the
American League in setting July 1 as the time limit for the selling or
trading of players. Six votes were needed and lacking, so August 20
will continue to mark the end of the swapping season. Thirty-five
as the limit of players a club may carry during the off-season will
be returned to, with twenty-five the limit during the season.
The elimination of freak pitching accomplished by the rival circuit
was concurred in with the difference that all spit-ball pitchers in the
National League will be allowed the year of grace instead of no more
than two to a club, as in the American. There are a total of only
twelve hurlers using the moist delivery in Heydler's wheel. Pitts

and Mitchell. Douglas of the Giants, Hogg of the Phillies and Fisher
of the Reds also come under this heading. The American League has
even fewer of real merit. The Griffmen will not be affected by the
rule at all, and neither will the Tigers. About the only top-notch
manipulators of the damp shoots are Shocker and Sothoron of the

that I can intelligently discuss the

If the umpire call a game at any
time after five innings have been
completed the score shall be that of
the last equal innings played, except
that if the side second at bat shall
have scored in an unequal number
of innings, or before the comple

one run more than the side first at

has made In five innings, then it is a
legal game and scores as made stand.
The last part of the rule is always
clear. We will assume Boston and
Cleveland are playing at Cleveland. In
the first inning Cleveland scores a run.
In five times at bat Boston fails to
score. When Cleveland comes to bat
for the last of the fifth rain causes the
umpire to suspend play and finally calls
the game. It is a legal game, of course,

Several years ago the St. Louis club

score of that ball game? The papers

istician, to dig up .the error. If you

Slsler was entitled to his homer and

We will assume the score is 2 to 0
