According to Col. John S. Chambers,
chief of the personnel division of the
quartermaster general's office, the
United States Army was twenty years
ahead of the country at large in abol
ishing strong drink. In a special report
on "the passing of John Barleycorn,
from the service" he shows that liquor
was part of the Army ration up to the
early part of the civil war. In 18#3.
however, due to the restricted supply
available, he says, only one gill per
man daily was allowed for issue in
cases of excessive, fatigue or severe
exposure. After the close of the civil
war the Issue of liquor as part of the
ration was abolished by failure to in
clude its provisions in the appropria
tions for substistence of the Army.
Thereafter the history of the career of
liquor in the Army wts intimately
connected with the gradual evolution
of the post exchange. In 1882 Con
gress required the inspector general
of the Army to prepare a list of ar
ticles to be sold by "sutlers" to offi
cers and soldiers of the volunteer
service. That list included all articles
of ordinary merchandise and showed
that the sale of intoxicating liquors
was prohibited. Following the civil
war the Army was scattered mostly at
isolated posts in what was then the
"great west." and the vicinity of the
posts became cluttered with saloons,
gambling joints and similar places
designed to separate the soldier from
his pay. To remedy that situation
Congress In 1870 authorized the main
tenance of one or more trading estab
lishments at frontier posts by persons
appointed by the Secretary of War. It
also was provided that these traders
ahould be under the protection and
military control of the commanding
officer as "camp followers." Then grew
tip a custom of seling light wines
and beers, which In tnose days were

liquors was the chief source of reve
nue of the traders after 1868, as an en
terprising commissary general sup
plied the soldiers with most of the
ordinary articles of merchandise.
"That," said Col. Chambers, "was
the real beginning of our post com
missaries. The name of the post sut
ters was gradually changed to that
of post traders and all other traders
were prohibited from doing business
on military reservations. Orders were
issued In 1889 authorising the estab
lishment of military canteens where
there wore not post traders. The sale
?r use of ardent spirits in the can
teen was strictly prohibited, but light
wines and beer were authorized to be
sold therein by the drink, on week

such beverages within the post lim
its bad the effect of preventing the

were various arguments, pro and con.

separate bill for the same purpose.
In the Senate, the opposition to the

in past years.

cember 31, 1920. But the House adopt

be eligible for transfer to other po
sitions under the control of the Treas
ury Department, or to positions in
other departments.
The subtreasuries were originally
established as depositories not only ot
trust funds, but of all general funds
of the government. Gradually and Tor
many years, their depository func
tions have been withdrawn, and even
before the federal reserve system was
adopted most of their functions had
been (absorbed by the national banks.
The abolition of the subtreasuries will
be a real saving to the government.
