CHICAGO. February 9.?'What probably will be the biggest gath
ering of base ball men in years gets under way today, and will continue
to hold the boards until the latter^art of the week. Every club owner
and league official and a majority of the managers of the major cir
cuits will be represented, and practically every minor league will have
a delegate, with an unusual number of players of varying degrees ot
greatness, in addition to a small army of scribes.
With nearly every angle and aspect of the national pastime due
for an airing, the outlook is for a momentous week. How much of the
large grist of business will be disposed of is problematical, but that
there will be plenty to occupy the attention of the leaders of the sport
is apparent from a perusal of the docket. Here are some of the things
down on the calendar to be attempted

ALL BUSINESS UNLIKELY TO BE FINISHED.
It is improbable that all the business on the program will be
accomplished, but it will be attempted, and from the very nature of
some of the items on the list turmoil and strife promise to be very
much in evidence. Prospects are excellent, for instance, that the row
precipitated by the insurgent faction of the American League will be
lanned to fever heat when Messrs. Ruppert, Huston, Frazee and Comis
key arrive on the scene in the wake of that fresh batch of court pro
ceedings instituted against President Johnson and magnates backing
him. Little hope is entertained in any quarter that this trouble can
be ironed out now. Too much has been said and done by both fac
tions to make an adjustment anything but a Herculean task. Besides,
the lawyers need the money.
Then there is the question of the commission chairmanship. No
solution of this question can be looked for without a merry battle.
The National League committeemen have several names to suggest
and the American League members also will present a number of nomi
nees. Each will view with suspicion any move made by the other,
fearing trickery on the part of the rival, and one of these camps is
itself divided, with each clique bound to oppose any measure favored
by the other.
All in all. this promises to be the "fightenest" base ball convention
ever held, in addition to being the largest. As a matter of fact, the
scrapping already is under way, verbal guns of large caliber having
been tired at the preliminary meetings of the rules committee yester
day. Th is gathering, called for the purpose of a short, informal dis
cussion of the regulations on which formal recommendations for re
vision are to be made at the meeting today^occupied more than four
hours.

man on the part of the catcher shall entitle the batsman to take first
and base runners to move up a notch. It was unanimously agreed, as
has been forecast, to eliminate spitters. shiners and other freak de
liveries. with the likelihood that suspension and a heavy tine will be
stipulated for the very first offense.
The question of legislating the intentional pass out of existence
provoked heated arguments. All were in accord that the deliberate
walking of a batsman in a pinch is unsportsmanlike and should be
abolished, hut the\ got nowhere in trying to determine what measures
should be adopted to end the practice. The umpires contend it would
be almost impossible for them to determine, in many instances,
whether a smart pitcher with good control is purposely trying to walk
a hitter, and the penalty that should be inflicted in case a pitcher is
adjudged guilty of intentionally walking a man also found the mem
bers greatly at odds.
