WIRELESS TELEPHONE
EXHIBIT IS MADE HERE
National Research Council Offers
Models Illustrating Advance
of Hadio Science.
An interesting exhibit of the wire
less telephone, including the earliest
device for recording sound waves and
the last word in wireless telephonic
inventions, was opened today in the
exhibition rooms of the National Re
search Council. 1201 16th street,
where it will remain indefinitely.
I Members of the diplomatic corps
and Congress attended the opening.
The publio is invited to visit the ex
hibit and witness the motion pctures
which are shown at 10 a.m. and 12 m.
and 2 and 4 p.m.
The exhibit is under the direction
of Dr. K. W. King, engineer of the
Western Electric Company, and H. E.
Howe, vice chairman of the division
of research extension of the National
Research Council.
The exhibit was prepared by the
i American Telephone and Telegraph
Company and the Western Electric
Company. It shows not only various
forms of the wireless telephone in op
eration. but also many working models
illustrating scientific discoveries upon
which the entire radio art is founded.
In fact, the exhibit has been designed
to impart to the visitor a broad and
clear view of the fundamental electrical
discoveries without which there could be
no wireless telegraph or telephone.
Two particularly noteworthy models
show Faraday's discovery of the first
known relation between light and
electricity, and Hertz's discovery of
the electrical character of light. The
operation of the "vacuum tube" in its
several capacities is also featured
The exhibit is the first of a series
j which the national council is arrang
I ing with a view to awakening popular
interest in scientific research as con
trasted with practical invention. It is
understood that each exhibit will il
lustrate some modern adaptation of
science to industry which has re
sulted in widespread public benefit.
The campaign of the research coun
cil contemplates the display of these
exhibits in the principal cities of the
country.

Shall the wage schedules for the

aries minus the bonus, or on the base
of the present statutory salary, plus
the bonus?
There is no doubt that the aver
age government employe here regards
the bonus which he is receiving as an
I integral part of his salary. If there
I was any disposition at first on the
i part of employes "To look upon the
bonus as a gift, a "good." pure and

Statement* at Hearing*.
At hearings before the reclassifica
tion commission last November and
December, the various groups of fed
eral employes presented figures to
show that even with the bonus they
are largely underpaid.
I Now the question confronting the re
classification commission, in its final
decisions as to wage schedules, upon
which it is engaged at this time, has
much to do with the present bonus
being given the federal clerks and
other workers. This question is en
tirely separate and different from that
involved in the consideration of the
suggestion made that basic salaries
be established, with a bonus to vary
year by year, according to the cost
of living.
The pressing problem of the bonus
is whether to consider the employes'
present salaries as simply the statu
tory salaries or the salary plus the
bonus. If a clerk is down on Uncle
Sam's wage book for $1,200, is it fair,
in discussing his salary, to say that
he is drawing $1,200 only. when, as a
matter of fact, lie is drawing $1,440?
Opinion* on Subject Vary.
Divergence of opinion upon this
j question has arisen in more than one
j quarter. This note of different start
ling points has been struck many times
in the halls of Congress.
"What is the salary of such-and
such a clerk?" one congressman asks.
"Twelve hundred dollars a year,"
comes the answer.
"Does that include the bonus?" is
asked.
"Oh, no," comes the reply.
"Then his salary is $1,440," is the
answer.
And "Oh. no." or "Oh. yes." is the
forthcoming answer, according to the
viewpoint of the answerer.
In the considerations of the re
classification commission some such
dialogue as the above has come up,
with the result that a general consid
eration of the problem involved arose.

It will make some difference to the
government employes, it is believed,
which base is taken to work from in
setting up the final salary schedules
to be presented to Congress next
March.
Stress is to be placed, however, on
the idea that perhaps no great final
difference will result no matter which
base is taken. If the statutory salary
I merely is taken as a base, it is gener
ally believed that a higher increase
j of salaries will have to be given than
j if the present salary plus its present
I bonus is taken as the base. The final
result, in both cases, would be ap
I proximate.
It is generally believed among the
clerks themselves that if the salary
plus the bonus is taken as the base,
they will fare better in the end than
if the other base is used.
