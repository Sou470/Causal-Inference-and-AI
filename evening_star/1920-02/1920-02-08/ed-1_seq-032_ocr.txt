Labor's Two Lists.
In a few days two lists will be pub
lished by the American Federation of
Labor, one giving the names of mem
bers of Congress who are rated as
favorable to labor and the other nam
ing those who are rated otherwise.
Members of the federation and all
other labor bodies will be urged to j
j work for the re-election, if nominated,
of the men who are in the first list and
[against those who are named in the
second, and to be on guard in the cam
paign against all others who are un
friendly to labor, or to work for the
election of those who are friendly. In
other words, labor enters the campaign
to elect, if possible, a Congress that
will vot? measures favorable to labor
interests.
This is the most ambitious political
move ever made by organized labor in
the field of polities. Heretofore indi
vidual candidates have been favored
here and there by the unionR and
others have been the object of adverse
activity. But never before has the en
tire Congress been put on the grill, so
to speak, or the nation-wide field been
entered.
Publication of the lists is only fair,
both to labor and to the legislators and
candidates. This is a wise move in the
political game that labor is about to
play. Everybody will be put on warn
ing. If a member of either house has
incurred the opposition of labor by his
vote he can explain or justify his po
sition. Those who are rated as friendly
can do the same. Proscription works
both ways. It may make friends for
the proscribed, as well as opponents, j
Certain issues are bound to be joined
in this campaign bearing upon the in
terests of organized labor. The two
principal parties will be called upon
to declare themselves at Chicago and
San Franeisco on such matters as. the
future of the railroads, the national
ization of which labor demands, and
perhaps on the question of strike-pre
venting methods. It will be interest
ing to observe how the candidacies of
men rated as friendly, named by a
party which does not fully meet the
wishes of labor in these and other re
spects, will fare in the polling.
Heretofore it has been impossible to
"deliver" the labor vote in the United
States to any party, or to direct it
bodily against either side in the na
tional political equation. This present
move is not partisan, as between par
ties. It is partisan to labor. Under
the scrutiny that the American Federa
tion of Labor undertakes to direct at
the personnel of the two parties there
can be no dodging. Labor has elected
to go to the polls this year and the re
sult will be momentous to individuals
tnd perhaps to the nation.

Tapping on wood" is an old super
stition.
The rite is done after this fashion:
Whenever one says anything that
seems a bit bragging, especially as to
ones present good health, luck or
what-not, the sayer must run to wood
and tap vigorously thereon several
times, in order to allay the turn of
fortune which will follow, it is said
unless the tapping is conscientiously
performed.
This is a survival of strange, old
ways of thinking, undoubtedly, in the
childhood of the race, when people
thought that jealous gods watched
their every movement, and were en
vious of too much happiness on the
part of mortals. Let a man boast of
I his good health, the god?probably
Pan, the god of nature, whose home
I was In the woods?would surely hear
I him and send him ill health to" mock
him.
So down through the apes has come
the superstition of "tapping on wood."
It is done blindly nowadays, usually
on a chair, or table, or door?any
thing wooden, sometimes even on
heads as a jest. People have forgot
ten its origin, but conscientiously per
form the rite.
Since the world has turned from
forest habitation to apartment houses,
it is not strange that most of the
tapping is done on wood worked into
the shape of table, door or chair.
Perhaps the antiquity of the thinx
explains the perplexity of two women
who were walking along 16th street
one day last week.

ly." said one girl.
