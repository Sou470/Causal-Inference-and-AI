Criminal Negligence at Mus
cle Shoals and Powder Plant
at Nashville Alleged.
Charges of wholesale incompetence,
waste, criminal negligence and graft
in connection with the construction j
and operation of two government war j
plants?the air nitrate plant at Mus- j
cle Shoals, Ala., and the Old Hickory i
powder plant at Nashville, Tenn., were
placed before the special House com
mittee investigating war expenditures
by Mark H. Kvans of Arcadia, Calif.,
formerly of the military intelligence
sefvice.
Dumping a hamper full of type
written reports and other data on the
table before the committee, Mr. Kvans
gave a graphic report of conditions
as he had observed and reported to
Washington and to local representa
tives of the Department of Justice?
until he was called ofT.
He said he hafl eer? to Washington
123 affidavits supporting his charges
regarding the conditions as he saw
them at Muscle Shoals during thir
teen months he was detailed there on
special government service, but had
not been able to ascertain that any
body had been haled into court.
Car* Idle When Moat Needed.
While a cry was going up from all
quarters for cars to move munitions
and foodstuffs, 7.823 cars stood idle
at the plant from ten to twenty days,
lie said, with nobody seeming to care
whether they were unloaded. De
murrage charges ranged from $3 to
$300, and records showed, the wit
ness declared, that failure to unload
material cost the government $81,000
during the first six months of 1918.
"There was absolutely no reason for
keeping the cars standing there."
Evans said. "It was criminal negli
gence on the part of the contractors."
Muj Surplus Saddle Horses.
Evans said fully 300 saddle horses
were kept in readiness for service at
the plant when there was no demand
for them. The old system of trying
to make two blades of grass grow
where one had grown before was re- i
versed in the case of mulesi according
to Evans, for at this plant six were
hitched up to haul a load hardly big I
enough for two and anybody who
wanted a horse or an automobile got I
one.
"Thousands of automobiles and j
trucks were used to haul employes 1
back and forth from the plant," Evans j
said. "No effort was made to have
this traffic handled by the street'ra.il- i
way people, and the purpose was
plain. The builders wanted to buy
all the equipment they could because
on every dollar spent in constructing
the plant they got a commission by
the cost-plus plan.
Teams Hidden From Observers.
"There were so many teams that
orders were given to keep them on
the move before somebody complain
ed, and at one time a flock of eighteen
were sent to the bushes, 'way out by
the pest house and away from view.
Bob Oliver, a subcontractor, was paid
$800 for bossing teams, including his
own. He was paid for his mules and
then paid for bossing them.
"Every foreman drawing $150 a
month?and you didn't have to be
much of a man to draw it?got an
automobile. Hundreds of trips charged
to the government were mere Joy
rides."
Cost of Powder Plant.
The Old Hickory powder plant at
Nashville. Tenn., which the du Pont
Engineering Company reported to the
War Department cost $65,000,000, ac
tually cost $87,000,000, Mr. Evans said.
"The plant site comprised 5,553 acres,
and real estate men said the govern
ment paid an excessive price," Evans
testified. "About 2,200 buildings were
erected as quarters for employes, and
only 5 or 6 per cent of these had been
occupied at Christmas. 1918."
Evans said he got his figures on the
cost of the .war plant from the ac
countant of the ordnance department.
"On a two-acre salvage lot at Old
Hickory there was first-class material
of every kind, which was sold at low
figures," Evans said. "Copper wire and
valves, never taken from <he boxes in
which they were received, were sold
as so much junk."
Evans said he never saw more than
50 per cent of the employes at Old
Hickory at work, and that the labor
supply was overstocked. He ascribed
the general loafing and wastefulness
as due to the cost-plus system by
which the plant was built."
The witness gave the committee a
report showing 244 carloads of "good
material sold" as salvage, and de
clared two-thirds of it was bought
by a Junk dealer at Nashville, whose
brother was in charge of the salvage
lot.

Regarding the labor situation at
Old Hickory. Evans said:
"There was such a demand for
?killed labor that common labor was
employed and paid skilled labor
wages. Nobody said anything by
way of protest. Nearly every employe
felt that as the government was pay
ing the bill there was no use to hurry
or worry."
Thousands of hams and thousands
of pounds of other foodstuffs in the
commissary department were sold at
half the Nashville retail price to em
ployes, with no limit to the amount of
purchases.

said he sent full reports to the chief

ington. and copies went to the federal
district attorney at Nashville.
