Ever since the inception of the

dramatics at Princeton have been

"The Isle of Sunrise," an oriental

ment. which comprises this season's
offering, is announced &a of a na
ture to make a national appeal. The
plot centers around ihe warm, dreamy
atmosphere of a sunny isle in the

humor and enthusiasm of youth, the
production is claimed to exceed all
its predecessors.
'The Isle of Surprise" la said to es
tablish a precedent, in that It is the
sole product of undergraduate effort.
This plan has even been carried to
the stage settings, which are designed
by a student who formerly lived In
India, the scene of the action.
One of the features of the play is
the musical score, writ'.en by Erd
man Harris, the president of the club.
He has taken Gilbert and Sullivan as
his ideal. The music contains three
melodic elements, the native Indo
Mohammedan. the British military,
and the American, which are dove
tailed into the scenes. Although the
tunes are primarily semi-classical,
the composer has retained an under
tone of syncopation and melody which
presage an enthusiastic reception.
