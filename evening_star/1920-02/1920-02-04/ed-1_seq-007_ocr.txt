To the Editor of The Slat*:
The commission has before it a copy
of The Star of recent issue which con

<alobe, in wl^ich comment is made con
cerning the vacancy in the office of
postmaster at Boston. The article
states that Acting Postmaster Hur

then goes on to say:
"The 1 rouble is that the Civil service
makes no adequate allowance of ex
ecutive experience in the postal serv
ice and that its percentages are not
sufficiently based on the idea of pro
motion.
"This is a serious mistake. It is the
kind of error which prompts an em
ployer to go out and. comb the bywa>s
and hedges for a man to fill a vacancy
in a responsible position, and pass
over the likeliest candidate?the man
next in line in his own shop."
Sot Vnder Civil Service Hole.
It seems pertinent to point out first
of all that presidential postmaster
ships are not filled in accordance with
the civil service law and rules. They
are not in the competitive classified
service. Were they subject to th*> civil
service rules, employes within the
post office service might be promoted
upon demonstrating their fitness be
fore the commission without, compet
ing with persons outside the service
just as is done now, in filling many su
pervisory positions in the classified
service. To bring these postmaster
ships within the classified service ac
tion by the Congress is neeessp.ry.
The commission believes The St.n will
agree that there should be no iron
bound rule that all positions of post
mast, r should be filled through the pro
motion of subordinates. In the opinion
of the commission such a policy would
not be in the best, interests of the serv
ice and would no! be sustained by pub
lic sentiment.
Pre?ident Direct* KsamlnatlPM*
Examinations for presidential post
masters are held by the commission on
direction of the President to assist him
in making his selection for nomination
to the Senate for its confirmation. They
are competitive, all applicants being
measured by the same standard. Per
sons in the post office service are con
sidered on the same basis as persons
outside the service. The question of
what should be required for eligibility
for postmaster at any office is one of
judgment, on which there may be honest
differences of opinion. The require
ment fixed for offices the sizt* of Boston
is seven years of experience in a re
sponsible executive capacity and the
sole reason Mr. Hurley, with his ad
mirable record as assistant postmaster
at Boston, was not given an eligible rat
ing was that he had not occupied this
responsible, executive position for the
required seven years.
The commission requests that The
