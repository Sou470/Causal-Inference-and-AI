cratic quarters. As for instance: "We
must do the best we can at San Fran
cisco, and, later, in the campaign for

Federal Railroad Control.
Director General Hines' statement
to the newspapers of the United States,
a summary of which is printed else
where in The Star today, aims to cor
rect impressions that have prevailed
respecting the costliness of the fed
eral transportation control, under
taken as a war measure and soon to
be relinquished under X're3'dential
proclamation, and,^ft is expected, leg
islative provision. He meets with fig-
ures certain conceptions in the pub
lic mind inimical to the Kailroad Ad
ministration. For instance, that while
increased operating costs in private j
enterprise were due to war conditions i
a corresponding increase in railroad
costs were due to mistakes of the ad- I
I
ministration. He also combats the no-
tion that extravagant increases iu
?wages were paid for railroad labor,
and another that there was excessive
increase in the number of railroad J
employes under federal control. All
of these, he says, are untrue.
In regard to the deficit from rail
road operations, Mr. Hines jioints to
two factors?first, increased costs due
to conditions to which all industries
were subject, and inadequate revenues
attributable to failure to advance
transportation rates to the point of
actual costs. Had the deficit been
avoided by higher rates the burden
which now rests on the public iu the
form of taxes to meet it would have
been carried by railroad users in the
form of higher prices for service. As
adequate service was rendered under
federal administration during war con
ditions and immediate post-war con
ditions as could have been rendered
by private administration. On this
puiat Mr. Hines says that "the Kail
road Administration, with an inade
quate plant, has actually performed an
exceptional volume of service, and it
has done this with less congestion than
was ever known in previous periods
of heavy business in recent years."
In respect, to the condition of the
property, an impression prevails that
the railroads are dilapidated and "will
be turned back to private owners in
a condition hardly susceptible of op
eration.'' This, says Mr. Hincs, is ut
terly untrue. Ijoeomotives and ears
are in good and safe condition, al
though for reasons that are perfectly
plain the equipment, now on the aver
age two years older than at the be
ginning of federal control, has not
been replaced at the rate of normal
railroad administration. Shop strikes!
and coal strikes have militated
against upkeep and have laid heavy
burdens upon the lines. Material and
labor shortages incident to the war
have checked betterments, and in
some respects hampered maintenance,
but in no greater degree because of
federal control than would have been
the case under private management.
Altogether the director general makes
out a strong case for federal admin
istration, not for the purpose, it is
evident, of pointing to continued and
permanent government operation, but
to meet criticism that has become prev
alent.
Senator Glass.
Mr. Gla?s' appearance in the Senate
is opportune for himself, for his party
and for the country. His year in the
Treasury Department must have
yielded a great deal of information
about the finances of other countries
as well as of our own.
Everybody knows in a general way
that the world's finances are in a seri
ously tangled state. The strongest
countries across the Atlantic arc puz
zling over money problems. All are
bonded heavily. All are taxed op
pressively. All are struggling with
widespread business dislocations. And
America is in similar toils. Our bond
ed indebtedness mounts high. Our
taxes bear heavily. Our business is
greatly out of plumb.
But details are not a matter of com
mon knowledge. They are not acces
sible to the everyday reader, while
the majority of legislators are too
much occupied with other subjects to
give them full attention.
Here is where Mr. Glass' advantage
comes in. While Secretary of the
Treasury he had these details before
him, and, of course, mastered them.
As senator, therefore, he will be
primed with material of value when
financial questions (a re taken up for
action by Congress.
The return of Mr. Glass to the legis
lative arena is probably very much to
his liking. He served a long time in
the House, and participated in much
important bnsiness in that body. He
knows the legislative ropes thoroughly.
And there is no reason why he
should not serve as long a time in the
Senate. Virginia seems to be unshak
ably democratic; and the democrats of
the state show appreciation of capable
men in commission. They kept the
late Mr. Martin in office a quarter of
a century; and he died in harness. Mr.
Glass resembles him, in that he ad
dresses himself in a businesslike way
to business subjects, and leaves what
may be called the parade of politics
to others. While in the House he was
much better known as a committee
man than as a debater in the open
chamber, and became an authority on
subjects that engaged his interest.
At sixty-two Mr. Glass is probably
in his prime. He has had to do for
twenty years with large affairs, and
his contributions to important legis
lation have been considerable. With
the whole world in disorder, this coun
try need9 men of experience in office
to help shape its coarse; and Mr. Glass
is of the kind to give a good account
of himself.

The Service Pay Bill.
Yesterday the Senate passed the bill,
pending for some time, providing for
increases iu the basic pay of commis
sioned and enlisted personnel of the
Army, Navy, Marine Corps, coast
guards and public health service. It
now goes to' the House for considera
tion. Under its terms the base pay of
officers of these services is increased
10 per cent and of enlisted men and
members of the female nurse corps 20
per cent. In terms of ration and com
mutation allowances the actual increase
in the pay of officers is computed at
about 51 per cent.
This bill is one of the most urgent
pieces of legislation now before Con
gress. Its passage is required to pre
vent a continued and increasing de
moralization in the ranks of the fed
eral military services. Officers are un
able to maintain themselves at the
rates of pay now prevailing. Their
expenses have increased in common
with those of all other people. Vet
they are getting no more than before
the rise in prices. They have gone
into debt to meet their absolute ne
cessities, trusting to the government
to come to their rescne in terms of bet
ter pay. Their uniforms and equip
ment cost them heavily, in the cases of
junior officers far beyond their means.
This increase proposed in the Senate
bill will barely permit them to break
even. If it is not granted they must
resign from the Army and Navy and
the other branches of the service.
The enlisted men are not attracted
to the federal services by the rates of
pay now prevaMing. Once in, of course,
they must stay, but they do not re
enlist when their terms expire and re
cruiting has become very difficult. A
20 per cent increase in the base pay
of the enlisted personnel will enable
the government to attract into the
ranks men who are now inclined to go
into other employments with wider op
portunities. It is not desired that the
?United States should be in the posi
tion of competing with the industries
for the services of men, but it should
surely so finance its essential organi
zations as to recruit them adequately,
and this measure, which the House is
about to consider, is a means to that
end.
The Low Exchange Kates.
If foreign exchange continues to fall
to lower rates American trade must
inevitably be seriously affected. Al
ready, indeed, it is impaired by the
unwillingness of shippers to take
chances on losses due to money rate
shrinkage while goods are in transit.
The present low levels are beyond
precedent. With the British pound
touching $3.33 in New York obviously
the exchange market is in grave con- i
dition. This is due to the fact that
there is no British trade credit in this
country. Imports from England must
be promoted as a means of restoring
the price of the pound here. Loans by
the government will not, in any di
rect influence they exert, effect a cure
of the conditions that cause the for
eign exchange to fall. They are but
temporary stimulants which do not
reach the root of the trouble. What
England, France and Italy most ur
gently need today is an industrial re
establishment. To flie end of facili
tating that process loans may be val
uable, but they are not to be regard
ed, in whatever form, as corrections of
the exchange dislocation save through
their effects in quickening production
on the other side of the Atlantic.
