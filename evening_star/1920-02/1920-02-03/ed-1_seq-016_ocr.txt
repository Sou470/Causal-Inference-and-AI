T Dead Days
I sing the olden golden days,
for which I've often grieved;
the common tip was then a dime,
and thankfully received. With
princely hands I gave 10 cents,
and filled with wholesome glee
the souls of all the helpful gents
who did small things for me. I
acted thus for many years, in
many a shire and clime, and men
were often moved to tears, when
they received the dime. But
now the porter with his brush,
the waiter with his plate, jeer at
such specimens of cush and
breathe a hymn of hate. A quar
ter, even, is too small, too
trifling and too punk, to soothe
the current of their gall?they
ought to have a plunk. Some
day the people will arise and
raise a howdydo, and swat, as
they wquld swat the flies, the
whole blamed grafting crew.
Some day a statesman will ap
pear, a germproof law he'll
frame, to make the tip of yes
teryear an outlawed, penal
game. The tipping of the olden
time was free from sin and
guile, for then a man could give
a dime and get a winning smile;
but now he gives a half a bone,
which he can hardly spare; the
porter takes it with a groan,
and goes outdoors to swear.

Directors Favor Continuance
at 9th Street?Planning
Shadbake.
Continuation of the branch post
office at 9th street and Pennsylvania
avenue until the postmaster of Wash
ington is able to locate another site
for the branch, was urged yesterday
afternoon at a meeting of the board of
directors of the Washington Board of
Trade. This office, which is said to be
rented from the Y. M. C. A., has proved
of great convenience to the many
merchants in that section of the city,
and the Commissioners of the Dis
trict are requested to allow more time
before its removal.
'There are in the neighborhood of
one thousand merchants using this
branch station from time to time,"
said Capt. James F. Oyster, president
of the board, "and although we have
succeeded in getting the removal date
advanced from February 1 to Febru
ary 29 more time is needed to secure
another station in the immediate
neighborhood. We hope that the Com
missioners will extend the time for
the vacating of the building."
The matter was referred to the In
dustrial interests committee of the
board, of which Isaac Gans is chair
man.
Will riaa Shadbake.
President Oyster was authorised to
appoint a chairman and the necessary
committee to make arrangements for
the annual shadbake of the organiza
tion. It is understood that the shad
bake will be held some time In the
latter part of May, but on account of
the many arrangements to be made
the committee will be given ample
time to work out details.
That an acute shortage of empty
freight cars is imminent unless steps
are taken to unload the cars now on
the siding is the assertion of Fielding
L. Marshall, commercial freight agent
of the Baltimore and Ohio railroad,
in a letter to the board. Mr. Fielding
urges that the merchants of the city
take Bteps immediately relative to the
unloading of cars. A pamphlet also
has been sent out by Walker D. Hines,
director general of railroads, advis
ing agents of the heavy traffic during
the next month, and urging that steps
be taken to meet the emergency. This
question also was referred to the in
dustrial interests committee.

The report of the executive commit
tee was read relating to matters
taken up by this committee since the
last meeting of the board of directors.
The report dealt with the election of
fifty-nine new members, $100 contrib
uted to the charities indorsement com
mittee. $1,000 to the building fund of
the United States Chamber of Com
jnerce, and $100 for membership in the
National Rivers and Harbora Con
gress.
The report was approved by the
board of directors.

board by the French mission, in com

for the midwinter dinner of the mem
bership committee, to be held at
