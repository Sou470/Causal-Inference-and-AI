A bitter arraignment of the French
judiciary system, with Its brutal
"third degree" and its insatiable thirst
for "convictions, life sentences, capital
punishment." is the underlying motif
of "The better of the Law," an Eng
lish version of the work of Eugene
Rrieux. which captivated a capacity
audience last night at the Shubert
Garrick.
Lionel Barrymore. in the role of
Mouzon. examining magistrate, vividly
depicts the depths to which unscrupu
lous members of the legal profession
may descend in order to obtain a
"confession" of guilt. While Mr.
Barrymore, with all his accustomed
verve and personality, easily holds
first place in the affection of his au
dience. Miss Doris Rankin, as Yanetta,
wife of an alleged murderer, runs a
close second. As a matter of fact,
there is not a member of the cast
who does not more than ably fill
the part assigned to him or her. But
Miss Rankin displays rare ability,
and after the second act. during which
she dominated a scene of intense emo
tion, she was compelled to "take" a
number of curtain calls.
Included in the cast are Zeffie Til
bury. Leona Hogarth, Russ Whytal,
Josephine Wehn, Allan Xelly. Maud
Hosford. Frank Kingdon, Clarence
Derwetit, Charles Ooghlan, James P.
Hagen, Wallace Jackson, Charles N.
Greene, Jacob Kingsberry, Goldwin
Patton. Charles White, Ada Bosliell
and Lionel Hogarth.
The play does not progress into
brisk action until the second act,
when, in the office of Mouzon, the ex
amining magistrate, there is enacted
a double "third degree scene which
would put to shame the public's con
ception of the methods used by Amer
ican detectives to force a confession
from a suspect.
Barrymore Is at his best in this act.
As a swashbuckling, bullying, unctu
ous and sympathetic (in turns) exam
iner, he endeavors to break down the
sullen denials of Rtchepare (Goldwin
Patton) that he committed a particu
larly brutal murder. Miss Rankin, as
Etchepare's wife, is brought into the

the young woman's life in order to
inveigle her into declaring her hus
band committed the crime.
In the third act, pangs of remorse
have eaten into the heart of Vagret,
attorney for the state, who conducts
the prosecution before the jury. He
realizes that in order to obtain the
conviction of Etchepare he has de
liberately discounted several bits of
evidence which would tend to prove
the man's innocence. Deliberately
sacrificing his opportunity for promo
tion to the judgeship, he recalls, the
jury, advances the evidence and wins
the acquittal of Etchepare.
"The Letter of the Law" will go
down in the annals of drama as one
of the most forceful plays produced

the dealer in coffins and caskets as there is between the trained and experi

distinguish between professionalism and commercialism.
