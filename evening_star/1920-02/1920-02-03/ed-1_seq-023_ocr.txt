BUSY MONTH IS AHEAD
FOR GOVERNING BODES
OF THREE BIG SPORTS
Lawmakers of Gridiron, Tennis and Base
Ball Meet, With Radical Revisions
Assured in Code of Last
BY DEN MAN THOMPSON.
This is to be a busy month for the guiding spirits in the world of
sport. The powers that be in at least three of the national pastimes?
t'oor ball, tennis and base ball?are to foregather for the purpose of
giving rules and regulations a careful inspection, and. contrary to cus
tom, some changes ot a radical nature are expected to result.
Midwinter always has been the season in which the reformers
reach the maximum of their activities. Then it is that the wails of
protest against "deplorable conditions." "evil practices" and "objec
tionable ieatures" in the world of athletic endeavor are longest and
loudest. Then it is that those charged .with the supervision of com
petithe games are deluged with bright ideas and suggestions for their
betterment.
Hearings annually arc accorded to the would-be helpfuls who are
actuated by the desire to uplift, and. as a rule, their proposals are re
ceived. acknowledged with thanks, and. after due deliberation, con
signed to the well known pigeonhole.
But. it's to be different this year, as regards base ball, at least, if
not the gridiron and court games. The attention of the foot ball rules
committee at its forthcoming meeting is to be concentrated on the goal
irom touchdown, a traditional feature of the sport, with no predictions
as to whether it will be done away with, altered in form of accom
plishment or tinkered with as to scoring value.
The tennis world has been seethrr.g over insistent demands for
changes in the scoring rules, handicapping and foot-fault regulation,
with revisions of the last almost assured, and improvement in the
method oi handicapping very probable, although it is extremely doubt
ful that the colorful language and methods of scoring, as ancient as
the game itself, will be sacrificed.
EXTENSIVE REVISIONS IN PROSPECT.
In base ball revisions of the code promise to be many and far
reaching. When the magnates assemble in Chicago next week they
will be confronted with several tasks of grave import, including the
selection of a new chairman for the national commission and a pos
sible revamping of the tribunal. Also there will be the question of
settling the strife that grips the American League, adoption of some
policy for dealing with the large army of holdout players and adjust
ment of the lawsuit still being contested with the Baltimore Heds. All
this in addition to the enactment of legislation to bar trick pitching,
elimination of gift steals in base running and to accord full credit to
batsmen for long hits registered late in games where the maximum
distance obtainable from their blows is not necessary to decide the
issue.
A comprehensive effort will be made to clarify the rules and elimi
nate those now regarded as obsolete. The committee intrusted with
the task is to be a representative one. with the umpires and members
of the Base Ball Writers' Association conferring with Magnates Grif
fith, Mack. Veeck and Baker, who comprise the joint rules com
mittee.
The rules conferees are scheduled to meet next Monday, but so
great is the importance attached to their deliberations they may be
asked to start them Sunday so as to have all their work disposed of
by Tuesday, when the individual meetings of the two leagues are
listed to be held.
