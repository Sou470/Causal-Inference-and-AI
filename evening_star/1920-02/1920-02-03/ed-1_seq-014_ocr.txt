Dr. Buhrer Declares Demand
for Moral Pictures Must
Be Created.
"A demand for moral motion pic
tures must be created in this city
as well as a supply," stated Rev.
Dr. J. D. Buhrer, in summing up the
results of his investigation for
"cleaner pictures" in his speech last
night at the meeting of the Sixteenth
Street Highlands Citizens' Associa
tion. in the Sixth Presbyterian Church.
Kennc'Jy street near 16th.
Dr. Buhrer at a recent meeting was
appointed chairman of the committee
to investigate the subject of a cen
sorship in the city. He stated that
some of the owners of the large pic
ture houses here had been inter
viewed. Dr. Buhrer said that he
knew it waa a difficult task to get.
these large owners to consent to a
strict rensorship, for it was a mat
ter of business with them, and it
was only too clear that the public
did not demand censored pictures.
Onuored Picture* Not Popular.
This was definitely evidenced, he
stated, by a theater here which tried
this system of running "unquestion
able" pictures once a week, and ad
vertised the fact to the various homes
in the neighborhood. At first the
children were sent on that night, he
said, but soon the children came dur
ing the other nights as well, and the
attendance dropped off on the nights
of the censored pictures.
Dr. Buhrer then offered a solution,
saving that lately he had met some
of the board of Pennsylvania censors,
who told him that the only way to
procure the right kind of pictures
would be to procure a board of cen
sors for this city, compelling the the
ater owners to show only what pic
tures the board would pass upon.
A motion was made by Z. L. Darby
asking Dr. Buhrer to present some
definite request, which, upon the ac
tion of the association, would be pre
sented to the Federation of Citizens'
Associations. He claimed that a united
action on the question would greatly
enhance the probability of obtaining
a censorship for the District of Co
lumbia. This motion was unanimous
ly adopted.
Better Ask Colleetlom Urged.
A resolution was adopted by the
association, protesting against the
non-appearance of the ash collector,
and urging better service. The secre
tary, Capt. C. E. Johnston was in
structed to send copies of this request
to the Commissioners.
Capt. Johnston stated that at a re
cent meeting of >the executive com
mittee. it was suggested that a public
meeting be held later In the year, to
which the wives and friends of the
members of the association would be
invited. This meeting, he said, would
have some interesting addresses and
a musical entertainment. This idea,
lie stated, was suggested that it would
tend to increase closer community
feeling. Because of the absence of so
many members, the motion was tabled
until the next meeting.
Clyde B. Asher, in the absence of
the president and two vice presidents,
was chosen to preside at the meeting.
The absence of the officials and many
members was necessitated by the at
tendance at a meeting of the Chamber
of Commerce. James T. Lloyd, former
representative from Missouri, was
scheduled to speak, but was absent
for the same reason. It was stated,
however, that he would speak at a
meeting in the near future.
One new resident was elected to
membership in the associa tion.
POWHATAN PUMPED OUT.
HALIFAX, N. S.. February 2 (by the
Associated Press).?The work of pump
ing out the American Army transport
Powhatan, which was recently towed in
to port in a disabled condition, has been
completed and the majority of the crew
who have been living ashore today re
turned to their quarters on board the
steamer. Fires have been lighted under
two boilers and sufficient steam raised to
heat the ship.
A United States Army board is mak
ing a survey of the vessel preparatory
to putting her in shape to again go to
sea.
VOCATIONAL WAGE
Students Claim That $80
Monthly Is Not Enough
to Maintain Them.
Representatives of 1,500 vocational
training- students of New York city
canvassed Congress today with the
request for a bill providing for "suf
ficient income to continue the course."
Headed by President A. B. Conlin,
a committee of ten former service
men, now taking the vocational train
ing course, submitted to members of
Congress the fact that <80 per month
was not sufficient to provide for ex
penses and maintenance of students
taking the courses of training under
the auspices of the Federal Board of
Vocational Education, even with the
additional sums obtained by several
of the disabled men working after
school hours.
$100 May Be Sought,
It is expected that a bill will be
introduced in the House within the
next few days raising the allotment
to at least $100 per month, which
was declared the lowest possible sum
that the service men could receive
and continue their courses by the
chairman of the committee.
"The men are doing everything pos
sible to keep the advantage granted
through the Federal Board of Voca
tional Education," declared R. I.
Deason, who is here with the com
mittee today.
"But unless more than an $80 al
lotment is .granted the men will find
it virtually Impossible to continue the
courses in training and the purpose
of the board will be nullified."
The committee of ten started the
canvass of the House of Representa
tives this morning, a set quota of rep
resentatives being visited by each
committeeman.
Mr. Deason declared that the stu
dents had the backing1 of the Ameri
can Legion, following a conference
with H. H. Reage, chairman of the
legislative committee of the legion,
yesterday afternoon. The American
Legion, it is declared, is backing- the
men in their plea for an increase im
mediately to $100 per month.

CENSUS COUNT FOR D.C.
READY IN TWO WEEKS
All of the Portfolios From 367 Dis
tricts Now in Hands of
Supervisors.
The official population count for the
District of Columbia is expected to be
made public in about two weeks.
With all the portfolios from the 367
census districts in the District of Co
lumbia in the hands of the supervisor
for the census here, those that have
not already been turned in to the cen
sus bureau are being checked up and
corrected. Official announcement of
the end of the census in the District is
expected to be announced by the cen
tral census bureau within the next
two days. t
Officials estimate that compilation of
the returns from the District of Co
lumbia should not take more than two
weeks, and that the official count of
the population of the District should
be given out within that time.
Brooklyn is said to be finished with
its census taking, but the census bu
reau has not been officially advised of
the end of the count, and has not re
ceived all the portfolios from Brook
lyn. Several other cities, particularly
those in the middle west, are nearing
the end of the census work, but on
account of the time it takes to get
mail from the middle west and far
west the full number of portfolios
have not been received.
DEATHS CAUSED BY ICE FLOE.
NEW YORK, February 3.?A great
ice floe crushed a small duck boat in
Jamaica bay at midnight last night,
causing two fishermen to drown,
while the other occupants saved their
lives by clambering onto the floating
ice and finally swimming to shore.
The fishermen were on their way to
oyBter beds on Smelk Island, when the
mass of Ice loomed before them in the
fog. Before they could change their
course the frail craft was ground to
pieces and all four thrown into the
water.
Stenley and Clayton Lundy, brothers,
disappeared almost immediately. An
other brother, Allen Lundy, and Ed
ward Shecyleton managed *o climb
upon the floe after a desperate strug
gle. Their cries for help being un
heeded they were forced to swim.

WORD AWAITED OF ARMY
FLYERS TAKEN IN MEXICO
No Trouble Anticipated in Obtain
ing Release of Aviators Who
Made Forced Landing.
EL PASO, Tex., February 3.?Word
was awaited today concerning Lieuts
G. Ik Usher and L. M. Wolf, American
Army aviators stationed at Fort Bliss,
who were captured yesterday by Mexi
can soldiers near Nacozari. Sonora,
Mexico, and taken to Nacozari, tech
nically under arrest.
The Americans on a flight from Fort
Bliss to Nogales, Ariz., became lost yes
terday and were sixteen kilometers
south of Nacozari when engine trouble
necessitated a forced landing.
Maj. Gen. Robert I. Howze, comman
der of the El Paso district, said he
anticipated no difficulty in obtaining the
release- of the aviators. Although it is
believed their detention was merely to
get a formal statement from the Ameri
cans as to their excuse for venturing
south of the border, American Consul
Francis J. Dyer at Nogales has taken
the matter up with Gov. Adolfo de la
Huerta of Sonora. Reports last night
said the Americans were being treated
with consideration. It was said their
machino was damaged in making the
landing. Whether it could be repaired
to make the return trip under its own
power was not known.
