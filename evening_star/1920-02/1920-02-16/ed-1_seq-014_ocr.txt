Will Give Figures.
Complete figures, showing the work
of the sixteen visiting nurses em
ployed by the I. V. N. a. In guarding

This Is the regular annual meeting

generally, and all friends and beno

Partial figures now available show
the extent of the aid rendered by
these nurses In the epidemic just over.
The usual average of new cases for
the 1. V. N. S. during the past year
was 231 a month. During January.
1920, 684 new cases were reported to
the society and received the care of
the nurses, the great majority being
influenza and pneumonia cases. The
nurses worked without regard to
hours or fatigue as long as their help

Aniwfr Many Appeals.
"Many desperate appeals for assist
ance came to us," reports Miss Alice
Bagley, superintendent of the I. V. N.
S.. "and they camc from people of all
types and classes, of all nationalities,
in all parts of the city.
"In many cases whole families were
ill and help could bo secured neither
for household work or for nursing.
In one family the mother, father and
three children had influenza. Not
only were they without help, but
their coal supply had given out. An
I. V. N. S. nurse went to the house,
gave tho family the necessary nurs
ing care and later sent a practical
attendant. Coal was also secured
and sent them through the help of a
friend of the society who had offered
his services in such an emergency.
In another case a woman, who kept
a rooming house in which all the
lodgers were men, was sick and with
out any care at all. One mother in
an apartment in the northeastern
section of the city w*s struggling
with the care of two sick children
when her third, a baby, succumbed.
She called a visiting nurse, who came
and was taking tho baby's tempera
ture when it went into convulsions.
The nurse stayed with the child until
the danger of further complications
Was past, helped with the car<< of the
other two children and gave the
mother the necessary directions about

"Instances such as these could be
multiplied. They tell the story of
