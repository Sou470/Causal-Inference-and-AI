Time I.oann at 10 Per Cent.
Some time loans of the shorter
dates, namely, thirty and sixty days,
are being made at 10 per cent. The
amounts, however, are small, says the
Wall Street J.ournal.
Ostensibly these loans are demand
loans, but the understanding between
the borrower and lender is that they
will be neither paid off nor called,
within the period stipulated. Either
party to the contract would lose
standing If that were done.
Some of the demand loan contracts
made under these conditions contain
a clause to the effect that the rate is
10 per cent, "unless this loan is sooner
paid than (say) March 5," or, "It Is
agreed that the rate of interest on
this loan will not be changed in thir
ty days."
The transaction is drawn up in this
way ?o as to comply with the statu
tory requirements. The usury law
does not apply to demand loans.
If the loan remains unpaid longer
than the period stipulated the legal
rate becomes 6 per cent and the bank
has no right to take a higher rate of
interest from the borrower. Should
the borrower, however, refuse to re
pay the loan on the date specified, in
order to take advantage of the statu
tory rate, the Ijank has the recourse
of selling the collateral behind the
loan.
To all intents and purposes, there
fore, a demand loan drawn up in this
way becomes virtually a fixed time
accommodation.
