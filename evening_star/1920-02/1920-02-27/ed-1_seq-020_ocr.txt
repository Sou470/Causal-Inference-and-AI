How the President
Has "Come Back"
President Wilson "knows that he is now quite up to his fighting- weight. As to his meintal
vigor, it is simply prodigious. He is giving splendid attention to the affairs of state, and we
have every assurance that he will become progressively more active in these matters with the
advent of spring and sunshine," according to one of the President's physicians. Disturbing,
however, are the words of Dr. Arthur Dean Bevan, former President of the American Medical
Association, who says that the disease of the arteries," from which the President is suffering,
"is permanent and not a temporary condition." Whatever the President's mental and physical
condition, in the opinion of the medical men, he has emphasized his retttrn to public life by
receiving a delegation of railroad labor leaders, by sending to the Allied Supreme Council a
virtual ultimatum on the Fiume controversy, and by dropping his Secretary of State. The latter
action startled the public and aroused a storm of criticism, much of it unfavorable to the Chief
Executive.
