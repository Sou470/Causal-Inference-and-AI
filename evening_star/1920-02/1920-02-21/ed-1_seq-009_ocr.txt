BY DENMAN THOMPSON.
Base ball in Boston this year is due to receive attention from
both the American and National leagues in reverse ratio to the inter
j- e> t of the fans, judging from the present outlook. The Huh, acknowl
edged to b? one of the best ball towns in the country, is in a sad plight
so far as its representatives in the major circuits are concerned, and
there is little question but that the powers that be ill the sport will
take cognizance of the situation and attempt to improve conditions.
For the past five years, or ever since the Braves made their comet
like rush from last place on July 1 to a pennant and subsequent
triumph orer Philadelphia in four straight games for the world cham
pionship the National Leaguers have amounted to little more than a
chopping block for the other entries in Heydler's circuit. After their
sensational rise to fame they were expected to ride at the crest for a
year or two at least, but they fell victims to overconfidence, and
George Stallings, the widely heralded "miracle man" of 1914, has been
able to accomplish little with them since.
As a result Braves' Field, the largest stadium in professional base
ball, erected to take care of the enormous crowds which never have
materialized, has proved a regular white elephant on the hands of the
owners, the overhead charges eating up more than is taken in the box
office year after year. The Braves appear destined to wind up near the
bottom of the ladder again this year, and patrons of the game there
promise to continue giving them the absent treatment.

i The slump into disfavor of the Red Sox is a direct result of the
business policy pursued by President Frazee since he relieved Joe

piloting a sixth-place club, because it boasted in Babe Ruth one of

come known in an emphatic manner, however, and now that Frazee's
promises to use the money he received for Ruth in buying talent cal
culated to make the Sox a contender have failed to materialize, the
prospects for a prosperous season at Fenway Park are dim indeed.
If the club should land no higher than sixth with Ruth in the line-up,
what will it amount to without him? That's the question the fans
are asking, and empty seats in the Back Bay inclosure probably will
be the answer.
For more than a week now rumors have been current that Ban
Johnson has been busying himself in an effort to find a purchaser for

the American League executive and Frazee. who obtained control of
the Red Sox without Ban's sanction. While the league war was on
and Frazee had the support of the New York and Chicago clubs, it
J was a difficult matter to dislodge him, but now that the scrap has been
