One Explanation Relates to Effort to
Undo Evils of Inflation; Another
to Foreign Trade.

Special Correspondent of Tfcf Star.
NEW YORK, February 7.?This
week's collapse in Wall street de
mands a different interpretation en
tirely from that placed upon the
break in November. Then it was a
clear case of the banks, under the
urging of the federal reserve board,
violently shutting- down upon specula
tion and forcing- liquidation of loans
on stocks in order to ease the general
overstrain on credit. This week's de
cline. on the contrary, came in a
market in which the speculative ac
count was relatively small, that is,
small by comparison with what it was
three months ago.
It could not be looked upon as a
process of correcting overspecula
tion. for. at the level of prices current
at the start of the -week, there had
been ample deflation and certainly no
exaggeration gauged by the standard
of intrinsic values.
Two other versions are to be con
sidered in relation to this latest
break. According to one version, the
stock market has merely been drawn
along in a general liquidtion which
reached into the commodity markets
and into industrial transactions as
well as a liquidation 'rfcause it was
the only way to undo the evils of
past inflation brought about, by l.ar
financing and help solve the problem
of the high cost of living. According
to the other version, the stock mar
ket has been discounting a very
serious slump in the foreign trade
and its sequel, a decline in prices
and a let-up in general business at
home.
Sptralatim Outside Market.
As it looks -now. there is consider
able truth in both of these explana
tions. The Wall street market had
seemed to have done its share toward
righting the credit situation in the
severe decline last autumn. Yet,
week after week, the federal reserve
statements failed to show improve
ment. The official discount rates
were raised very sharply in what ap
peared to be an effort to force
liquidation in speculation channels
outside of the stock market.
For a while last month W"\1I street
was saying that it was not the stock
market this time that th-! govern
ment was aiming at, but tat'ner spec
ulators in cotton, in fooJ products
and in many other industrial lilies.
At first, the drop in foreign ex
change. as it started from the 3.80
level for sterling, had no serious ef
fect, because it was supposed to be
due to the calling of loans, in which
bills of exchange formed the collat
eral. Even last week, in the face of
rising money rates, the market re
fused to be much concerned.
Two things happened to upset this
complacent attitude with the start of
the present week. First, of course,
there was the extraordinary collapse
in the exchange and second a con
traction of funds available for bor
rowing. owing to the heavy draft ot
the government upon its New York
deposit account.
Teat Will Be Offered.
The government funds withdrawn
this week are not. of course, going
to be locked up. They will be paid
out again in a short time and will
j find their way back to the financial
I centers. It may be that in this way
j money rates will work somewhat
lower. If this happens we shall have
j a test of just what part the credit
(pinch has played in the recent dis
orders and how far. on the other
I hand, they are ascribable to the tre
mendous fall in the exchanges and
I the unfavorable possibilities attach
in>g thereto.
The opinion is expressed in well in
j formed banking circles that the ex
trerae low points for sterling and
I continental rales were reac hed this
week, and that, while as yet there is
not apt to be much of a recovery, these
low levels will not be exceeded.
In the meantime the damage has
been done. The hope that was felt up
i to a week ago that a. way might
have been found to correct the ex
change situation before it had seri
ously interfered with European pur
chases in the An"rican market must
now be abandoned.
We know already that Kurope has be
gun cutting down heavily its orders for
American cotton and other staples, that
cancellations have occurred and that in
some instances American goods shipped
to the other side have been sent back be
cause it has been more advantageous to
pay the extra cost of transportation and
insurance than to complete the purchase
of dollar commodities with depreciated
pounds and francs.
The January trade figures will rot
reveal this falling off to any great ex
tent, but it will begin to appear in the
February returns. The question then
becomes how serious the backing-up of'
the export surplus will be for the home
irarkets. As has been pointed out here i
before, the most encouraging "thing in
this connection is tnat in so many of
our staple lines demand is far behind
supply.
There can be no great industrial
depression without overproduction,
and it will take a lot of falling off in
the foreign trade to convert the pres
ent deficit in the home industrial
markets into an uncomfortable sur
plus.
No doubt prices will come down,
and this is not by any means unde
sirable.
Xo doubt, too. there will be more or
less hesitation, increasing caution
and consequent showdown in the gen
eral business volume.
Ltn Proipfroi* Condition.
As against all this prices on the
stock exchance have had an enor
mous fall from where they stood
three months ago. They have cer
tainly measured a less prosperous
condition in business affairs, and
always there remains the fact that,
no matter what the eafning power
may be. the accumulated profits of
past years have not been distributed
in the case of our standard indus
trials and of themselves are suf
ficient to warrant prices for their
stocks, which make the present level
seem conservative.
One of the most disturbing features
in the market of the week has been
the extreme weakness in the railway
shares, in which there had been no
speculation and against which the
charge of inflation could certainly not
lie. People have asked themselves
whether there was anything the
pending railway biH to cause this
fresh outburst of selling. The reply
has been that, while the compromise
measure to be reported from the con
ference committee is by no means
satisfactory according to old-line
standards, it does contain sufficient
safeguards to investors in railway
securities not to warrant these being
sacrificed at present levels.
The more reasonable explanation
for the liquidation in the rails is that
the selling has been due to two other
causes.
First, banking syndicates. Some of
them are tied up with a lot of stuff
which they cannot get rid of. and the
only way they can get into more
liquid shape is by throwing over, even
at a sacrifice, stocks like the rails,
which have a ready market.
The other is that the tremendous
premium on the American dollar In
Europe makes even current prices for
the remaining American securities
held abroad seem attractive. Conse
quently. Europe is selling, and this
selling bears with special fdrce upon
the market for railway stocks.
