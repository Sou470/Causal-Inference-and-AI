year
There are number of excellent reasons why Mack is beating the
barrier, so to speak, in convoying his athletes southward at such an
early date. The most important is that the lean leader has a con
suming ambition to haul his entry out of last place. For five years in
succession the end of the American League campaign has found the
Philadelphia club firmly mired in the rut. This constitutes a record,
one that would prove fatal to the
popularity of any manager except
Mack, and is particularly galling
to the tall tactician in view of his
previous accomplishments as a
pilot.
Prior to 1915, when the Quaker
chieftain dismantled the famous
machine so spectacularly humbled
by the Boston Braves in the world
series of the previous season,
Mack established the unparalleled
record of annexing six league pen
nants, a mark since tied by Bos
ton, which had accounted for three
up to that time. Furthermore, in
the fourteen seasons of competi
tion that preceded Mack's first in
duction into the cellar his teams
finished out of the first division
only twice, landing fifth in 1904 and
sixth in 1908. It takes a flock of
reversals to hurt the prestige of a
pilot with such a record, but Mack
has had them, and he now is
hankering to regain his old place
in the sun.
