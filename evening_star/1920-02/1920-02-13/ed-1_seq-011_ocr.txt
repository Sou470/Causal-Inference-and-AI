Dispels British View That
Failure to Ratify Means De
sertion of Europe.
BY FRANK H. S1*0*DS.
The immediate and most considera
ble service rendered by Viscount Grey.
in his letter to the London Times, lies
in its complete refutation of the view
held in Britain, and in Europe gen
erally to no small extent, that the
United State* haa. by its course
toward the treaty of Versailles, ac
tually repudiated an obligation. This
belief has been held in Britain and it
has been encouraged to no small de
gree by certain American champions
of the league of nations, who hav*
argued that to decline to ratify the
treaty as written would be to desert
Europe.
Americans have believed that Euro
peans generally recogniied the di
vision of authority in the matter of
treaty-making in our system of sov
ernment. and thai this criticism of
the Senate's course was actually the
fruit of desire rather than of convic
tion: that the desire to see the treaty
ratified as written led to criticism and
allegation not actually believed.
Exceptions Sotfd.
This has not been the fact, in the
larger number of instances. In all of
Europe the men who actually repre
I sen ted the nations at Paris were, in
addition, the responsible leaders of the
majorities in the several parliaments.
Lloyd George was able to speak for
the British government and his signa
ture carried the later approval of the
house of commons with it, because he
was leader of the majority in the
house.
This thing works both ?ways. More
than once George was compelled to
modify his positions, taken at Paris,
because they were unsatisfactory to
the majority in the house of com
mons. Thus the British parliament
expressed its will, in a certain meas
ure, during the actual making of the
treaty. A repudiation by parliament
afterward would thus be unthinkable
and in a degree a repudiation of an
obligation actually entered upon.
I 8. Situation Dlfrrnt.
Of course, our situation is quite dif
ferent, but the appreciation of our
situation was not generally under
stood in Europe. While the President
was in Paris he. and those with him,
spoke for the Upited States without
qualification. To Europe the Presi
dent' seemed the official spokesman
of the nation and there was not the
smallest suspicion that there could be
any subsequent questioning of his de
cisions in the conference.
Grey's frank letter puts this" phase
of the question correctly before the
British public and it is an odd but
unmistakable fact that it was neces
sary that some Englishman of his
eminence should do this, because no
American statement on the subject
was sufficient or even satisfactory to
the British public, which was the
more confused because Lloyd George
publicly and through his friendly
newspapers continued to assert that
the sole explanation was discoverable
in partisan political consideration.
Antkn Aspect
There is another aspect of the ques
tion which Grey could not explain
with equal frankness for obvious rea
sons. Underlying all else in the Amer
ican attitude, popular rather than offi
cial. has been the instinct against any
actual commitment of the United
States to European entanglements?an
instinct which has been disclosed in
British history for three centuries
From Cromwell to Lloyd George, Brit
ain has intervened in continental con
tests innumerable times, when thes?
contests threatened to affect British
interests adversely, under circum
stances at least recalling our inter
vention in the world war.
But invariably, after the immediate
danger has been disposed of. Great
Britain has retired from the European
field, resuming that "splendid isola
tion" from which she was called the
last time, vhen Germany set out upon
her pathway of world domination.
Even the actual participation in wars
which seemed to have a British inter
est haa been opposed by large num
bers of Englishmen.
British Policy at Paris.
At Paris there was unmistakably the
same general tendency in British pol
icy. What Britain was seeking to do
was to reduce her own obligations and
commitments on the continent, but this
time her course was to seek such a re
duction by insuring the association
with herself of the United States.
British statesmen believed that their
country and ours in close co-operation
could exercise an enormous restraining
influence upon Europe, and thus great
ly reduce the danger of new complica
tions, which would necessitate new
military operations on the continent.
The American instinct, however, was
plainly revealed in this country to be
in favor of American withdrawal, alike
from European and from British part
nerships. What the United States
seemed to want was to resume that
complete freedom from foreign obliga
tions. in the sense of treaty commit
ments. which existed before we en
tered the world war. And this, after
all, was only complete imitation of
British policy in the past.
BifrHi Only United Partnership
Europe is beginning to appreciate
that the best that can now be ex
pected of the United States Is a
thoroughly limited partnership, a
consent to Bit in the council of na
tions. but a consent conditioned upon
European consent to permit us to re
tain our entire freedom of action, to
remain unbound by any decision made
in the league of nations conference,
which is not subsequently ratified by
Congress.
Grey had recognized this, although
the limitations imposed upon him by
his position have necessarily re
stricted his public utterance. The
effect of his letter will be to demon
strate that Europe is now prepared
to welcome American participation
in the league of nations on American
terms. It has demonstrated already
that Europe is eager to have any
possible measure of American par
ticipation at any cost in the way of
concessions.
What Grey's letter does not rec
ognise is the fact that this partici
pation is likely to be rigidly limited
for an indefinite period of time. His
view is that once we enter the league
of nations we shall become. by
the process of association and de
velopment. full partners, that insen
sibly we shall drift into a resump
tion of the role played by us, through
President Wilson, at the Paris con
ference.
Hope Not Well Koaaded.
Undoubtedly Europe will expect
this, but American evidence certainly
does not sustain the foreign hope.
The notion that we shall join in ul
timatums delivered to Italy, Turkey and
Jugoslavia, share in making policies
affecting German reparations, deal
ing with Russian chaos, hardly finds
any warrant in the views and tem
per found in Washington today, or
reflected in public sentiment through
out the country.
On the contrary, it would seem that
the nation is passing through a pe
riod of pronounced reaction against
all American interference in Euro
pean affairs. So far Viscount Grey's
otherwise admirably clear and eccurate
pronouncement may prove misleading,
leading.
