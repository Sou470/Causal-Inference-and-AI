Associate Justice Stafford Honors
Memory of Lincoln; Gompers
Pleads for League.
Six hundred Elks of Washington
Ixxlge, No. 15, celebrated the thirty
eighth birthday anniversary of the
local organization with a banquet at
Wardnian Park Hotel last night.
Wendell Phillips Stafford, associate
justice of the District Supreme Court,
delivered what was characterized as
a "prose poem" on the life of Abra
ham Lincoln. On the platform with
him was Samuel Gompers, president
of the American Federation of I^abor.
Early this week, in a speech, the
former made antagonistic comments
on labor.
Mr. Gompers responded to the toast,
"The Brotherhood of Man," with a
plea for the league of nations, declar
ing that the league would make for
the brotherhood of nations, which
would be the foundational step for the
fraternity of humanity.
Senator A. O. Stanley responded to a
toast on "Elks and the World War"
by declaring that the order, "which
fanatics decry, had done more to
brighten a burdened world than any
body of men since the lowly Na*arene
was laid in the tomb. That spirit
which dominates Elks in times of
peace made them heroic in times of
war," he declared.
"The Eleven o'Clock Toast" was re
sponded to by Roger J. Whlteford,
after the hall had been darkened for
several minutes in a silent tribute to
the memory of deceased members.
Frank J. Hogan acted as toast
master. Raymond Hitchcock presented
several humorous stories and George
O'Connor and Matt Home sang.
The opening address was made by
Edward J. Murphy, chairman of the
banquet, and the festivities closed
with music by the Marine Band, after
a song by an octet of Elks.
