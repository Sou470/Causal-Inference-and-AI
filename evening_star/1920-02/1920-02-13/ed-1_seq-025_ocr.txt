Balance.
One of the subtlest points in the
art of house decoration and the ar
rangement of furniture, pictures and
smaller articles is the sense of bal
ance. Jia!? the time, when the effect
of a room is unrestful and uninvit
ing, the secret of the trouble is that
what is called the "composition" of
the furniture and the walls lacks that
nice balancing of weight and line
and color that makes perfect equili
brium and therefore perfect harmony.
Balance used to be carried to ex
tremes in the day when a vase at one
end of a mantel had to be offset by
another exactly like it at the other
end, when pictures went in pairs and
A BALANCE OF LINE AND COLOR.
a chair at one side of the fireplace
had to have one to match it at the
other side. Now, except in very for
mal rooms, broken lines on wall and
shelf and table are preferred, provid
ed they follow the general law ot'
proportion, which says that one large
picture may balance two small ones,
j and that irregular placing of small
objects is in good taste if the equili
j orium is preserved.
Formal balance is desirable in some
i things?in candelabra, for example?
but the placing of things in pairs has
for the most part disappeared. In
the sketch there is formal balance
shown in the sconces at either side
of the old print, but if the vase
of flowers were in the exact center
of the table under the picture the
effect would be stiff and uninterest
ing. As it is. the height of the vase
at the left is balanced by the two low
articles at the right, and the result is
pleadingly informal and irresrular.
In the arrangement of pictures bal
ance must be carefully considered.
Many walls look topheavy because
the pictures are too massive for the
furniture or because they are hung
too high. A horizontal picture in a
vertical space often throws a room
out of balance seemingly. A good
thing to remember is the pyramid line
in composition. A large picture at
one side of a wall space, with a small
one beside it. is apt to throw the bal
ance too far one way; whereas the
large one in the center, with groups
of small ones irregularly placed at
each side, keeps the sense of equi
librium.
Study your walls and your furni
ture. and see if there is. in general, a
st nse of one part of the room bal
ancing the other part, without having
articles placed with mathematical pre
cision or in formal relation to each
other. Weigh things with your eye.
and don't let one part of a group of
furniture, pictures or bric-a-brac out
balance another part.
