The Rule of Law.
Elihu Root, the recognizcd leader of
the American bar, said last night in
addressing the City Bar Association of
New York:
"The rule of law is all that stands
between our civil society and the un
bridled hordes of barbarism."
The spirit of lawlessness is growing.
Lawbreakers and men who threaten to
become lawbreakers are joining hands
and influences, and working together
openly and aggressively.
Lynchers are multiplying, and op
erating in the boldest fashion. A few
years ago they wore masks, and did
their deeds at night. But they make
no concessions now. Their successes
have banished all precautions, and
they "pull off a stunt" barefaced and
in broad daylight. The law be dashed!
But the lynchers are no bolder than I
those men who in opposing a measure!
under discussion for enactment into
law announce a purpose to disobey the
measure if it is written on the books.
They are ready to become lawbreakers
?to lynch a statute if they happen
not to like it. Tliey thus seek to in
timidate legislators: and sometimes
they succeed.
It is entirely legitimate, of course,
to oppose a measure, and bring influ
ence to bear against making it a law.
But there is but one legitimate course
open after the measure has become a
law, and that is to accept and obey
the law while the law remains on the
book*.
It is likewise entirely legitimate to
agitate in favor of amending or re
pealing a law; and the processes are
familiar to all.
A feature of the existing lawless
ness, and contributing materially to
its growth and menace, is the assault,
often made, on the good names of Con
gress and the courts. Both those tribu
nals are accused of being under in
fluences inimical to the public inter
ests. Hence the demand for the initia
tive, the referendum and the recall,
and the abolition of life tenure for
jndges.
To undermine confidence in estab
lished tribunal* and their processes is
to work wide injury. The men who
make and the men who interpret the
!?rws should not be assailed upon mere
suspicion. Unless, and until, they defi
nitely forfeit it, they are entitled to
the public's respect. Our legislators
are chosen after open contests in which
their private, as well as their public,
lives enter, and judges appointed for
life pass muster both for character and
professional equipment.
These are times when plain speak
ing, such as Mr. Root indulged in last
night, is absolutely necessary to bring
home to the people the proper concep
tion of what faees them. If the law
goes, order follows, and nothing is left
but chaos and violence?evejy fellow
for himself, and devil take the. hind
most.
The sultan is to be permitted to
hnve a palace, but no army. Absence
of an army may help him to live in a
little less fear of being assassinated
by one of his immediate entourage.
Europe is finding the study of that
hitherto unfamiliar branch of economic
aeienee known as "American politics,"
interesting, not to say exciting.
Occasionally a "red" is arrested who
appears to feel complimented by yie
fact that his utterances have been
taken so seriously.
This Year's Platforms.
"Let me write the platform at San
Francisco and I care not who runs for
President on it."
While this is not the attitude of any
democrat of presidential size now dis
coursing about what that deliverance
should contain, yet many suggestions
on the subject are coming from high
sources. Mr. Bryan and Vice Presi
dent Marshall have just spoken, and
there is every reason to expect some
thing from the President in due sea
son. The topic is most alluring.
This is a welcome sign. It shows
recognition of the fact that a party
platform this year will be scrutinized
closely, and the party using it held
to a strict accountability.
Platforms and platform-making have
in times past been lightly regarded?
have even provoked wit and merri
ment. A platform was once described
as "a thing to get in on''; and when
the description was made good by the
event?when after getting in on it the
winners kicked it away promptly and
unceremoniously protests were un
availing. The offenders laughed?just
laughed.
Some recent democratic history is
giving some of that party's leaders
pause. Take the platform of 1912.
That deliverance indorsed the legis
lation which had granted American
coastwise shipping free passage
through the Panama canal, and Mr.
Wilaon in accepting the nomination
for President had specifically pledged
JUMftU Um And jet soon
after his inauguration he asked for
the repeal of the law, and secured
repeal through his influence with Con
gress.
Take the matter of a second term
in the presidency. The party, through
Mr. Bryan's influence, limited Mr. Wil
son to a single term in the White
House. But Mr. Wilson did not ac
cept that plank of the platform, and
when the time came stood for, and ob
tained with ease, renomination. Even
Mr. Bryan did not object. And to
day there is sentiment in the demo
cratic party favorable to Mr. Wilson
for the third nomination.
But 1020 is not 1912, nor yet 1913.
A platform now must be more than "a
thing to get in on." And this will
apply at Chicago as well as at 8nn
Francisco. Voters are deeply anxious
this year. The changes produced by
the war and the heated discussions
about what in the way of legislation
they call for have set everybody en
titled to the ballot to thinking about
how best to employ that slip of paper
next November.
Let us have a full and frank decla
ration of principles by each of the
two leading parties, and, later, a faith
ful redemption of all promises made
by the successful party and the man
for whom it has secured the presi
Ideney.

The matter of the ex-kaiser would
seem to be in way of settlement some
what as that of the Germans accused
of war crimes. The allies have sent
another note to Holland repeating the
request for his extradition and recit
ing in vigorous terms his offenses
against civilization and humanity. But.
coupled with this repeated request is
a suggestion that if Holland still feels
unable to comply she will take pre
cautions to keep the refugee under
close surveillance in order to prevent
his exerting a "disastrous influence in
Germany in the future." This sug
gestion is conveyed in a significant
manner:
"The powers cannot conceal the
painful impression made upon them by
the refusal of the Dutch government
to hand over the ex-emperor to them
without any consideration of the pos
sibility of reconciling the scruples of
Holland with some effectual precau
tionary measures to be taken either
on the spot or by holding the ex-em
peror at a distance from the scene of
his crimes, making it impossible for
him to exert his disastrous influence
in Germany in the future. Although
a proposal of this nature would not
correspond fully to the request of the
powers, it would at least have afforded
proof of those feelings which Holland
cannot but possess.''
Holland, it is now reported, will soon
reply accepting the suggestion thus
made, and it is understood at The
Hague that the kaiser will be removed
to Doorn, which is at some distance
from the frontier and susceptible of
closer guard than Amerongcn, where
Hohenzollern is now living. Thus far
there has been no official attempt in
Holland to confine the ex-kaiser to any
particular radius. He has remained at
Amerongen wholly at his own initia
tive, to gain seclusion and also, it is
believed, to be able to consult the
more freely with his partisans. If Hoi
land undertakes to keep him in closer
bounds it may have to give some ef
fectual pledges of such a supervision
as will prevent intimate intercourse
between him and Germany. Holland
is so small a country that any point
in it is not difficult of access from the
German frontier, and it was on this
acconnt that t&e allies intimated as an
alternative to "effectual precautionary
measures taken on the spot" the hold
ing of the ex-kaiser "at a distance
from the scene of his crimes." Upon
the measure of the assurances given
by Holland will perhaps depend the
future course of the allied powers.

The Army Truck Juggernaut.
High-speeding trucks in the streets
of Washington have been the cause
of numerous accidents during the past
few months. The other day a citizen
was killed by an Army truck in cir
cumstances to leave no doubt in the
minds of some who witnessed the oc
currence that the driver of the "offi
cial" machine was at fault. A coro
ner's jury mil render a verdict on this
question. It is meanwhile important
to note that ever since the United
States entered the war and the capital
became the scene of intense military
activity the streets of this city have
been virtually ruled by these vehicles,
often driven with reckless disregard for
regulations and the safety of the peo
ple. Despite efforts by officers in charge
of Army truck work many drivers
of these machines have persisted in
taking the right of way at all points,
and in breaking rules as to turnings
and speeds, seemingly possessed of the
idea that they ware exempt from re
straint of any kind. The fatal acci
dent, whatever may be the finding of
the coroners jury, should cffect a cor
rection of the evil condition. No ve
hicle has privilege in the streets. Se
vere penalties, promptly administered
to offenders, whatever their employ
ment, should bring about a higher re
gard for the law, if not for public
safety primarily. The fullest co-opera
tion by officials of the government is
to bo expected to check this deadly
nuisance.
