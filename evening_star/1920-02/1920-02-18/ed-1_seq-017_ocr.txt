Philadelphia Orchestra.
Seldom, tf ever, in the history of
musical events in the National Capital
has there been an affair of such super
lative interest as was the offering of
Leopold Stokowski and the Philadel
phia Orchestra yesterday afternoon at
the National Theater, where even
standing room was at a premium. In
collaboration with the orchestra were
three eminent pianists, Mme. Olsa
Samaroff (Mrs. Leopold Stokowski),
Harold Bauer and Ossip Gabrilowitseh.
any one of whom alone would have
been an attraction" of the first magni
tude. As befitted so imposing an array
of musicianship, the program was
made up of compositions of purely
classic dignity. Clock's overture to
"Iphigenie en Aulide," Mozart's "Con
certo in K Flat," for two pianos and
orchestra; Beethoven's "Kighth Sym
phony" and Bach's "Concerto in C," for
three pianos and orchestra?works that
demanded not simpiy a master tech
nician, but virtuosity of the most sub
stantial and highest type.
Interest centered naturally in the
concerted numbers, though both the
Gluck and Beethoven compositions,
as read by Dr. Stokowski and inter
preted with finished artistry by the
orchestra, could not fail to delight
a music-loving audience. Mozart's
"Concerto." played by Messrs. Bauer
and Gabrilowitseh, with the orchestra,
is one of the composer's most char
acteristic works, with its spontaneous
and buoyant melodies. The main
theme of the opening movement was
announced by the orchestra, then an
swered by each piano, sometimes al
ternately, sometimes in unison, the
more graceful and delicate shading
in some of the lighter passages being
especially delightful in Gabrilowitsch's
velvety tone.
The Bach number, with the three
artists and orchestra, proved the
piece de resistance of the superb pro
gram. The classic and well defined
themes, pure contrapuntal forms and
wonderful harmonic progressions
were developed explicitly and intelli
gently. as was to be expected, and
such perfect co-ordination in the en
tire ensemble could only have been
accomplished by the most finished
artists. The beautiful orchestration
was clearly developed by the or
ganization, which, while abundantly I
sustaining, never overshadowed the
pianos, even in the softest phrases,
and the entire performance, from
the first faint notes of the strings
to the massive climax of the ensemble
at the close, made an impression long
to be remembered.
