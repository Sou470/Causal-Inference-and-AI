With Opening of Training Season Only a Few
Days Off, Most Clubs Have Less Than
Half of Regulars Under Contract
BY DENMAN THOMPSON.
Indications are that the base ball magnates are going to have
need for all the additional cash they expect to get as a result of in
creasing admission prices this year. They will if they meet the de
mands the players are making for salary boosts. With the time for
reporting for preliminary work now "a matter of days rather than
weeks, on!v one or two clubs have a majority of their regulars under
contract. By far the greater number have less than half of the first
string performers in line, and no encouraging progress is being made
toward inducing the backward members of the fraternity to sign on
the dotted line. Practically all of the owners, including those of
Washington, took cognizance of conditions and voluntarily hiked the
pay of the athletes, but that these raises have failed to satisfy them is
apparent from their tardiness in accepting terms.
Clark Griffith makes it a rule never to discuss the financial ar
rangements entered into with his men. He feels sure "they'll all be on
hand when the time comes," but. however that may be, less than a
dozen actually are in the fold, and only about half of these are tried
performers who can be banked on for steady service. They comprise
one catcher, three pitchers, a couple of inhelders and a lone gardener.
Picinich. Johnson, Erickson, Courtney, Leonard, Ellerbe and Roth,
that's the list.
Such essential cogs as Rice, Milan. Judge, Gharrity. Shanks, Shaw,
Shannon and McBride still are on the other side of the fence, and a
similar condition prevails in the camps of nearly all the other big
league clubs. There is little question but that most of the holdouts
will be lined up by the time they are due to trek for the south, but the
moguls will have to meet some stiff demands to turn the trick, for they
cannot go into the market and buy material, and the players know it.
The material isn't there to be bought. There are not enough players
of real big league caliber to meet the demand.
WAR RESPONSIBLE FOR SCARCITY OF PLAYERS.
One of the reasons for the great scarcity of talent is, of course,
the war. which put most of the minor leagues out of business, thereby
grpatly restricting the source of supply. Another is the great growth
in number of industrial teams, also a development of the war. A
premising player assured of a job all the year around at a salary
oftentimes in excess of that available in the larger minor circuits,
with opportunity afforded for picking up additional remuneration from
Saturday and Sunday games, naturally cannot be induced to start at
the bottom af the base ball ladder somewhere in the sticks,"br, if he
has played in the bushes, to return and continue his apprenticeship
in the hope he will catch the eye of some scout.
Resumption of relations between majors and minors by the
promulgation of a new pact when the Baltimore Federal Leaguers' suit
has been settled is necessary for the bush league circuits to exist in
the numbers thev did before the war. The "gentlemen's agreement"
effected at the Chicago conclave will help in the production of young
sters and their advancement to fast company, but in the meantime
it is to the colleges that the major league moguls must look for recruits.
Connie Mack years ago demonstrated that the varsity squads are rich
sources of material. The prejudice that once existed in the minds of
professional pilots against campus-trained players now has almost
ceased to exist. There is scarcely a team in the majors today that
hasn't one or more of them on its roster, and next June is expected
to mark the graduation of an unprecedented number of collegians
from amateur to professional ranks.
MARTIN AND ALTROCK OFF FOR TAMPA.
A reminder that the opening of the spring training season now
is less than two weeks away is the departure today for Tampa of Mike
Martin and Nick Altrock. The trainer and coach of the Nationals,
accompanied by one of the dusky turf manicurists employed at the
local park, will combine business with pleasure by making the trip
south by water on a boat leaving Baltimore -this evening. The sail,
down the coast comprises the recreational part of their jaunt, the
work starting at the end of their journey next Monday morning, when
they will take up the task of getting the playing field in shape 'for the
first squad of athletes due one week later.
The sending of a ground squad in advance of the players is a pre
cautionary measure dictated by experience. In some instances clubs
which have .relied upon assurances of .local authorities that the parks
were in good shape have arrived to find that a circus had wintered
on the site: that the grounds were studded with hurdles for cavalry j
drills; that the fences had been blown down orthat some squatter had
part of the acreage under cultivation. It will be recalled that only
last spring the first of the Griffmen to reach Augusta found the grounds 1
in bad shape from neglect. The unattended-to matters included a j
luxuriant growth of dry grass, a little detail that Harry Harper sought
to eliminate through the simple expedient of applying a match, with
the result that ?ome of the grass and a lot of the fence disappeared. I
President Griffith has been assured by the management of the
hostelry that controls the park yhich adjoins it that the field already
is in tip-top condition, with the grass cut and ground rolled, etc., but
is taking nothing for granted. He is anxious to have the playing
grounds in the very best of shape, especially the infield, to help him
in the intensive drilling necessary for the development of a capable
squad of base guardians..
