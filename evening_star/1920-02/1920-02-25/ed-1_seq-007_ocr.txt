The methods and systems in vogue a few years ago, and still adhered to
by many are no longer applicable in this age and generation. A better un
derstanding of the duties of the funeral director has changed the old order.
When anesthetics were first discovered many good people held up their
hands in horror, declaring that those who used them were defying God.
The use of a school house was denied, in 1823, for the discussion of the
question of a proposed railroad on the ground that a railroad wa3 a "device
of the devil" and that God "never intended that His intelligent creatures
should travel at the frightful speed of fifteen miles an hour."
One of the most difficult problems confronting the modern funeral director will
be solved when the public realizes that service is the principal and, in fact, only
commodity which he has to offer.
The casket and funeral appurtenances are negligible, or practically so, requiring
little or no experience, and, in the matter of expense, consistent only with the cost
of production.
Service is the main consideration?the care and disposition of the body of the
one who has passed on, knowledge and ability, and a readiness to relieve the burders
of those who are left to mourn.
