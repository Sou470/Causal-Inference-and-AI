hours indeed, Abraham Lincoln's most
famous speech was that remarkable ora

coln's power of condensed expression,
a tribute to George Washington, de
livered at Springfield, III., on Wash
ington's birthday, 1842, when Lincoln
was thirty-three years of age. It has
just been discovered in an obscure file
of a local newspaper and given to the
world. Though it has already been
printed in the news columns of The
Star yesterday, it is appropriate to re
peat it here, that it may become even
more familiar to those who hold in
high respect the memories of both
Washington and Lincoln:
"This is the one hundred and tenth
:itniveraary of the birthday of Wa*h
ngton. Wo are met to celebrate this
lay. Washington is the mightiest
aame on earth?long since mightiest
in the cause of civil liberty; still
mightiest in moral reformation. On
that name an eulogy ig expected. It
cannot be. To add brightness to the
sun or glory to the name of Washing
ton is alike impossible. Let none at
tempt it. In solemn awe pronounce
the name, and in its naked, deathless
splendor leave it shining on.''
As an example of genuine tribute
and full appraisal this brief but per
fect expression will stand alone. It

the country, even as the Gettysburg
address of twenty-one years later has
