TO CHINA ON INCREASE
Gain of 714 Per Cent in Fourteen
Years?Figures in Other
Countries.
Imports to China from Japan in
creased 714 per cent between 1904 and
1918, from the United State 244 per]
cent arid from Kngland 49 per cent,
according to a commercial handbook
on China issued today by the bureau
of foreign and domestic commerce of
the Department of Commerce.
The I'nited States has made sub
stantial progress in selling merchan
dise to China, shipping jll7.000.000
worth in the fiscal year 1919. as
against J57.000.000 In 1914. In one j
notable item, however, a great de
crease has occurred; United States
sale of cotton goods to China has
fallen off from nearly {30.000,000 in
1906 to $1,200,000 in 1918.
Julean Arnold, the American com
mercial attache at Peking, and the
other contributors to the handbook
express the conviction that American
producers need to appreciate more
justly the tremendous possibilities in
the orient, where democracy, indus
trialism and modern education are
bringing about far-reaching changes.
They direct attention to the fact that
among the 400,000,000 inhabitants of
Chins even a slight modification in
the prevailing mode of life is capable
of creating an enormous market for
specific classes of imported goods,
and, with the entire social structure
in a state of flux and progress, the
trade opportunities may be character
ized as limitless.
China's foreign trade, the new book
shows, is five times as great today
as it was thirty years ago. When the
per capita forei^h trade is equivalent
to that of Australasia the total will
be $65,000,000,000 instead of the pres
ent $1,200,000,000. and Commercial At
tache Arnold believes that this figure
may eventually be attained, basing his
opinion on the country's vast wealth
In coal and basic metals, in vegetable
and animal products, and the indus
trious, adaptable character of Its pop
ulation. Taking the single item of
coal, it appears Chat China has enough
to supply the world with a billion
tons a year for a period of a thou
sand years. Hundreds of millions of
tons of iron ore are available. All
the prerequisites of industrial devel
opment are present, and even now
cotton mills in Shanghai, Hankow
and Tientsin are paying dividends as
high as 25 per cent.

neva Red Cross Meeting, Talks

LONDON, February 14.-?The arrival
of Henry P. Davison on the Mauretania

his trip has any bearing on the inter
national finance situation. Questioned
on the subject. Mr. Davison told the
Associated Press he would remain in
i^ondon several days and would then go
to Paris en route to Geneva for the an
nual meeting-of the International l,easue
of Red Cross Societies. He said he had
nothing to add to this statement.
It had been reported in financial cir
cles that Mr. Davison would confer un
officially with London financiers.
Mr. Davison is quoted by an inter
viewer of the Graphic as saying:
_ "My visit is both official and unofficial.
Finance is my business. Nobody in my
position can avoid doing all possible to
help the financial problem to a happier
state."

vate financiers, Mr. Davison is quoted
as replying:
"I must not say. It is enough that
finance will occupy a good part of my

LOWER FOOD PRICES
IN NEW YORK PREDICTED
NEW YORK. February 14.?Lowet
prices for butter, eg-gs. fruits and
vegetables will follow resumption of
normal rail transportation and truck
ing- when the snow and ice are cleared
from New York's streets, Edwin J.
O'Malley, commissioner of markets,
predicted today. Huge quantities of
these commodities are "waiting at the
door" of the city, Mr. O'Malley said,
adding that railroad yards just out
side New York and steamship piers
here are being piled up with them
because of lack of facilities to move
them to markets.
Arrival of shiploads of produce now
on the way from Denmark and Hol
land and withdrawal of England and
France from the meat market with
cancellation of their orders with
United States packers are additional
factors which will bring down food
prices, O'Malley asserted.
