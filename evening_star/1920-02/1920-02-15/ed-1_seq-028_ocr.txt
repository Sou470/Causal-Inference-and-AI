WEDNESDAYS TO BE LADIES' DAY HERE.
The custom of inviting fans of the fair sex to the ball games one
day of each week, as the guests of the club, is to be resumed here this
year, according to announcement made yesterday by President Griffith,
it follows:
"Every Wednesday during the championship season on which a
game is scheduled will be 'ladies' day.' On all days designated ladies
will be admitted to the grandstand free of charge, upon payment of
the w?r tax of ten cents. It is not necessary for ladies to be escorted
to take advantage of this courtesy. In order for a lady to occupy a
box seat on these special ladies' days, it is necessary to purchase a box
seat check from the exchange ticket seller inside the park. A special
entrance gate for ladies will be provided on these days."
Ladies' days will not be a novelty for Washington, having been
in force here off and on for many seasons. They are a time-honored
custom in some cities of the circuit, where the plan has been in vogue
uninterruptedly for years.
WAR COST YANKEE OWNERS $60,00*.
Any kind of warfare is costly, the base ball variety included, as
Messrs. Ruppert and Huston can testify after footing up the bills in
curred in their six-month campaign against Ban Johnson. It is
estimated that the litigation growing out of the Mays case cost the
New York magnates on an average of about $10,000 a month. The real
answer to the question of who won the war is "the lawyers."
The Yankees bought Mays from the Red Sox on July 31 for a
reported price of $40,000 and almost immediately the suspension of
the player was announced by Johnson. The New York club's first
move was to obtain an injunction restraining Johnson from inter
fering with Mays pitching for the New York club and thereafter legal
actions followed in such bewildering array as to make it difficult to
keep track of the changing aspects of the litigation. All the papers
in the Mays suit have been printed and the mass of evidence in the
various cases forms a volume as bulky as the cause of the row. Ac
cording to one of the officials of the New York club, the expense in
court procedure, attorneys' fees, preparation of cases and printing
will reach the $60,000 mark. The New York owners are not disposed
to crow over the outcome of the conflict, but believe it has placed
the game in the American League on a new footing and regard the
expense as well worth while. Vice President Huston is quoted as
saying:
"The American League has been rescued from one-man rule,
which, after all, was the main object in our fight. The dismissal of
the Mays case and the awarding of third place money to our play
ers were secondary to the important clause in the settlement which
provides for a board of review, of which Col. Rupper is a member,
to hear all cases which members of the league feel affect their con
stitutional rights. This provision of the settlement, of course, has
widespread influence in the conduct of the league's affairs.
"The feeling among the club owners was, I am very glad to say,
most cordial, and 1 firmiv believe that all the owners were sincere in
tl>eir expression of delight that the factional differences were ended."
