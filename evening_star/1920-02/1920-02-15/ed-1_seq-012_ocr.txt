An interesting feature of the Wood
bury exhibition, now in progress at
the Corcoran Gallery of Art, Is the

by Mr Woodbury shown in the ante
I room, through which one gains ad
mission to the semi-circular gallery in

A great portrait painter, one of the
greatest of the day, once said that
she could not do landscape because
she could not draw sufficiently well.
This indicates, however, unusual
knowledge rather than .ignorance, for
it is those who draw least well who
imagine that a knowledge of drafts
manship is not essential to landscape

Mr. Woodbury shows beautiful
pencil drawings of landscape sub
jects. Very different are these from
his etchings, as different as work in
different mediums should always be.
tetching is essentially an art of elim
ination and thjs Mr. Woodbury under
stands.
In his etchings, as in his paintings,
he shows interest in the problems not
merely of light and shade, but of
motion. When he etches two great
elephants (and who could fancy a
subject less congenial supposedly for
transcription in this medium?) ha
shows the great beasts not stupidly
standing like brazen images, but as
swaying from side to side as is their
habit. The s>'a that hp etches moves,
the waves dance, and through their
foam one sees leaping fish. Hut when
Mr. Woodbury etches the winter
woods he sets forth absolute still
ness. absence of motion and of sound
paucity of nature.
As an etcher Mr. Woodbury ranks
with the best. His line is rather nig
ged and broken, but it is full of sig
nificance. His work is not to be
measured by the works of others, it
is essentially individual and is his
own. and it is charming.
